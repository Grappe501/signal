# Chapter 2

The table caught the morning the way it always did—gold on the laminate, the cheap kind that didn't pretend to be stone. Through the window, New Manhattan kept its lanes open: school pods drifting toward docking slots, kitchens waking on other tiers, maintenance drones creeping tower seams with mechanical patience. The city doing what it did best, which was continue.

Noah poured coffee into the mug with the chip at the rim and listened to the Second dock hum in its alcove—a soft sync tone, polite as a door held for you. The LED strip along the cabinet edge pulsed once, confirming link strength, and the overlay began to edge his vision: work queue loading, calendar tiles stacking in the lower left while his hands stayed on real things. Kettle. Pan. The weight of a spatula that still needed cleaning from yesterday. Steam climbed. The building's optimized hush held outside the glass; inside, the kitchen kept its honest noise.

He had made breakfast every morning since his wife died—first alone in a too-quiet apartment where the silence had teeth, then with a Second when grief got too loud to carry in one room at a time. The first months he'd felt guilty for splitting himself, as if attention were a finite currency and spending it at work meant stealing from the table. Then one morning Iona had laughed at something he'd said from the link, and the guilt had thinned into something like relief. The ritual wasn't escape. It was how the day agreed to start.

He cracked eggs into heat. Butter. Toast under the grill until the edges blackened the way Iona liked, because some preferences didn't need an algorithm to remember them. A polite chime sounded at the edge of his hearing—the overlay asking permission to deepen—and he let it, the way you let a familiar guest through a door you no longer bothered to lock.

The link snapped clean.

Urban Water Allocation filled the lower half of his sight—tier maps, pressure flags, the boring grids of a job that kept civilization drinking without anyone needing to think about it. Boring was the point. Noah's body remained at the table while his attention went where the city paid him to go. Two places. One morning. He resolved a pressure flag with his thumb without looking away from the pan, the motion so familiar it felt like breathing in two lungs at once. Data reflected in his eye like weather. Relief arrived before he could name it—ordinary, earned, the kind of gift you stopped thanking because it came every day.

Somewhere in the building's maintenance tier, a valve he'd never see adjusted itself because he'd cleared the flag. That was the part people forgot when they complained about optimization—someone still had to be in the room with the problem, even if the room was only in your head. The miracle had stopped feeling miraculous, which was how he knew it had become true.

Iona came in sleep-mussed, socks wrong, hair fighting a braid that had lost overnight.

"Morning, ghost," she said, and stole the first piece of toast straight off his plate.

Noah laughed—half a second late, the link still finishing the joke in his ear before his mouth caught up. "That's my breakfast."

"You weren't eating fast enough." She pulled out the chair across from him, fully present, chewing with the unselfconscious appetite of someone who still believed the world was built to feed her. The window chair behind Noah held his body instead—eyes aimed at nothing in the kitchen, jaw working on food he wasn't tasting, hands loose on the mug like a passenger in his own life. The shell kept chewing on schedule. Believable. Empty.

Iona glanced at the vacated version of him and didn't comment. That was its own kind of love at twelve: noticing the split and choosing not to make it a sermon.

He resolved an allocation flag with his thumb without looking away from her. "Pod's in twenty."

"I know." She watched a notification sparkle at the edge of her vision—school schedule, soft fonts, the kind of assistance that never raised its voice. She had learned to work through it. Growth or surrender—he wasn't sure which yet, and the not-knowing felt like part of the deal.

"You're still on mute," she said, mouth full.

"I'm not on mute." He surfaced a fraction—voice ordinary, presence not—and she rolled her eyes the way she always did when his laugh arrived after the punchline had already died in the room. She forgave him anyway. The latency was a tax. The tax was worth it.

On the wall screen, the morning loop ran its gentle circuit: transit on time, weather mild, markets calm. A push notification arrived with a success chime too bright for the hour—*Your score is stable.* Gold band. Noah glanced and looked away. Stable was what you wanted. Stable was what you built a life on. He didn't screenshot it. He didn't need to. The number had been in the same band for months, drifting the way healthy things drifted when nobody was performing for the graph.

Outside, Iona's pod slid into its lane on schedule—white hull, clean docking arc. Iona tracked it with one eye on her homework, liking proof more than she admitted. Somewhere above them, a child practiced an instrument badly—the sound arriving messy and human through the glass, louder than the optimized mornings he sometimes borrowed through the link. He didn't borrow this one. He stayed at the table in both senses that mattered to her.

A transit car chimed its harmonic welcome one tier down, flawless and bright. Iona tilted her homework toward the screen edge—equations on glass, auto-correct waiting politely for wrong answers—and worked a minute in silence he didn't fill. Fork scraping plate. The sync hum kept its steady bed beneath everything—the infrastructure of a morning that had already decided to work. Crumbs. The room felt full enough.

Iona's fork stilled against the plate. Then, without looking up:

"Do you think people liked having only one life?"

The question landed too large for the kitchen and too quiet for the city below—like someone had opened a museum door and asked you to smell the past.

Noah laughed.

Not cruel. Not even dismissive. Just the reflex of a man who had signed up for a world that made hardship optional and hadn't yet learned to miss what hardship had taught. The idea sounded like nostalgia for something their civilization had politely deleted—one body, one room, one attention, no overlay waiting when grief got heavy.

His thumb found the chip at the mug rim. Bitter. Real. Iona watched him the way children watched adults dodge truth—patient, already learning which questions the world preferred unanswered. The homework tile resumed. Noah's overlay chimed once, polite, asking him back to the grids. He let it. *Running Double* again. The phrase lived in the building's slang the way all true things did—half joke, half boast.

The question stayed in the room after the laugh faded, the way questions did when you loved someone too much to answer honestly and too much to lie.

The sync tone pulsed. The city continued. Breakfast continued—miraculous, ordinary, theirs for as long as he could keep both channels open without dropping either.
