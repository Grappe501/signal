# Chapter 34

Sleep arrived wrong before she knew she was asleep.



Month Three, week eleven—the same week the city was learning new questions about kindness scores and filter toggles while Mara drove back from CIB with orange fire still living behind her eyelids and the sync hum returned like a patient parent. She had not slept well since the Veil stuttered. Investigators called that workload. Bodies called it something else when the pillow finally accepted their weight and the room refused to stay a room.



Her apartment was gray and honest in the dark—no overlay bloom, personal layer off because sleep was the last jurisdiction she still controlled. The notebook on the bed edge held paper cases the tablet couldn't carry. **8841-A**. **8853-M**. **SIG-MERGE-01**. Tabs she refused to close because closed was how departments learned to forget.



The hum started thin.



Not the building sync tone. Not the verification chime from Month One's basement. Something at the edge of hearing—pattern without label, the way server fans pretended to be neutral until your body decided they weren't. Mara lay still and listened the way she listened to witness statements that matched suite output until they didn't.



Then the memory arrived that wasn't hers.



Hands—her hands, wrong knowledge—adjusting a calibration dial on equipment she had never seen in waking life. Metal cold through gloves. A window showing white not city, flat and vast, the color of places that did not bother with tiers. A voice in the memory said *repeat at seven point two* in the tired cadence of lab work, not myth. The hands wrote in a margin she couldn't read when she tried to look directly: *receiver before localization*.



Not Cord Junction. Not Ch 13's institutional wrongness—records that clarified nothing, suites that passed while the world failed. This was **sensor wrongness**, body wrongness, the dream borrowing muscle memory from nowhere and charging it to her account.



The place shifted without transition.



Corridor. Low ceiling. Cable trays. Concrete that had never learned to perform beauty. She had never walked here—she was certain the way investigators were certain about alibis that didn't exist yet. The geometry felt mapped by need instead of planners: doors where maintenance wanted them, windows where heat escaped, a turn that opened onto a platform overlooking—



White.



Ice shelf under glass, or glass under ice; the dream didn't resolve the order. A facility clung to the edge of the visible world—modules linked by enclosed bridges, antenna arrays thin as bones against a sky that held light without warmth. Not a postcard. Not a briefing slide. **Texture**: frost on interior glass, boot scuffs on a threshold, red warning glyphs in a language the overlay would have translated if the overlay had been invited.



People moved through the facility in the dream without faces she could keep.



A technician in thermal gear looked up as if expecting her. "You're late for window fourteen," he said—not angry, just factual, the way maintenance spoke when schedules outranked introductions. She opened her mouth to say she didn't have a window assignment and heard her voice answer in a register that wasn't quite hers: "Lag again."



The word landed with body knowledge she hadn't earned awake.



A woman with an accent Mara couldn't place stood at a console, gloved hands hovering over controls labeled in abbreviations the bureau would have redacted on principle. "If the map arrives before the feet, we log it," the woman said, not to Mara, to the room. "We don't broadcast it." She laughed once without joy and added, "Not until embodiment stabilizes," like she was quoting a memo Mara had read in another life under another clearance.



Neither face belonged to Mercer. Neither belonged to anyone in her case files. They looked at her the way people looked at someone who was late to a meeting that had never been scheduled on any calendar she owned.



On a wall screen the dream showed a waveform without legend—peaks repeating at intervals her skin recognized before her mind consented. Not noise. Not STATIC, not yet a name she would allow. Just pattern wearing patience.



The thin hum thickened—not louder, **closer**, as if the dream had routed audio through bone instead of air. For one heartbeat Mara heard a man's voice degraded by old equipment, clinical and tired, mid-thought rather than mid-speech:



*—What if consciousness is not produced by—*



The sentence cut. Static swallowed the rest. The dream did not offer a replay button.



She tried to move toward the platform rail. Her foot found the step a half-second before proprioception agreed. Lag. The kind of lag embodiment cases measured and grief loops hid and dead researchers wrote in margins when shareholders needed miracles instead of numbers.



The white outside the glass **breathed**—not weather, not alive, just scale so large it fooled perception into motion. Antenna arrays blinked. Somewhere below the shelf, water darkened and vanished into a geometry the map refused to simplify.



For one held moment the dream offered the image whole: polar night or polar glare, she couldn't tell which; ice meeting steel meeting glass; a research station clinging to the edge of a continent that didn't care about continuity scores—a place colder than New Manhattan's performed winter, older than the Veil, built for listening to something the public myth never put in brochures. No name attached. No map essay. Only white vast enough to need a word she did not have on waking tongues.



Mara reached for the notebook she kept in waking life and found only air—the dream's cruelty was small and precise: paper existed when the institution wanted signatures, not when she wanted anchors.



The hum held. The people unknown. The place never visited. The memory not hers.



Then the white filled everything.



* * *



She woke without a gasp.



Pre-dawn gray through the window. Real apartment. Real weight of blanket. Wrong certainty that something had been **true** in the dream long enough to leave residue on her skin—the way orange fire had, the way raw street sound had, the way certain sentences lived in rooms after the recording stopped.



Mara sat up slowly. Bare feet on floor. Cold that matched cold.



In the bathroom mirror her face looked like her face and also like someone else's fatigue wearing it—Ch 24's hairline rhyme without the name, identity as a thing that mostly held until it didn't. She brushed her teeth and watched her hand move and could not fully trust the timing between intention and reflection.



Day bleed, micro, not psychosis: the hum at the edge of hearing for three breaths after the faucet stopped. A flash of white behind the tile grout—not image, **afterimage**, the facility platform refusing to stay in sleep where it belonged. She swallowed water and tasted metal she hadn't tasted in waking life today.



In the kitchen she poured coffee and watched the surface hold still—no adaptive ripple, no comfort slider on steam. Honest liquid. Her reflection in the window showed the tower tiers waking gold as the city re-enabled bloom block by block—the same polite mercy half the city had chosen after the honest week. Mara did not catalog who had chosen it. That was not her jurisdiction this morning. It was also not unrelated, which was worse.



She swallowed coffee too hot and felt the burn arrive on schedule—the body confirming cause and effect when everything else lagged.



The city beyond the glass was audible without bloom—honest again for early hour, transit distant, building settling. Week eleven continued. The queue would continue. Chen would not ask whether continue was enough.



Mara dressed in bureau layers because layers were how investigators rebuilt competence. Coffee in the mug from the cabinet—not the bureau desk mug, the home one, cheap ceramic, honest chip at the rim she didn't remember owning until now. Her hands shook once on the kettle handle. Once only.



At the desk by seven, green terminal glow. **8919-E** waited where she'd left it. **8853-M** pulsed once in the corner—unresolved, verification pass, records fail—then faded when she didn't open it. **SIG-MERGE-01** held its three lines without commentary. Normal queue behavior. Normal wrongness wearing normal clothes.



The dream sat beside the queue like a witness the suite couldn't interview.



* * *



She did not write the polar image in the notebook.



She did not write *facility* or *ice* or *receiver before localization*. She wrote what the institution could metabolize:



**Sleep disturbance · post-stutter workload · embodiment tangent residue · no operational impact.**



The file fields accepted the grammar without judgment. Stress dropdown. Sleep quality: **fair**. Case load: **elevated**. Optional cross-ref: **8921-E** closed · **Vale archive** sidebar · **misfiled tangent**. The system offered a banner—**WELLNESS MODULE AVAILABLE**—and she dismissed it the way she had dismissed calm return modules after fire nights.



In the margin she typed what supervisors read:



*Single episode vivid dream · foreign locale · no recurrence reported · investigator fit for duty.*



Fit for duty was a checkbox civilization used when it needed bodies to keep walking past questions that didn't fit forms.



She almost tagged **Signal · Preliminary** from the Vale partition—almost connected the hum to waveform peaks and redacted notebook scans and a dead man's tired hypothesis she had closed in green two months ago. Her finger hovered. The wrong feeling sharpened into fascination—the basement lean, the investigator appetite for sentences that arrived before evidence agreed to carry them.



*Passed. Failed.* The old verification grammar tried to arrive and she refused it entry. There was nothing to verify—only a dream with foreign hands and a white horizon and a voice that cut mid-sentence. Not relevant, she told the empty suite, performing competence for the room the way she had after the archive terminal in Month Two.



She did not click.



Connecting was how tangents became religions. Dismissing was how institutions kept moving. Mara chose the motion she knew: file closed, primary queue forward, drawer not lock.



**8841-A** stayed open in another tab because some doors were habits, not decisions.



Chen pinged optional—*Everything green on my board. You sleeping?* Mara typed back: *Fine. Closing a wellness note. 8919-E first.*



Not a lie. Not the whole truth. The kind of reply that kept mentorship functional while the wrong feeling stayed in the room without permission.



She signed the sleep note. The terminal chimed once—polite, satisfied, the acoustic equivalent of *within tolerance*.



Outside the glass New Manhattan rehearsed calm markets and open lanes. Inside CIB-4 the fan hum matched something at the edge of her hearing for half a second before she shut it out without naming it. She wasn't ready for another noun.



The file icon showed **closed** in green.



The question did not close. It never had—not since Month Two's archive terminal, not since the waveform peaks, not since the dream borrowed hands and white horizons and voices that cut mid-sentence. Seg 022 held: never confirm, never deny, never rescue with exposition. Ambiguity was the only honest jurisdiction left when perception, records, and sleep all failed on different schedules.



Mara opened **8919-E** and read the guest's band numbers the way she read numbers when she needed the world to pretend metrics still measured something true.



The wrong feeling returned anyway—not fear, not dread. **Recognition** without coordinates. The body remembering a lean toward a sentence it couldn't finish in waking or sleep.



File closed.



Wrong feeling open.



She worked.
