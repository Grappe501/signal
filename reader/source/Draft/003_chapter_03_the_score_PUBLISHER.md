# Chapter 3

CIB Cognitive Anomalies did not believe in mornings the way the Spine did. Its floors arrived pre-caffeinated—light the color of policy, air filtered until even dust looked compliant—and Mara had learned to trust that more than she trusted charm. She sat at a desk that had never been assigned to ambition, opened **8841-A**, and let the file become the room.

The case header loaded in the gray interface the bureau preferred: honest equipment, no curated glass. **Hush Spire 11-A.** **Guest homicide.** **Legal Second present.** **Owner remote-verified.** Everything she had stood in that morning, translated into fields she could sort.

Alina Ward, forty-one. Compliance counsel. Overnight guest of Julian Crane, Continuity Infrastructure Director. Review session logged **11:14 PM**; building systems placed her asleep by **4:12 AM**. Cause of death pending full coroner synthesis—cranial compression consistent with Second-grade force, minimal blood, the neat violence of strength applied without struggle. Mara had seen the body. The file did not improve it.

She wrote in her paper notebook anyway—pen in the spine, ink that could not be autosaved—because some details arrived better through the hand. Her father had kept a ledger on the kitchen table long after the city told him paper was sentiment. *Numbers lie slower,* he used to say, which was not wisdom so much as habit dressed up to survive.

The HVI column populated when she tabbed to victim profile.

Human Viability Index was not a concept Mara had ever been asked to explain on a form. It was simply there—like gravity, like transit priority—sorting lives into bands the way weather sorted seasons. She had used HVI in a hundred investigations without feeling anything about it. Good scores meant stable neighborhoods, faster queues, fewer questions at the door. Bad scores meant friction you learned to navigate or learned to drown in. The system did not pretend otherwise. It just made otherwise expensive.

She did not question the column yet. She noted it.

A sidebar offered context the way sidebars always did—helpful, frictionless, already sure of its conclusion. **Elevated residence anomaly.** **Platinum tier.** **Continuity preservation active at time of dispatch.** The language wanted a hardware stall, a contained incident, a building that had corrected itself before anyone important had to feel it. Mara minimized the sidebar and kept reading.

* * *

By midmorning the file had company.

Forensics returned on schedule—images she did not linger on, measurements she did trust. Cranial compression. Minimal blood. No sign of struggle in the suite's curated geometry. The coroner note used the word *decisive* without using the word *human*, which was how professionals kept their hands clean.

Parallel to the stills, a tier map flickered in a training overlay she had opened for a different case and not closed. New Manhattan in bands: transit priority, housing access, emergency routing. A queue simulation ran in the corner—same hour, same weather, two HVI bands side by side. The higher band cleared in forty seconds. The lower band waited four minutes and missed a pod slot that would not come again until afternoon.

Mara watched the simulation finish and felt nothing she could put in a report. Invisible class was not a conspiracy. It was a schedule.

She closed the overlay and pulled owner chain again.

Julian Crane's alibi held with the same cheerful certainty it had offered at **7:08 AM**. Retinal turnstile, **Arcology One**, **0402.** Executive Continuity Briefing Room **14-C.** Session seal **0408–0700.** Remote continuity authenticated. Biological anchor verified. No anomalies flagged.

The man had been elsewhere in the law's eyes while his legal Second sat in a chair that would not stop looking at her.

Mara did not chase the contradiction yet. Contradiction was a luxury you earned after you understood the shape of the thing. She queued a remote interview and went to refill her mug—the scuffed enamel one, bitter at the lip—while the system placed the call.

* * *

Julian Crane appeared on her screen with the polished fatigue of someone who had been performing competence for longer than his face could hide.

He was forty-four in the file and looked both younger and older on video—skin too even at the jaw, eyes carrying a weight the rest of him had learned to sell as diligence. Continuity Infrastructure Director. The title sat in the corner of the frame like a credential that expected trust.

"Mara Voss," she said. "CIB Cognitive Anomalies. I need to confirm guest context and your movements overnight."

"Of course." His voice was flat-polite, the register of a man who had already spoken to counsel, building security, and whatever private version of panic Platinum owners kept off the feeds. "Alina stayed after the compliance review. Standard for a late session. I was at Arcology One before four. The seal will show you."

"It does."

She let the silence sit long enough to see if he would fill it with something the chain had not. He did not. He waited with the patience of someone who believed the record would do his grieving for him.

"Walk me through why she was in the unit," Mara said.

"Overnight review. Sensitive filings. The building prefers guests remain on-site when sessions run past transit curfew." A beat. "She was colleague, not—" He stopped, recalibrated. "Not a stranger."

Mara wrote *colleague* in the notebook and did not underline it.

"Your Second was present in the living area during the stall window."

"I am aware."

"You did not take the live confirmation request at **0708**."

"The briefing seal—" He exhaled once, controlled. "Deep continuity session. I don't break seal for non-critical pings. Your dispatch classification didn't read as homicide on my end."

It was plausible in the way expensive systems made plausibility a product feature.

She tabbed Alina Ward's profile back to center screen—not for Crane's benefit; he couldn't see her workspace—and the HVI field finished loading.

**934.**

Platinum band. Near the top of the chart the city pretended wasn't a chart.

Mara stared at the number longer than the UI expected. HVI 934 meant stability in every band that mattered—housing, health routing, credit of attention, the invisible lubricant that kept a life from snagging on friction. It meant the victim was not the shape of random violence. It did not mean she was impossible to kill. It meant the killing had happened in a context the easy stories would not fit.

Crane's image had shrunk to a corner tile while she read. She expanded him again.

"Did you know her score?" Mara asked.

"I knew she was valued." Careful word. "The firm tracks retention metrics. HVI is—" He waved a hand at a concept he did not want to touch on record. "It's not why she was there."

"No," Mara said. "It's why the sidebar thinks this is a malfunction."

His jaw tightened—a human tell, finally. "My building is not a malfunction."

"I didn't say it was."

She ended the call with procedure, not warmth, and sat with the screen after he vanished. In the notebook: *Platinum guest. Platinum score. Wrong room for a wrong story.*

The scar above her brow pulled when she leaned back. She touched it without thinking—the old habit of checking which version of her face the morning had left alone.

* * *

Chen's voice arrived in her earpiece before the hour turned.

"Walk me through where you are," Deputy Director Marisa Chen said, dry as always, already reading the same fields.

"Guest homicide confirmed. Owner alibi intact. Second present, ocular anomaly logged yesterday—nonresponsive motor, active tracking." Mara kept her voice level. The office around her continued its quiet work, investigators pretending their files were the only emergencies in a city that still looked solved from every window. "Forensics supports Second-grade force. No struggle."

"And the narrative?"

Mara knew which narrative she meant. The cheap one. The one that had already trended on a sidebar she had dismissed at **7:08** and could still feel in the building's breath.

"Hardware stall," she said. "Isolated incident. Platinum corrected before CIB finished coffee."

Chen was quiet a beat—choosing precision over comfort.

"That would close fast," Chen said.

"It would."

"And your read?"

Mara looked at **934** on the screen. At *colleague* in her handwriting. At a city that sorted lives before it sorted crimes.

"I don't have a read I can file yet," she said. "I have a shape that doesn't match the stall story."

"Don't chase spectacle. Keep it quiet until we know what we're looking at." Another beat, heavier. "You're still lead. Give me language when you have it."

"Understood."

She signed off and closed the notebook without closing the case. The HVI band glowed in the victim field like a promise the world had made to Alina Ward and could not keep retroactively.

Outside her window, New Manhattan kept its lanes open. School pods drifted toward docking slots. Kitchens woke on other tiers. The efficient dream continuing—for everyone who had not spent their morning in a perfect room with a dead guest down the hall.

She had built her career on chains nobody argued with. The file offered her another kind of proof—**934**, *colleague*, stall story—and asked her to pretend they rhymed.

Something doesn't fit, she thought—not fear, not yet, only weight—and she wrote nothing else on the page until she knew which sentence the file would accept.

The case stayed open.

She cut the sidebar before it could suggest otherwise, and went back to work.
