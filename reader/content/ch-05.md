# Chapter 5

The request was professional curiosity wearing its Sunday clothes.

Mara had told Chen she was cross-referencing Continuity Infrastructure personnel for the Crane file—true enough to file, vague enough to mean almost anything. What she wanted was context: the mythology that made men like Julian Crane speak in seals and credentials as if grief were a scheduling problem. The city loved its heroes the way it loved its scores—publicly, numerically, with graphs that pretended feeling was a lag issue.

**8841-A** stayed open in the corner of her workspace like a second monitor she couldn't close. Alina Ward's Platinum band. Crane's perfect alibi. Eyes in a chair that had not been consulted. *Something doesn't fit* had become a private weight she carried between beats, not a line she repeated aloud.

She logged into the CIB historical archive with her investigator clearance and typed a name the whole world already knew.

**Adrian Vale.**

The interface offered her tiers of access—public myth, institutional record, restricted aftermath. She chose the middle path the way she chose most paths: enough depth to matter, not enough to trigger a review. The preview theater dimmed. A single chair. Headphones optional. The bureau's idea of intimacy.

The first clip was childhood.

Not dossier childhood—no birth metrics, no school rankings—only a boy on a roof with a cheap telescope and a sky that had not yet been priced. Wind in his hair. A voice off-camera asking if he could see it yet. He shook his head, grinning the way children grinned when wanting was still allowed to be loud.

Mara watched longer than the clip required. Her father had kept a ledger, not a telescope, but the posture was the same: leaning toward something the world hadn't finished explaining.

The archive advanced without asking permission.

Early Relay footage followed—hardware that looked obsolete and holy at once: crown rigs, cable trays, technicians in shirts that said *FARSHORE* before Farshore became a verb. Vale younger, sharper at the edges, speaking to interviewers with the exhausted charisma of a man who had learned that audiences would forgive almost anything if you sounded like you knew where you were going.

Myth forming in real time—not because he lied, but because the world needed a story larger than its fear.

* * *

The montage that came next was not the Prologue. Mara had seen the full walk in training modules years ago—billions watching, the kneel, the visor, the line that still lived in quote databases: *I can feel the cold.* The archive gave her fragments instead: prep rooms, suit checks, technicians arguing about tolerances in voices that didn't know they were making history.

Farshore Station on screen as a dot of human intention against Mars red. Launch countdowns she did not replay beat for beat. A clip of Vale on Earth in the sync rig, eyes closed, hands steadying on straps—the biological body the public forgot when the Second looked perfect on regolith.

She did not need the whole miracle again. She needed the shape of it: **billions watched** because the world believed continuity could be exported, that distance was a problem engineering could politely delete.

Interview segments rolled with warmer lighting—Vale seated, not performing exhaustion, performing competence. He laughed once at a question about fear and the laugh was real enough that the audience in the archived studio laughed with him. Mara felt herself lean forward the way people leaned toward warmth in cold rooms.

He was not a saint. He was worse: he was likable.

Mission highlight reel: comm traffic stripped to poetry—*nominal, nominal*—the Second on Mars moving with the precision of a man who had rehearsed wonder until wonder looked like skill. Red dust. A visor lift in a still frame she did not linger on. Applause audio layered over graphics that made the planet small and humanity large.

The archive knew what it was doing. It always did.

On the lower tier of her screen, a notification pulsed from **8841-A**—forensics addendum, nothing urgent. She muted it without reading and let the theater stay dark.

* * *

The consciousness segment arrived without fanfare—title card plain, date twelve years after Farshore, interviewer off-screen.

Vale looked older. Not broken. **Haunted**, maybe, in the way people looked haunted when they had seen a thing the language for which had not yet been approved for public release.

The interviewer asked about continuity. About whether two locations could both feel *real*.

Vale was quiet long enough that the silence became content.

"People want Mars to be the hard part," he said. Not to the camera—to the space slightly left of it, where truth sometimes sat when cameras were too bright. "Rock and vacuum and distance. They want a frontier they can draw on a map."

The clip held on his face. Lines at the eyes. The showman's discipline still there, but thinner.

"The body is a node," he said. "The link is not travel. We keep treating it like travel because travel is a story we know how to clap for."

Mara's pen moved in the notebook without her deciding to write. She stopped herself after three words. Some sentences belonged to archives, not cases.

More quotes came—half-sentences about signal, about receivers, about the embarrassment of being in two places and trusting neither completely. Nothing that explained. Everything that **unsettled**.

Then the locked line, delivered not as a reveal but as a correction:

"The greatest frontier was never space."

The theater stayed silent after it. The archive did not append applause. Vale in the clip looked at the interviewer as if waiting to see whether the world would accept the revision.

Mara sat very still.

She did not know what frontier he meant. She felt the sentence anyway—in her chest, in the scar above her brow that mornings still left alone—the way certain truths arrived before evidence agreed to carry them.

* * *

She closed the archive window and the office returned in gray honesty: policy light, coffee bitter in the scuffed mug, **8841-A** waiting with its wrong-shape and its frozen Second and its owner verified elsewhere by a chain she still trusted more than she trusted her instincts.

That was the fracture, she thought—not that the system lied, but that it could tell the truth beautifully and still clarify nothing.

Vale's myth did not solve Alina Ward. It did not explain eyes that tracked in a chair classified idle. It did not touch Julian Crane's seals or HVI **934** or the sidebar's appetite for malfunction language. Two files, one mind: hero on the screen, homicide on her desk, and no merge the world would accept yet.

Wonder and dread occupied the same frame without agreeing to share.

Mara looked at the case tab, then the notebook where she had almost written *node* and stopped. **8841-A** still refused the stall story. Vale's sentence still refused to stay in the archive.

She did not file the Vale request under obsession. She filed it under context and closed the theater.

Outside, New Manhattan kept its lanes open. Somewhere in Dubai, a city was probably still performing correctly for crowds who forgot half-seconds that did not matter. Somewhere in a kitchen on another tier, a man was likely pouring coffee into a chipped mug while his attention ran in two places at once, and a child was learning which questions the world preferred unanswered.

Mara did not know those scenes. She knew her own: case open, myth echoing, the greatest frontier named and not mapped.

She went back to work and let both sit without choosing which one to feed.
