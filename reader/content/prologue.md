# Prologue

The Continuity Broadcast Center was built the way people build things when they want the future to behave—white walls, glass, no visible seams—so when the future misbehaved you felt it in your body before you had a word for it. From outside it looked like a hospital mated with a launch temple: low white disc on the Nevada flats, antenna arrays like metal trees, floodlights still blazing though the sun was already up, because the event required its own noon. Inside Suite Seven the air was too cold and smelled of sterilised plastic and somebody's citrus cologne pretending this was normal medicine. Rubbish, but fine. You worked with what you had.

Adrian Vale lay in the sync rig with his eyes closed and his hands trying to betray him. Thirty-degree recline. Cranial crown locked over dark hair that needed three kinds of attention and had received none. Forty-eight-hour stubble the cameras would hate if the visor came off. Chest harness over a wrinkled Relay tee that had been white three days ago and was now the colour of not sleeping. Medical gown hanging open like a costume someone hadn't finished. Wrist straps over veins that pulsed too honestly for a man about to walk on another planet by proxy. Stabiliser line in his left arm—the way most of his miracles arrived: on schedule, without asking.

The tremor started in his left fingers anyway.

Small. Humiliating. Three seconds he couldn't take back.

On-screen stats showed it—a spike in the corner of every feed on Earth, **FARSHORE — LIVE** in clean type under his real pulse. Sleep debt **9.2 days**. Cortisol in red. Quite readable, which was the problem. Somewhere a girl in Johannesburg had her father's tablet tilted toward her cereal bowl so she could watch a man shake. The graph jumped. She went still.

Then the stabiliser caught it and smoothed the line into something the markets could pretend to trust: calm of a man who was definitely not terrified.

The world had learned to read his biometrics like a chart. Every twitch a prediction. Most of them wrong. All of them priced in anyway.

Beyond the glass, the largest audience in human history waited in a silence that wasn't silence—markets paused mid-tick, governments frozen in briefing rooms, children pulled from sleep to watch a man in a medical gown become a planet. In the glass he saw himself doubled: exhausted face over blur of gallery light, nations reduced to brightness. Viewership rolled upward. **4.8 billion** and climbing. **RELAY INDEX: PAUSED**. The whole late-2026 economy holding its breath until his nervous system performed.

He couldn't make out faces in the gallery—just tiers of shadow, screen flare—but he knew who was out there. Money. Regulators. Technicians who hated him and needed him to win. True believers who thought Mars was a cathedral. He'd disappointed most of them at least once.

He hadn't slept properly in nine days. Had slept at all only because medical threatened delay, and delay was a luxury the year didn't have. Relay confidence graphs looked like a stairwell fall. His net worth had been disintegrating in public for weeks. His failure was already weather.

Under the right strap his fingers worked a smooth grey rock from a parking lot at seventeen, because it looked like a tooth. He rolled it when he needed to remember he had a body that wasn't a stock price. Warm from skin. Mars, somewhere in the link's impossible reach, was not.

*At this point, Adrian,* someone had said in a windowless room, charts all red on the wall, coffee untouched, *the launch is less dangerous than not launching.*

He'd nodded. Nodding was cheaper than screaming.

*I don't know if I'm trying to save humanity,* he thought, truncating before it became complete despair, *or stop everyone realising none of us know what we're doing.*

Forty hours ago he'd walked a press line and made a bankruptcy joke too honest for the room. Four seconds of silence. He'd almost been grateful—silence meant he was still a person, not yet the logo. Civilisation needed the logo. He had the person left, and the person was knackered.

A med tech adjusted the crown at his temple—careful hands, blank face—and for a second he wanted to ask if this was the right thing. He didn't. She wouldn't answer honestly.

Look—the crown wasn't ornament. Implant lattice, hospital neurology welded to quantum repeater research, the boring stack you could watch mature in journals if you had a decade and weren't also running three companies into the ground. Relay took the stack and refused the reasonable timeline. Neural read/write at the cortex. Entangled pairs between his tissue and the Second's coil at Farshore, refreshed through satellite trunks he'd launched in batches most boards would call a personality disorder.

You didn't upload awareness. You didn't send it like a video call. You correlated it. Critics kept calling it transmission anyway. That was their problem until today.

Farshore was the other half people kept calling magic. Habitat module landed fourteen months ago on a flight he'd half-funded by selling futures on futures—how you paid for a cathedral when the village was already on fire. Uncrewed first. Machines, water lines, pressure seals, the ugly work. His Second's synthetic body had sat on Mars six weeks before today, in station quiet, while Earth medical tried to get his biological nervous system to stop shaking long enough to prove the link at scale. He was not on Mars. His blood was in Nevada. His awareness was the experiment. Occupy Mars, except the verb turned out stranger than anyone's manifesto.

The tech lead's voice on open comm—dry, professional, afraid.

"Depth maximum in ten."

He nodded once in the rig. Jaw tight. On Mars—already, impossibly, already—his Second's gloved hand made the same small motion on the airlock frame in Farshore prep corridor C. Confirmation without words. Two bodies. One decision.

Through the link he saw his Second's arms as if they were his own. Matte white suit. Relay insignia on the shoulder. Slight forward lean of a man who couldn't stand still even when standing still. Narrow corridor, intentional: hex lighting cool blue along the bulkhead, dust-free deck plating with traction hexes embedded, Continuity logo above the cycle panel. Smile without a mouth. Corporate.

Polymer under his palms—the same instant bite the straps gave his wrists in Suite Seven. No lag. No ghost of delay. No polite allowance for the speed of light. Beyond the airlock, he could already feel the difference waiting: not polymer. Regolith. A world that didn't care about traction hexes.

His stomach turned. The rock slipped in his palm on Earth. His Second's hands did not slip.

Ten.

Nine.

Eight.

Integers dry in his ear. No music. No anthem. Just numbers. On the screen angled above the rig, two breath waveforms leaned toward each other like they wanted to merge. His chest rose in Nevada. His Second's chest rose in the corridor. Same rhythm. Same count. Two HUD timers he wasn't supposed to read at once and could.

Seven.

Six.

Sync bar filling green. Clean. Wrong. His body braced for the old failure—the half-second stutter, mind arriving after the foot, proof that awareness belonged in one skull and one set of bones. Every prior test had taught his nervous system to expect delay. Delay was how reality apologised for distance.

Five.

Four.

Three.

Two.

One.

Zero.

Mars was here.

He didn't move. Couldn't yet. Pressure sat in the boots on hex deck plating—weight in the knees, fan whisper in a suit that wasn't his biological suit but was, impossibly, him. In Suite Seven his ankles flexed against the straps like he'd taken a step on another planet while his body stayed horizontal on Earth. Suite Seven and corridor C occupied the same second. Wrong. Historic. He held it the way you held a match near fuel—knowing what came next.

Someone in medical swore softly off-feed. Good. Physics ought to swear back.

For one fractured instant he couldn't tell which floor he was on. Rig tilted. Corridor tilted. Both down. Copper taste—stress breaking loose, scatter threatening order. Neither body felt primary. Both felt borrowed. He reached for a joke. Dumb. Human. The joke died like it had seen the view.

On Mars, his Second did not stumble. Composed. Core calm. The version the link thought cameras deserved. That would bother him later. Not now.

*You're still thinking about transmission,* he thought—the fight he'd had with physicists for three years. *There's nothing to transmit. The receiver was always local. Paired states agreeing. No information faster than light. Correlation doesn't need to be.*

If reality was an informational structure—and he had become, privately, the worst person at parties for this—it wasn't a cartoon simulation on a server in the sky. Relay built a second tuner. He didn't say that on the feed. Some truths made civilisation buy in. Some made it flinch.

"Latency?" the control lead asked. Too careful.

"Zero," someone said. Again, like repetition made it safer. "Zero point zero."

Not latency. **Correlation lock.** The board said zero because the instruments measured the wrong metaphor—round-trip packet time for something that had never been packets. Adrian almost laughed—the helpless kind—and swallowed it.

The screen above the rig showed the control room going still—engineers at consoles, one lead pulling her headset off like it was broken, latency column a vertical line of zeros where classical networking wasn't supposed to allow zeros. Somewhere a physicist who'd called him a fraud in public was watching the same number and developing a crack in his worldview. Join the queue.

Walking was the only thing that still made sense. One awareness. Two places. The planet watching. Alone.

Each bootfall on the hex deck—small sound, disgustingly accurate through the link. When he reached the airlock his Second's gloved fingers hit the cycle panel from muscle memory. Earth fingers twitched in the straps. He'd look away from that later.

The inner door cycled. Amber light through the visor. Three seconds of pump vibration in the boots. Green blink.

Exterior door still closed. Red dust waiting on the other side.

The exterior door opened.

Red light flooded the visor—not corrected, not softened, not passed through the polite filters of the life he'd built on Earth. Raw Martian afternoon. A colour that wasn't quite red: iron, rust, dried blood in powder form, lit by a sun smaller than the one he'd been born under in Pretoria and learned to ignore because the maths of survival didn't care about nostalgia.

Inside the module: human order. Outside: a world that had never made room for human noise. The threshold was a line in the dust. He crossed it.

Sound deleted itself.

Not lowered. Deleted. Commentary in his ear cut mid-word—an analyst saying **"histor—"** and then nothing. Suite hum gone. The distant pulse of everyone watching—gone. His world narrowed to fan hiss, link tone, breath counting in the helmet. Wealth had deleted sound in penthouse elevators on Earth; Mars deleted it properly.

He let the wide shot happen.

Rust sky bleeding into black-blue zenith. Horizon curve so gentle it looked like a mistake. Farshore behind him—white module, landing struts, bundled cable runs like veins—already toy-small against a world that had never asked for toys. Phobos a pinprick if you knew where to look. He did. He'd been looking at sky maps since he was a kid in a bedroom full of dead cities while other children collected football cards or whatever normal children collected. He'd wanted extinction museums. He'd gotten one. He was standing in it.

Wrong kind of alive.

His boot touched regolith.

Dust behaved like dust: slow, indifferent, slightly ashamed to be disturbed. The print stayed—clean-edged depression in rust-coloured fines. First human punctuation on a page that had never needed language. He didn't lift the boot yet. Let the cameras count the hold. Let procedure panic in its own channel.

EVA timer on the HUD started—**00:00:47**, **00:00:48**, **00:00:49**—and he let it run. Proof wasn't a pose at the airlock. Proof was weight over time.

He walked because stunts were for people who still had margins.

He lifted his boot. Set it down one metre out. The print behind him was alone in the dust—a single mark, beginning of a trail the planet would carry longer than any broadcast. Then another step. The walk had started whether the world was ready or not.

One metre.

Regolith gave under the boot—a sensation so honest it made his eyes sting in Suite Seven where nothing had changed and everything had. Individual grains through the sole. Cold climbing the suit's thermal layers. Not pain. Not yet. Information from a world that didn't care if he labelled it. His shadow lengthened on the dust, darker shape moving with him, the only thing on Mars that looked like it belonged in a story with an ending.

He stood in that shadow one beat longer than procedure wanted. Fan hiss. Link tone. His breath. The planet could wait.

Two metres. He stepped around a fist-sized rock because his brain still insisted on courtesy even here. Second print, shallower on the near side where the fines were loose. The link made the balance adjustment feel like a gift and a theft at once. Both could be true.

Three metres. Sky thin black-blue above rust. Horizon curved with a gentleness that made Earth feel like a rumour you could choose to believe in. Beauty without comfort. The kind that didn't offer to keep you. He'd spent his life selling comfort to civilisation. Out here comfort didn't apply.

Four metres. Five.

The ground rose—a gentle slope toward a low ridge that existed for no human reason except to exist. Each step changed the angle of his boot on fines. The link translated the difference without mercy. Prints behind him—a sentence getting longer, fourth depression deeper where his weight settled, fifth angled where the slope began. A trail now. Proof you could read from orbit if you cared to look.

On Earth his legs twitched in the rig again—leak the public feed cut around, honesty the medical team wrote on a chart he'd never read. Mars body stayed composed. Steady breath on the HUD. Hands still at his sides. He noticed that and didn't have language for why it unsettled him yet: the version of him other people got to keep.

Six metres. Suit joints whispered when he moved—distant paper. Every schoolroom on the planet was watching this waveform. Geography had stopped mattering for **attention**. Not for bodies. Not yet. He could feel it in his knees—something already changed, no word for it yet that wasn't marketing.

Seven metres.

Commentary tried to come back—two voices overlapping, then dropping to whispers, then nothing. Scripts built for delay, for buffers, for the safe lie that he was somewhere else watching a feed. There was no feed between him and his footfall. Only now. Dead air where analysis used to live.

He almost said something stupid into the open channel—*Hello, Mars* or *We did it* or some other sentence that would age badly by dinner. He swallowed it. Humanity was the walk, not the caption.

He kept walking because stopping early turned miracle back into marketing.

Eight.

Earth hung in the sky. Bright pinhole. Blue-white. Impossibly small. Room where his blood was still warm. His boot came down clean while his left leg jerked in the rig—a split second the feed would cut later, honesty the medical team would chart. Connection wasn't together. He was learning that the hard way.

Nine metres. Ten.

HUD timer crossed from yellow into red. EVA window complaining. He ignored it the way you ignored boards until you couldn't. Sound thinned again—not deleted this time, just him refusing to listen. Shadow stretched longer downslope, his shape and the mast shadow crossing like bad geometry. Camera mast turned when he turned. He was absurdly aware of the camera angle. Still a geek. Still him. Good.

On Earth his legs twitched again at the red timer. Mars body didn't flinch. Steady breath. Composed face inside the helmet. The version they got to keep.

Eleven metres. The ridge was closer now—nothing heroic about it, just enough elevation to make the module look smaller behind him. Regolith colour shifted where wind had scoured a harder pan, paler stripe across rust. His boot left a shallower print there. Detail a drone would have smoothed out. He let it stay.

Twelve metres. Fan hiss louder in his ears—or maybe the silence on Earth had gotten heavier. In Suite Seven someone adjusted a strap he couldn't see. On Mars nothing adjusted. Suit telemetry in the lower corner of his HUD—oxygen green, pressure green, link green. Everything stable. Everything wrong.

Thirteen.

He stopped—not because the voice told him to, because his body asked. Not fear. Not procedure. Animal request to stand still in a place no animal had ever stood with this kind of attention.

He looked back.

Farshore had shrunk to a white toy against rust—landing struts, cable veins, airlock he hadn't bothered to return to. The print trail between them looked fragile. Human. The kind of thing that would become iconography before anyone agreed what it meant.

Earth still hung in the sky. Smaller from here. He couldn't love it properly from this distance. Couldn't hate it either. Planet that made him. Planet currently betting against him.

Wind didn't come to fill the silence. Almost no air to carry it. That emptiness was the point.

In Suite Seven the medical team watched his vitals climb and flatten—a separate drama no camera cared about.

"EVA window closing," the procedure voice said.

Adrian didn't move. Let the warning repeat. Let them wait with him. They'd waited on his biometrics long enough.

Off-channel, a panicked voice begged him toward the module—not for the feed. Protocol screamed visor integrity, contamination risk, liability language that had never once stopped him when the moment required a **face** instead of a helmet. He turned toward the cameras instead, toward the invisible thread of every open channel routed through his nervous system without being with him in the dust, and knelt.

Slow.

Knees sinking into fines. White suit joints creasing. Servos whispering complaint at the bend. Earth hung in the upper sky behind the mast. Dust clung to the knee panels—messy, human, the sort of detail a drone would have cut. Rubbish. The world didn't need another helmet. It had worshipped helmets long enough.

He removed the visor with gloved hands.

Slower than necessary. Showmanship and sincerity braided—the way he always performed best when he wasn't sure which was which. Latches released. Seal broke with a soft pop the microphone carried with insulting clarity. He lifted the visor away.

Cold hit his cheeks through a face that wasn't in the room where his biological jaw clenched against a tremor no one on Mars could see.

The camera pushed in. Dark hair matted flat inside the helmet ring. Chapped lips. Stubble on the Second's jaw—same stubble the cameras would have hated on Earth. Old acne line at the temple he'd never fixed because perfection was for products, not people. Breath fogging briefly in thin air. Blink. Ordinary. Therefore impossible.

Eyes. Brown. Tired. Slightly too wide. Dry—cold and thin air stinging, no tear, just the animal refusal to look away. Old animal proof. Not a drone. Not an AI. Not a clever mirror. Him—or close enough the distinction would become theology later and nightmare later still.

*Please let this be enough,* he thought. Engineer's prayer. Not a messiah's.

Words he wanted were too small. Physics words. Link words. Gratitude and terror braided so tight he couldn't find the seam.

His lips almost formed something else—gratitude, apology, a joke. He stopped. Let the silence do the work.

Meanwhile Nevada: warmth trapped under straps, stabiliser humming, left fingers trying to shake again while Mars cheeks went numb. Two maps updating at once—suit thermal, skin thermal, lattice beneath both that still lacked a polite word in English. A physicist would call it shared state. He called it feeling, because feeling was what made people pause their arguing and listen.

For a heartbeat neither location felt like home—just two rooms in the same wrong miracle.

His lips parted. He searched for language and found only the truth.

"I can feel the cold," he said.

Quiet. Not shouted. Slight crack on the last word. Breath fog on the second syllable.

Not to the physicists. Not to the investors. Not to the future that would tattoo this sentence on itself and call it hope. He said it because it was true before anyone could spin it.

The silence after the line was the first honest global silence of the Second age.

Nothing moved. Knees in rust dust. Visor in his gloved hand. Link still green—offensive in its stability. Fan hiss. Thin air. His breath in the helmet mic. Dust settling on the white knee panels where it belonged.

Wonder and wrongness. Both true.

Then black.
