The feed found them at dinner.

Month Four, week fifteen. Noah had cleared the plates and left the kitchen panel open the way you left a window cracked when the building promised mild weather—habit, not anxiety, the reflex that said *look once, confirm normal, return to the table.* Gold warmed the laminate where it always warmed on cheap surfaces that didn't pretend to be stone. Through the window wall, New Manhattan performed recovery: tier brows lit, pod lanes harmonized, the city he'd re-enabled after the honest week because grief was expensive and attention was finite and parenting inside unfiltered gray had tired him in ways virtue could not refund.

The sync hum settled in its old bed beneath everything. Iona's homework tile had closed green. The Second dock muted and respectful. Everything working. That was the part he still couldn't make peace with—not failure, but success this quiet after failure had been loud.

Then the public channel chimed.

Not alarm. **Update**—the polite tone institutions used when they wanted kitchens to feel informed instead of implicated. **CONTINUITY BRIEFING · HARBOR STACK · SERVICE INTERRUPTION RESOLVED · PUBLIC REASSURED.** Noah's thumb hovered over dismiss. Community threads had already been homework enough. He watched anyway because watching was cheaper than discovering later that your city had lied in a font you didn't recognize.

Iona watched too.

Twelve. Quiet. Precise—not frightened, not performing wonder, the way she went quiet when she stored something in a drawer he wasn't supposed to open yet. She had kept her overlay off for morning block since the failure and turned it back on for homework because, she'd said, *equations looked mean without it.* Noah had not argued. Difference allowed. Not optimized into a lesson.

On screen, the narrative arrived contained.

Smooth anchor voice. Smooth B-roll of Harbor Stack transit lip—crowd nominal, LED boards green, perimeter tape gone or edited into **temporary evaluation complete.** **Isolated sync anomaly.** **No public risk.** **Continuity secures your evening.** The words rhymed with containment copy Noah had learned to recognize since the failure—service interruption, counseling lane, tier benefits, the vocabulary that turned public fear into procedures kitchens could swallow with dinner.

**One Second unit held for evaluation.** Not *Still One*. Not *static*. Not the thumbnail building threads kept sharing with captions that asked questions officials wouldn't answer on camera. **Evaluation.** The noun was already a lid.

Noah wanted to believe it.

Sincerely. Not villainy. Not denial as ideology—**relief** pull, the body exhaling when someone else offered to carry the weight of a clip that had spread through the building thread faster than official copy. He had not watched the raw waveform at full volume. He had seen the thumbnail—frozen posture, wrong eyes, grain where speech should live—and closed it because full volume turned fear into parenting homework and he was tired of homework that didn't come with answers.

Running Double still worked. The habit was muscle. Tonight the muscle wanted the city to be administratively fine so tomorrow's pod schedule could stay green and Iona's equations could look mean in the honest way she preferred and he could be pleasant without performing courage he didn't feel.

"They fixed it," he said—not to Iona, to the room, to the overlay making the anchor's tie look trustworthy. "Temporary thing. You don't need to—"

"It's still on Mira's thread," Iona said.

Of course it was. Twelve-year-olds lived in threads adults pretended were optional.

She didn't show him the screen. She didn't need to. The existence of the thread was enough—proof that official containment and kitchen containment were different countries.

* * *

The briefing continued.

Deputy continuity director—name smooth, face familiar in the way public faces were familiar, trained for calm. **Minor incident at Harbor Stack resolved within standard response window. One Second unit held for evaluation. Primary supported. Gratitude for public patience.** Gratitude. The word landed like a lid.

A sidebar ticker ran beneath the anchor: **MARKETS STEADY · TRANSIT NOMINAL · NO TIER ESCALATION.** Recovery in numbers because numbers were how adults preferred fear to dress when it went out in public.

Noah rinsed a pan he had already rinsed. Hands busy. Hands busy, making safety out of motion when words would have been performance. Iona stayed at the table with her chin on her hand and watched the screen the way she watched adults who thought children only listened when you used their names.

The camera cut to officials flanking the director.

Three faces. Badges. **Calm** in the script—voices even, syntax mild, open lanes in the footer of their sentences. And beneath the script, bodies.

Micro.

The director's smile held one beat too long—the kind of hold that meant *keep smiling until the feed cuts.* The continuity liaison to her left blinked twice before answering a question that wasn't hard, then laughed at the wrong millisecond, a social reflex arriving after the sentence had already chosen calm. The harbor medical officer on the right held his professional stillness with shoulders tight and jaw set; when the anchor asked whether primaries should be concerned, his eyes flicked once toward the teleprompter as if the teleprompter might bite, and his yes arrived a half-second late.

They looked scared.

Not panic. Not incompetence. **Fear wearing calm** the way Noah wore pleasantness—costume with cost, visible if you knew where to look and weren't paying the overlay to hide it for you.

The narrative said recovery. The faces said other.

Dramatic irony in a kitchen with a chipped mug and a daughter who stored questions like treasures.

Iona did not blink.

"Dad."

"Mm."

"They're saying it's fine."

"That's what they're saying."

She watched the liaison's hands clasp too hard on the podium edge—knuckles pale against dark wood, the body betraying the script in the small language children learned before they learned civics. Noah watched Iona watch. The feed's polite audio bed continued beneath the image—the optimization hum of civilization insisting the mix board still worked.

The question arrived in Noah's chest a half-second before Iona spoke it—child and adult seeing the same gap, different obligations about naming it.

Iona said it quietly. Precise. Not cruel. Not the later question about adults lying when fear wore a suit—that would be a different broadcast, a different brightness, a different month. This was **now**, **Harbor**, **faces** on a screen while fruit steam looked cinematic on the table.

"Then why did they look scared?"

The line sat in the kitchen without comfort.

Noah's laugh almost arrived—the Month One reflex, warmth before thought, the deflect that made rooms believe they were safe. It didn't come. Month Four had taught him something too. He turned off the tap he hadn't turned on and put his hand on her shoulder, brief, present.

"They're being careful on camera," he said. "That's their job. Careful words so people don't—"

"Panic."

"Yes."

"Like the clip."

He didn't ask which clip. There was only one clip this week.

"It's complicated, Iona."

The same phrase from Month Three when she'd asked why the city had been honest for three days and then got sponsored again. Fragment only. Warm not cruel. **No answer** in it—hold, not debate, not a speech about bandwidth tiers and maintenance costs and whether mercy counted if someone else paid. He did not owe her a thesis at dinner. He owed her a parent who stayed in the room.

She looked back at the screen.

The director was smiling again. The liaison's hands had unclasped one finger at a time. Recovery narrative continuing on schedule as if recovery were a product you subscribed to and the subscription renewed whether or not you believed the footer.

Iona said nothing else.

That was worse.

* * *

Noah muted the feed.

The apartment did not stop being kind—gold on laminate, window wall blooming distance, sync hum polite—but the silence after the line had its own weight. Iona cleared her snack plate with the careful motions of someone practicing adulthood without requesting permission. She did not look at him. Noah wiped a counter that didn't need wiping.

In the lower corner of his overlay, markets ticked—background weather, not lecture. **CONTINUITY INDEX · STABLE · HARBOR INCIDENT CONTAINED · OPEN LANES.** A calm spin. Green at the top. Recovery narrative in numbers because numbers were how adults lied when they wanted the lie to feel responsible.

Smooth recovery. Smooth exterior. The shape of a civilization reflex he didn't have a name for yet—only the feeling of it in his chest, adjacent to the Month Three unhappiness when everything worked and the question *why* wouldn't rise to language.

Noah stood at the window wall one beat with the mug and the chip at the rim and watched New Manhattan perform being fine on purpose. Tier brows lit. Pod lanes harmonized. Strangers' faces arriving with friction filed down. Beautiful. Genuinely beautiful. The city he'd signed up for again because fatigue had a social score and honesty did not.

He was unhappy anyway.

Not storm unhappy. **Hairline** unhappy—the wrong shape inside a perfect container. He did not re-enable honesty tonight. He did not disable the Veil to prove bravery. He held Iona's question the way you held a stone in a pocket you chose not to shift—warm deflect spent, silence, trust fraying domestic where institutional reassurance had failed to reach a twelve-year-old who watched hands more closely than scripts.

Iona's door closed down the hall—soft, not slammed, the sound of a child choosing privacy over argument because argument required answers adults didn't have.

Somewhere in the building, Mira's thread kept the static. Somewhere in bureau tiers, investigators moved through channels Noah would never see. The city kept mild weather and calm markets and open lanes.

The feed stayed muted.

No answer came.
