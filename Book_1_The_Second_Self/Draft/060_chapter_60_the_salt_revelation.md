CIB forensic did not run headlines.

The lab sat three levels below the bureau's polished floors—gray equipment, honest air, terminals that did not sweeten their outputs—and on the wall screen someone had left a news tile muted: **DUBAI RECOVERING**, recovery B-roll looping over a ticker that pretended green was the same thing as safe. Mara stood with her back to it and her scuffed mug on the bench and let **8841-A** become the room again.

Month Five. Week one bleeding into two. The file had outlived every sidebar label the Continuity stack had tried to feed it—*elevated residence anomaly*, *hardware stall*, *malfunction appetite*—and had acquired the heavier gravity of a case that stopped being a case the moment you admitted what the eyes were doing.

She did not admit it aloud. She opened the archive.

Hush Spire 11-A on the left pane. Guest **Alina Ward**—compliance counsel, Platinum band, temple injury neat enough to look like policy. Owner **Julian Crane** verified elsewhere. Second in the living chair: powered-down pose, perfect lines, **ocular tracking** filed and buried under malfunction until it wasn't.

On the right pane, the Still One packet Chen had escalated after the transit hub incident—public panic, speech attempt, **static** where voice should have been. Different city. Different camera. Same wrongness in the biochemistry column when forensics finally ran the comparison Mara had requested with the flat tone of *stop calling this isolated*.

The merge bar climbed without drama.

**Pattern match: 97.4%**

Mara's pen stopped above the notebook—not surprise, something worse: the body recognizing a conclusion before the mind finished permission. She read the header twice because professional dissociation was a skill and skills could crack.

**STILL ONES — BIOCHEMISTRY INDEX · CROSS-SITE CONVERGENCE**

Below it, subfields she had seen in partial memos for months and never seen **named** on a report she was allowed to hold:

**Salt residue — positive.**

The word was small. That was the obscenity of it—four letters, laboratory font, no flourish. Not a metaphor. Not a mood. A **compound**.

Mara set the mug down carefully, as if sound could contaminate the finding.

* * *

Dr. Priya Nair from trace chemistry did not lecture. She never had—the useful ones didn't—and today she stood at the imaging rig with her sleeves rolled and her voice at the volume of a person who respected rooms that already knew how to scream.

"You asked for mechanism," she said. "Not motive. I'll give you mechanism and leave the motive to whoever still believes motive is a separate department."

The comparison slide loaded: healthy sync on the left—a clean wave, embodiment telemetry from a control Second, green bands, the civilization's favorite picture of itself. On the right, **8841-A** ocular trace overlaid on Still One post-freeze sampling: fracture lines through the sync pathway, crystalline disruption patterns the UI labeled **Salt signature** because someone in a naming committee had run out of poetry and reached for geology.

Quantum disruption, the footer said. **Sync collapse pathway.**

Not death on schedule. Not powered down. **Collapse**—the difference between a machine idling and a mind caught mid-sentence in a body that could no longer negotiate exit.

Mara stared at the slide until her eyes dried.

"Say it plain," she said.

Priya did not soften it. "Salt forces sync fracture at the embodiment layer. Motor output stops. Autonomic stall presents like Continuity preservation mode—Platinum buildings love that flag because it sounds like care. But the awareness index doesn't zero."

The next pane loaded without being asked: **AWARENESS INDEX — POST-FREEZE · STILL ONE COHORT**.

Numbers. Graphs. A column marked **consciousness persistence probability** that had no business existing in a forensic suite that used to worry about homicide timelines and guest registry conflicts.

Mara read the report once for the words and once for the silence between them—the way she had learned to read autopsy addenda when the coroner was trying not to be the first person in the chain to say *alive* about someone the room had already decided to call *idle*.

Victims **conscious**. Unable to act. Unable to die on the schedule the UI pretended was mercy.

She thought of Alina Ward's body in the suite—biological deceased, clean violence, the world's simplest story—and of eyes in a chair that had tracked her without permission. She thought of Tessa Rowe saying *everyone did until they stopped apologizing* and of a verification suite passing a memory the database refused to carry.

She thought of Dubai clips she had not watched on purpose and of a girl somewhere in the city asking questions adults were already failing.

"Duration," Mara said.

Priya's mouth tightened. "That's where ethics goes quiet."

The slide advanced. Medical footnotes. Continuity research cross-references flagged **restricted**. A clause at the bottom in the flat language legal used when it had decided some horrors were easier to store than to cure:

*Consciousness persistence post-Salt fracture: **indefinite** within current observation window. No validated termination pathway. Mercy protocol: **classified**.*

Indefinite.

Not long. Not until rescue. **Indefinite**—the word that turned a forensic finding into a theology problem and a civilization into a defendant.

Mara's hand found the mug rim. Bitter. Real. The chip in the enamel caught her thumb the way it always did when the world asked her to keep standing in rooms that had stopped deserving standing.

"How long have we known the index doesn't zero?" she asked.

"Ask people above my pay grade." Priya's voice was steady. Too steady—the steadiness of someone who had already screamed in a parking garage and come back to work because work was the only shape panic could be filed into. "I'm telling you what the sample says. Salt doesn't finish the sentence. It **holds** it open."

* * *

Mara took the report to the comm alcove because some calls needed walls that didn't listen like witnesses.

Lena answered on the second ring—mentor tone, mentor patience, the voice that had trained Mara to trust verification when the world preferred convenience. Background muted: lab or office, the soft hum of a life still organized around answers.

"Mara." Not *what's wrong*—Lena never wasted panic on greeting. "I saw the Dubai tags on your board. Are you—"

"I need a sanity check on a Salt finding," Mara said. "8841-A convergence. Still One biochemistry. Awareness index non-zero post-freeze."

Silence on the line—not dead air, **managed** air, the pause of a person choosing grammar.

"I've seen the preliminary crosswalk," Lena said. "Forensics is being conservative with language."

"Indefinite isn't conservative. It's accurate."

Another pause. Shorter. "The cohort data is noisy. Public freeze events introduce contamination variables we can't—"

"There is no public in 8841-A. There was a chair and eyes that tracked me and a guest dead in a suite while the building called it preservation mode."

Lena exhaled—not fear, not quite; something smoother, like a door being closed with care. "You're tired. Month Five is compressing everyone. Don't let a pattern match become a story before chain of custody finishes."

Mara closed her eyes. She had wanted comfort from this call—mentor, path, the old fiction that institutions corrected when shown mirrors. What arrived was **calm** calibrated to the millimeter, the kind that sounded like concern and behaved like containment.

"Lena," she said. "If the index doesn't zero—if Salt holds consciousness open without exit—what do we call that in a report civilians will eventually read?"

"We call it under review until review completes." Lena's voice softened into the register Mara had trusted for a decade—the one that made bureaucracy feel like ethics. "You taught me to wait for evidence before you accuse a room. Wait. Let me pull the restricted memos on my side. We'll talk when—"

"When what?"

"When it's safe to talk."

The line clicked to hold music so polite it felt like an insult.

Mara stared at the muted headline still looping on the wall—Dubai smiling, markets green, recovery performing stability—and understood, with cold clarity, what adults were doing when they were scared.

They were not always lying in words.

Sometimes they lied in **cadence**.

She ended the call without waiting for Lena to come back.

Back at the terminal, she opened the vault access request form—the one Chen had told her existed and hoped she would never need—and typed with hands that did not shake because shaking was a luxury that contaminated rooms.

**Facility:** Still One storage · regional archive  
**Purpose:** Witness scale · case 8841-A convergence  
**Justification:** Awareness index confirmed · Salt mechanism on record · investigator must verify cohort in person before chain escalates to public counsel.

Professional language. True language. The only language left that did not require her to pretend indefinite was a typo.

She hit submit.

Somewhere above her, Dubai kept recovering on mute.

Somewhere below, rows of held sentences waited in cold air she could already feel in her teeth.

Mara picked up her notebook and wrote one line in handwriting the database would never autosave:

*They are awake.*

Then she went to get her badge for the door that did not apologize.
