The warrant tone reached Cord Junction before the building knew how to be afraid.

Month Two, week eight. Night thin enough to show freight cables through the window like veins under skin. Mercer heard it on the public channel first—not his phone, not a smart relay, the old radio on the shelf he kept because radios lied slower than feeds and sometimes lying slower was the only way to hear truth arrive. **CONTINUITY SECURES INVESTIGATION · CONSULTANT LINK IDENTIFIED · PUBLIC REASSURED.** Mild language. Open lanes in the footer. The city's habit of selling calm while it moved bodies through procedure.

Then the secondary tone—closer, bureaucratic, the polite final chime of lives changing without raising their voice. Warrant noise. Not footsteps yet. Not badges on his door. The institution completing itself at the speed of channels he had spent Month Two learning to read from the wrong side of consultant disclosure.

A second ping hit the stripped interface on the table—consultant channel, not personal, the line the bureau kept for people it still called external minds until it didn't. **WARRANT ACTIVE · PHYSICAL APPREHENSION AUTHORIZED · SUBJECT: MERCER, ELI.** No drama in the font. Drama was for feeds. This was procedure announcing itself to the machinery of capture with the same calm illegibility paperwork used before bodies moved.

Mercer read it once and looked away. Reading twice was how guilty men behaved in stories. He was not in a story. He was in Cord Junction, where stories arrived late and left metadata.

His hands moved before his mind finished arguing.

Not guilt. That distinction still mattered in the apartment where the data wall waited like a witness who wouldn't testify in rooms that cleaned themselves. He had not killed Alina Ward. He had not authored the third peak. He had seen it—seen it early, seen it late, seen it **predate** his name on the axis until terror replaced pride—and the bureau would not care about distinctions that didn't fit sidebar grammar. **Illegal embodiment.** **Consultant overreach.** The nouns they needed now that pattern had outrun narrative.

He was afraid of the wrong authority closing on the wrong scale.

That was different from running because you had done the thing. Different from villain flight. Different from the cheap story the contained headline already sold. Mercer had spent a month adjacent to homicide files and public brilliance and the very reasonable possibility that custody turned graphs into evidence in rooms that forgot on schedule. Fear, not confession. Running from **size**, not from guilt.

The manual apartment heard everything the Spine forgot to perform.

No chime when the warrant propagated. No overlay sweetening the window. Just the fan whirring, the clock ticking, the lift shaft silent because the lift had been manual since before he signed the lease. Four flights down, field teams might already be assigning delay—manual building, relay dead zone, suspect who chose friction on purpose. He had chosen friction for years. Tonight friction was survival.

He crossed to the door and listened—not paranoia, calibration. Old building, old wood, old habits. Footsteps on metal stairs sounded different from footsteps on policy. Nothing yet. The warrant gave him minutes the smart tiers would not have given: dead relay, no lobby camera, no courteous countdown on a wall panel. Manual life had always been an aesthetic. Now it was math.

He went back to the data wall.

* * *

Erase trails.

Behaviour, not speech. Mercer had learned that lesson in symposium rooms where brilliance was performance and in Cord Junction where performance was a liability. He pulled cables from the data wall—three mismatched screens, fourth laptop on a dictionary, the ugly legible stack he had built because vendor dashboards lied by design. USB drives into pockets. Printouts into the sink, not the shredder—the shredder hummed like policy. Paper soaked faster when you added kettle water and dish soap, the ordinary chemistry of a life that might need to leave without arguing with metadata.

Hard drives met the hammer in the toolbox under the sink—two strikes each, enough to make recovery expensive, not enough to perform rage for an empty room. The sound was dull, domestic, wrong for a man the public channel was already teaching to call suspect. He worked methodically. Witness hands. Maintenance hands. The same hands that had scrolled an axis left until pride died.

Session logs wiped on the stripped interface. Not perfectly—perfection was a vendor fantasy—but enough for manual life: local caches cleared, merge overlays saved to nothing, the consultant disclosure folder deleted and deleted again because once was how amateurs worked. His credentials copy went into the go-bag, not because identity was guilt, because identity was a file until it wasn't.

The go-bag had waited half-zipped since week four—accusation of foresight, unfinished admission in the corner by the door. He finished it now: cash in inconvenient denominations, paper map in pencil, spare interface stripped of bloat, metal keys, toothbrush still in packaging, shirt folded wrong. Zip closed. Weight real on his shoulder. Not running yet. **Ready** for a man who had learned that seeing clearly was no longer compatible with staying convenient.

On the public channel, the sidebar refreshed—**CONSULTANT LINK IDENTIFIED**—and Mercer felt the animal part of him register capture geometry without needing a diagram. They had a name. They had a charge frame. They had his consultant token tied to Harbor pulls he hadn't authorized—or had he? The distinction blurred under fear the way distinctions always blurred when institutions moved. He did not stop to litigate. Litigating was for rooms with lawyers. Leaving was for maintenance pockets where relays died and schedules forgot to catch people who read telemetry with their own eyes.

He did not call Investigator Voss.

Still. The rhyme held from Month One—pride, fear, the graph that predated him too large for a phone call, the woman with the tight jaw and the punishment-coffee laugh who read raw logs only after the UI told her which ones mattered. He would not hand her a wiped wall and call it help. Not now. Not with warrant noise in the air and the larger thing still moving beneath every chart he had ever drawn.

* * *

The data wall went dark screen by screen until one layer remained—not merge telemetry, not case rhymes, the architecture scrape he had pulled from a back channel he didn't deserve and hadn't looked at until the predates axis made looking mandatory.

Infrastructure layer.

Not exposition. Not briefing. A visual leak—the kind that arrived as schematic sinew and routing bone, the city's body beneath its beauty. Merge signatures were surface weather. This was substrate: continuity pipes feeding embodiment relays feeding something older than consultant disclosure, older than **8841-A**, older than the third peak's first pulse. Lines converged on nodes the UI did not label for public tiers. A frame flickered—partial, corrupted, enough—showing traffic that did not belong to any case file he had touched.

He recognized the geometry from symposium slides and lied-about appendices—layers beneath layers, the place where **Continuity** stopped being a brand and became plumbing. Embodiment relays branched into trunks the public maps never drew. Session seals looked like weather from here. Owner windows looked like decoration. And through the trunk lines, traffic moved on a schedule that did not consult homicide investigators or deputy directors or consultants who asked women how often they chose difficulty.

Something moving through the architecture the way freight moved through cables: indifferent, scheduled, **not Mercer-shaped**.

The third peak ghosted on the overlay—left of his activation window, left of his experiments, left of every story that would have made the world simpler if he had been origin. It did not pulse for him tonight. It **held**. Patient. Connected to the leak frame by a thread the dashboard refused to name—a thin bright filament linking merge telemetry to infrastructure nodes that had no business sharing a screen with a Cord Junction apartment built from dead relays and honest dictionaries.

Larger mover.

No speech formed. No hero declaration. No experimenter unmasked. Mercer stood in front of the leak visual with the go-bag on his shoulder and understood only what witness work allowed: the conspiracy was wider than an arrest order, wider than illegal embodiment, wider than the man the bureau had decided to call sufficient. Something moved beneath continuity itself. He could not unsee it again. Predates had been terror. This was **scale**—the kind that made running rational instead of guilty.

His reflection in the dark glass showed a face without performance—no symposium smile, no consultant charm, no public brilliance purchased to camouflage private pattern. Just a man seeing plumbing move and knowing custody would shrink the sight into a charge frame that fit sidebar grammar.

He screenshot nothing. Saving would leave teeth. He looked until the image burned into the kind of memory that didn't sync—maintenance memory, manual memory, the kind you carried in body instead of cloud.

Then he killed the last screen.

Fan still whirring. Clock still ticking. Kettle cold. Dishes in the sink with dissolved paper pulp. The apartment looked like a man had been living here because a man had been living here—clutter honest, cables slack, chair learned to witness posture. Nothing dramatic. Nothing villain. A consultant's nest in a maintenance pocket, wiped but not staged.

* * *

Off-grid path.

Metal keys. Manual stairs. No relay ping when he latched the door—he tested it once, twice, the way you tested manual things because manual was the only honesty left. Four flights down without a chime. Cord Junction alley where freight shadows kept the Spine's polish at arm's length. The go-bag adjusted on his shoulder. His breath stayed shallow—not performance, not swagger, the spent calm of a man who had used charm all month in public and had none left for the street.

He moved east because east was wrong on purpose—away from the maintenance pocket's main stairwell, toward the service gap where scaffold met freight cable and the city pretended no one lived who couldn't afford to be seen. Boots on grit. No running. Running was how feeds learned your shape. Walking was how manual people disappeared—slow, ordinary, a body among bodies if there had been bodies, alone because there were not.

He did not look back at the window.

Fear complete. Guilt absent. The contained headline would continue without his help—consultant link, solved grammar, open lanes in the footer. Let them have it. Let the institution climb four flights and find what he left behind.

* * *

The apartment after:

Empty chair at the data table. Screens dark. Sink full of paper soup. Go-bag gone. Manual switch on the wall—off, because off was a gesture the search team would read as intention without requiring him to perform intention on a feed.

The consultant disclosure printout was gone— dissolved with the other paper—but the table still held ring stains from nights stacked on nights, coffee rings matching older rings the way cases matched cases when you stopped believing coincidence was a profession. A single soaked page clung to the sink edge—merge chart residue, three peaks blurred into ink, illegible now, useless for court and therefore safe for the story the bureau wanted.

What remained was ordinary mess. What was missing was the man.

Field teams would note delay. Manual building. Relay dead zone. They would climb four flights without a chime and find an apartment that did not know how to perform guilt—no packed luxury, no villain staging, just wiped drives and honest clutter and the fan still whirring because fans did not care about warrants.

Something larger kept moving in channels he no longer touched. Not named. Not explained. Visual residue only: in the dead screen's bezel, for one frame before power bleed, a ghost of routing lines converging where no case file had permission to look—then black.

Gone.

Fear, not confession.

Larger shadow.

Mercer was not in the frame anymore.

The latch had clicked. The alley had taken him. Manual life swallowed the trail the way manual life always had—without spectacle, without score, without the city's habit of making capture look like justice.

Night kept moving above Cord Junction—freight cables, mild weather, open lanes—while the apartment stayed empty for whoever arrived first with badges and delay estimates and the professional surprise of a suspect who had chosen not to be convenient.
