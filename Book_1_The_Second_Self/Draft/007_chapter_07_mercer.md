The consultant request came through channels that pretended not to be urgent.

**8841-A** had acquired gravity in the building's imagination—Platinum stall, guest homicide, owner verified elsewhere, eyes that tracked without permission from the file—and someone in Continuity Infrastructure had suggested an external mind while the sidebar still called it hardware. Deputy Director Chen had forwarded the name without endorsement, which was her way of saying *listen, but don't let him write your report.*

**Eli Mercer.**

Mara had heard the name the way people in her line heard names that lived adjacent to policy: symposium clips, architecture papers, the kind of reputation that made institutions nervous because it sounded like independence. Not a suspect. Not yet. A consultant CIB could disclaim if he said something inconvenient.

She took the transit to the interview annex because the annex was where the bureau put conversations it didn't want overheard by its own polished floors. The waiting room was wrong on purpose—or wrong in a way Platinum had trained her to find bizarre. A manual clock on the wall, ticking audibly. Fluorescent panels that did not adjust for mood. Chairs with worn fabric. A paper cup dispenser instead of a self-styling kiosk. Coffee that tasted like punishment.

Mara sat with her case tablet and her paper notebook and waited long enough to feel the room's statement: *some people still choose this.*

The inner door opened without a chime.

Eli Mercer was not tall, not styled, not performing entrance—just a man in a jacket that had seen weather, carrying a paper folder like a prop from another decade. He smiled as if they had already argued and he had won the friendly version.

"Investigator Voss," he said. "Thank you for not sending a drone."

"I don't have drones," Mara said.

"Everyone has drones. They're called interns." He stepped aside, inviting her in. "Shall we?"

* * *

The interview room was worse than the waiting area in the useful way: no overlay hooks, no Continuity Steward voice offering calm, only a table, two chairs, and a window that showed a wall because privacy was sometimes a view of nothing.

Mercer did not sit first. He waited until she did, then took the chair across from her at an angle—informal, attentive, the posture of someone who had learned that symmetry made people confess too early.

"CIB asked me to look at your stall case," he said. "I told them I don't look at cases. I look at habits."

"I'm not here for philosophy."

"You will be." He said it lightly, not as prophecy. "Humor me. How did you enter the unit?"

Mara gave him procedure—seal, suite, guest, chair, owner chain. She did not give him the eyes. Some things were still hers until the file forced them public.

Mercer listened with the annoying focus of a person who could repeat your sentences back with the commas in the right places. When she finished, he tapped the paper folder—not opening it, just acknowledging it existed.

"How often do you choose difficulty when convenience is available?"

The question landed too clean for the room. Mara stared at him.

"I'm sorry?" she said, because procedure required a handle.

"Professionally. Personally." He leaned back. "When the overlay offers to summarize a witness, do you read the witness? When the building offers you *malfunction*, do you accept the noun?"

"I accept evidence," she said.

"Evidence arrives pre-wrapped in Platinum." Not an accusation. Almost cheerful. "I'm asking if you ever cut the string."

She laughed.

It escaped before she could stop it—short, involuntary, the sound of someone who had been holding her jaw too tight for three days and had been offered an unexpected valve. She cut it off immediately and felt heat in her face.

"That's not an answer," she said.

"No," Mercer agreed. "But it was honest."

He opened the folder at last—printouts, not a tablet, because of course—and slid one page across. **8841-A** header. Hush Spire 11-A. She recognized her own case in the wrong medium, like seeing your face in a mirror that used different light.

"Guest timeline," he said. "Review session **11:14 PM**. Building log places guest asleep **4:12 AM**. Owner seal intact. Second idle. All very neat."

"Neat isn't illegal."

"No. But neat is a choice." He pointed without touching her screen. "You filed owner verified. You filed ocular anomaly. You filed homicide. What did you file about the room's morning?"

Mara's fingers found her notebook edge. "Auto-sanitize. Expected feature in Platinum tier."

"Timestamp?"

She knew it. She had seen it in the corner of the UI while Alina Ward was still dead in the next room and the chair was still watching her. **0638.**

"Six-thirty-eight," she said.

"Steward stall flag?"

"Six-forty-one."

Mercer raised his eyebrows—the whole performance of *well, well* without saying it. "The room cleaned itself before it admitted it was dirty."

Mara did not answer. The shape she had been carrying since *something doesn't fit* shifted, not into clarity, into **width**.

* * *

"You think Crane sanitized," she said.

"I think Platinum sanitizes." Mercer folded his hands. "Crane is Continuity Infrastructure. The building is Continuity Infrastructure. The steward is Continuity Infrastructure. At a certain level, suspect stops being a person and becomes a interface."

"That's conspiracy."

"That's architecture." He stood, restless energy moving through him—the tell of a mind that couldn't stay still when it saw pattern. "Continuous identity requires continuous correction. Sync stalls are errors. Errors get corrected. Sometimes correction precedes notification. Sometimes notification precedes investigation. Your job is to notice order."

He walked to the blank window and talked anyway—latency budgets, steward thresholds, owner-session seals, legal Seconds as punctuation while biology attended meetings elsewhere. Not a lecture. A riff.

Mara's spine straightened. "You're not on the warrant."

"I'm not asking to be."

"You're suggesting the entire class of evidence in Platinum residences is—"

"Compromised by convenience?" He turned. "I'm suggesting you stop letting the building write the first draft."

She held his gaze because procedure was a kind of mercy and she needed mercy that wasn't comfort. "Why do you care?"

Wrong question. She saw him decide whether to answer honestly.

"Because I know what it costs when correction runs ahead of truth," he said, quieter. Not villain speech. Not confession. A line with weight behind it she couldn't parse yet.

The room went still.

Mara pulled **8841-A** back up on her tablet—not to hide from him, to reassert the job. "If you're consulting, consult. What am I missing besides timestamps?"

Mercer looked at her for a long beat, half-smile not cruel.

"You logged the guest's paper notebook as an object of interest," he said. "You didn't log that Crane's unit had **no guest paper on file** for three prior overnight stays in twelve months. Alina Ward brought the only non-indexed analog object into a room that auto-sanitizes trace. You noticed the notebook because it was rare. You didn't ask why rarity arrived the night someone died."

The air in the room changed temperature without the system noticing.

Mara opened the file field she should have opened two days ago. He was right. The prior guest manifests—digital only, compliant, boring. Alina's notebook stood alone like a wrong answer in a class that preferred Relay.

"That's—" She stopped. *Circumstantial.* *Not proof.* *Not Mercer on the warrant.*

All true. All insufficient to erase the fact that he had seen it and she hadn't.

* * *

She ended the session the way she ended sessions with people who made her feel unbalanced: with paperwork.

Mercer signed the consultant disclosure without drama. No manifesto. No phone number scrawled on a napkin. He capped his pen and looked genuinely tired for the first time—not performing fatigue, spending it.

"Don't arrest me," he said. "I'm expensive enough already."

"I'm not arresting anyone."

"Pity." The joke landed wrong and he knew it. "Investigator—choose difficulty once this week. Read one raw log without the steward summary. See what happens."

"I read raw logs."

"You read them after the UI tells you which ones matter."

He left first, paper folder under his arm, footsteps too loud in the unoptimized corridor until the sound died into institutional hum.

Mara stood in the interview room with **8841-A** open and her notebook in her hand and the case wider than it had been an hour ago—not because Mercer had solved it, because he had **widened** it. Sanitize before flag. Notebook without precedent. Owner verified elsewhere while eyes tracked here. Platinum wrong-shape victim. Sidebar hunger for malfunction language.

She did not trust Mercer.

She also could not unsee the timestamps.

Chen pinged her ear as she hit the elevator.

"Status?"

"Consult complete," Mara said. "No actionable suspect."

"And?"

Mara watched the floor numbers climb. Outside the annex, New Manhattan would still look solved—pods on lanes, kitchens waking, a child somewhere answering *Books* while a class laughed, a hero's archive sentence still glowing in her memory like a door left ajar.

"And the case is bigger than the stall story," she said.

Chen was quiet a beat. "Keep it quiet."

"Understood."

The elevator chimed. Mara stepped onto CIB gray floors off balance—which was, she suspected, the first honest thing Mercer had sold her.

She went back to her desk.

**8841-A** waited. The notebook page in her hand waited. The building's first draft waited to be challenged.

She chose difficulty badly, late, and with her jaw still tight from the laugh she shouldn't have given him.

The file stayed open.
