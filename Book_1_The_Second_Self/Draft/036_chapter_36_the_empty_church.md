# Chapter 36 — The Empty Church

The sidebar item had no case number worth defending in daylight.

Month Three, week twelve—the same week kindness scores were being debated in feeds Mara refused to open before coffee and the bureau still called exhaustion *fit for duty* because duty kept queues moving. **Community gathering · off-feed edge · old religious site · attendance rising · no optimization flag.** Chen had attached one line: *Map only. Not infiltration.* The phrasing was familiar from Month Two, when community mapping had been a lie polite enough to file. After Ember Hall, after orange fire living behind eyelids longer than any overlay should allow, after a restricted file she had watched without tagging and a dream that had closed green while the wrong feeling stayed open—Mara drove toward the edge because her body had learned to treat unannotated rooms as evidence even when the forms called them tangents.

The city thinned the way cities thinned when polish stopped pretending to reach every hill.

Service corridors gave way to older pavement—cracked, repaired by hands instead of maintenance contracts. Transit pulse faded under her boots. Laundry appeared on lines between patched roofs. A dog barked once and was not muted. Her overlay sat at minimal by habit, then she killed it entirely at a fork where a hand-painted sign pointed down a slope: **DEVICES REST · PRESENCE DEFAULT.** Not Cass's sign. Same grammar. Different stone.

The walk down took longer than the map suggested because maps measured distance and bodies measured **permission**—whether the city still considered this path part of its continuity or a sidebar error it preferred not to index. Mara counted neither. She counted footsteps on gravel that crunched without haptic correction. Wind moved through bare trees the overlay usually rendered as decorative green. Somewhere below, voices—not feed voices, not optimized warmth—just people arriving because Thursday had become a habit their calendars couldn't fully explain.

The building appeared before she decided whether to call it beautiful.

An old church—or what had been one before the tier brochures forgot how to photograph gray. Stone dark with rain memory. Windows patched with real glass instead of responsive film. The steeple still stood, not restored, not spectacle: **almost abandoned** in the way places were almost abandoned when civilization had routed worship through feeds and then discovered some bodies still wanted walls that did not update. Tonight it was **strangely alive**. Light through windows—not overlay light. Candle light and late afternoon sun holding out against winter without asking permission.

Mara paused at the path.

No sync hum here. No soft suggestion she was late for something the city had decided mattered more. Just wind, distant traffic stripped of bloom, and voices inside the building—quiet, overlapping, human in the way voices were human when nobody was scoring them for warmth.

Her tablet stayed dead in her bag. Her badge relay clicked off with the same small surrender she remembered from the tier lip above Ember Hall. The silence wasn't empty. It was **unoptimized**.

She walked in.

Footsteps **real** on stone—no acoustic correction, no soft reverb the tier used to make institutions feel eternal. The nave opened wide enough to hold doubt: ceiling height that had once been theology and was now just **air** nobody charged for. Daylight through patched glass fell in uneven rectangles on the floor, honest about clouds. Candles burned in jars along the side aisles for people who trusted flame more than fixtures—a choice, not a doctrine lecture. Mara passed a bench where someone's coat had been folded with the care of a body that expected to return. Nobody asked her name. Nobody offered a waiver overlay.

* * *

The nave surprised her by not performing ruin.

Stone held cold the honest way stone always did—radiating age without monetizing it. Rows of benches, some missing, replaced by folding chairs that squeaked when people shifted. No megachurch screens. No billions banner. No adaptive prompt suggesting how to participate. At the front, a simple table with candles in jars and a chipped bowl for coats. On the wall beside the door, three curved strokes in faded paint—flame grammar, **ORG** in the old register, though nobody said the word aloud.

People gathered the way people gathered when **physical** was the point.

Mixed ages. Mixed wrists—Bronze beside Silver beside bare skin beside someone who still wore a believer's cord at the belt without embarrassment. Not a Rawist enclave. Not a tier chapel. A **meal** in progress in the hall beyond the nave, smell arriving before sight: bread, soup, the sharp sweetness of onions cooked slow enough to count as time.

Nobody scanned her.

A woman near the coat bowl looked up—sixties, practical hair, hands that had done dishes for more years than Mara had done cases. "You're welcome to sit," she said. Not *verified*. Not *cleared*. **Welcome.**

"I'm—" Mara stopped before bureau could become a personality. "I'm looking."

"Most people are, the first time." The woman's smile was small and not selling anything. "Food's through there. Leave devices at the bowl if you want. Keep them if you need. Nobody audits."

Mara left the tablet in her bag. Kept the notebook—paper still felt like truth in rooms that didn't trust clouds. She moved toward the hall.

The meal was **messy** in the way Ember Hall had been messy and the tier had forgotten how to allow: pots on a sideboard, ladles dripping, a child carrying spoons with the solemn focus children gave real tasks. Real food. Real clatter. Laughter that arrived unmixed—no laugh track, no warmth slider, just the abrupt honest sound of someone finding something funny because it was funny and not because the room needed morale.

Mara took a bowl because her body asked before her job did.

Soup first—thick, oversalted, perfect. Bread torn, not sliced for presentation. She sat at the end of a long table where conversation happened in pockets instead of threads. An older man in a worn sweater talked to a teenager with offline boots about fixing a window latch; the teenager listened the way teenagers listened when respect was offered without a score. Two rows over, a woman with a believer's shawl bowed her head while the man beside her—bare wrist, Rawist calm—waited without mockery. Not agreement. **Coexistence.** Scene grammar older than the product.

"You're new," someone said.

Mara looked up. A man her age, flour on his sleeves, the tired competence of people who fed rooms instead of audiences. "First visit?"

"Yes."

"We do this Thursdays." He said it like weather, not recruitment. "Started with six. Now the hall fills. Nobody optimized it. People just kept coming back after the stutter."

Another woman—believer cord, flour on her hands from the bread table—added without looking up from a tray: "Some of us prayed here when the building was empty. Some of us didn't pray anywhere. Now we wash dishes together. That's the whole politics."

The teenager with offline boots snorted softly, not cruel. "Politics is when someone tries to sell it."

"Exactly." The woman smiled. "So we don't sell it."

The stutter. Mara did not ask them to perform trauma for her file. She nodded instead, and the flour man moved on with a tray of cups—ceramic, mismatched, **real**.

She watched.

That was her job in rooms like this, even when Chen called it mapping: watch for what the infrastructure didn't measure. Here, what didn't measure was the point. No Veil required. No Second dock in sight. No verify prompt at the door. **Presence** default—the phrase from the sign outside made sense now not as ideology but as logistics: you arrived with your body and the room did not ask for a translation layer.

A child dropped a spoon. An adult picked it up without making a lesson. An elderly couple shared one bench corner, shoulders touching, talking quietly about a neighbor's heat pump—not philosophy, **maintenance**, the ordinary intimacy of people who still knew each other's problems without a kindness index.

Mara felt something loosen in her chest she had not authorized.

Not conversion. Not belief. **Surprise**—sharp and embarrassed: she had expected performance saints or manifesto cosplay. Instead she got awkward realness—a joke that landed wrong and was forgiven without a moderation flag, a pause in serving while someone found the ladle, a believer saying grace while a Rawist waited with patient boredom and nobody treated either as victory.

They seemed **happier**.

She did not say the word. She did not trust it yet. But she saw it in faces unfiltered by bloom—in the absence of the small frantic check people did when tiers were watching—in shoulders that had dropped half an inch since the stutter taught the city how much it had been charging for relief.

Not utopia. Not proof. A room that had decided contact was not the same as connection and had built a **meal** around the difference while the polished city still called both success—the same grammar Ember Hall had taught her by fire, different scale, stone instead of orange, **shared dishes** instead of a ring, but the same refusal to let infrastructure measure whether you had shown up.

The flour man returned with tea in a chipped mug. Steam rose without a comfort slider. "Still looking?"

"Still looking," Mara said.

"Good." He nodded toward the nave where someone was stacking chairs for next week without a ticket system. "That's allowed here. So is leaving when you're full."

She almost asked what they called themselves—what name the cloud would use if the cloud ever cared. She swallowed the question. Names turned rooms into movements and movements into targets. The meal was enough evidence for one visit.

* * *

She left when the light through the windows went amber—not because anyone asked her to go, because investigators learned when to leave before rooms asked them to stay.

At the door the woman from the coat bowl touched Mara's sleeve without possession. "Come back if you want. No card. No signup. We don't do lists the cloud can read."

"No card," Mara repeated, absurdly grateful and absurdly suspicious of her own gratitude.

Outside, the city waited at the hill crest—sync hum audible again before she turned the relay on, overlay bloom waiting like a patient parent with better manners than truth. She stood in the doorway light a moment longer, stone cold at her back, warm air at her ankles, the hall still murmuring behind her with the clatter of dishes being washed in a tub.

They seemed **happier**.

The thought landed locked—not argued, not endorsed, **witnessed**. Mara felt the envy arrive on its heels, honest and ugly in the way envy always was when you had spent your life trusting rooms that measured you: she wanted what she distrusted. The ease. The unbilled warmth. The way nobody had asked her to perform competence or file a tag or close a tab green while the wrong feeling stayed open.

She wanted to sit one more hour where presence was default and nobody called it recalibration.

She did not go back inside.

Shame micro—swallowed, no speech. She was not Rawist. She was not believer. She was not quit. She was bureau, tired, softened in the small way that mattered and angry at the softening because softness without infrastructure felt like a trap even when it tasted like soup.

Mara turned the badge relay on.

The sync hum returned under her thoughts—not loud, never loud, just **present**. Transit green at the tier lip. Weather mild. Messages stacked with the soft urgency of a world that believed continuity was mercy. It looked **too smooth** again after stone and chipped mugs and mismatched chairs.

Orange fire lived behind one eyelid when she blinked. The week's wrong feeling lived behind the other—file closed, body open, questions the bureau would never tag. Here, in the hill dusk, **happier** lived in her body like a question she could not close: not *true*, not *false*, just **felt**—and that was worse, because feelings were what the product had learned to govern while this room had simply fed her and let her leave.

She thought of mentor sessions scheduled in warm offices with good coffee and ethical language that always arrived on time. Lena's voice, smooth recovery after hard cases—**institutional** warmth, the kind that measured care and billed it as mentorship. Inverse, she understood now, without naming it: one warmth asked nothing back but presence; the other warmth asked you to stay functional.

She walked toward CIB and the mentor session on tomorrow's calendar—Dr. Lena Orra, routine debrief, trust default since Month One—and did not ping Chen.

Happier?

Mara did not know. She knew she envied people who looked like they did, and she hated that she envied them, and she said nothing to anyone because saying it would have made it fileable, and some hungers still deserved to stay off the record.

The city resumed around her—polite, optimized, still useful, no longer sufficient.

She kept walking.
