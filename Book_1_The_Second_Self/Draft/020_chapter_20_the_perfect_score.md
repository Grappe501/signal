# Chapter 20 — The Perfect Score

The alarm was polite.

That was the first wrong thing—Month Two, week five, Spine interchange choking on a coolant loop failure, crowd density spiking on three platforms, and the public address tone still choosing reassurance over urgency. *Minor service interruption. HVI Focus volunteers please proceed to marked stations. Estimated recovery within tolerance.*

Mara stood at the ops rail with her bureau badge clipped high and her tablet showing feeds she didn't fully trust. CIB had sent her as liaison because embodiment casework now included civic stress when bodies and routing disagreed, which meant the bureau had widened her reach without widening her explanations. Green terminal glow lived in her memory two floors up. Down here the light was honest fluorescent and the air tasted like recycled panic.

Transit security had cordoned the upper lip with soft barriers that looked temporary and permanent at once. A tier screen on the far wall still advertised mild weather and open lanes, as if the city could not stop selling continuity while continuity failed. Mara kept her overlay minimal—feeds, band readouts, ops channel—because full bloom would have smoothed the crowd into statistics and she needed bodies today, not summaries.

On every wrist a band color appeared before anyone asked who could turn a wrench.

Today the expensive part was visible.

HVI Focus had deployed within six minutes.

She watched the bands appear on wrists and collar screens—Platinum first, then Gold, the city's habit of sorting competence before it sorted need. Three high-band citizens in clean transit wraps moved through the crowd with the confidence of people who had never missed a pod because the queue simulation said they wouldn't. Routing icons on the overhead board pulsed green beside their IDs. **ASSIST · PRIORITY · STABILIZE.**

They were useless.

Not cruel. Not stupid. Just the wrong tool for a problem that wanted hands on metal and patience with valves that didn't read tier maps. One volunteer tried to talk a platform screen through a reset sequence that assumed software failure. Another offered crowd-calming language from a Platinum wellness module while a mother shouted that her child couldn't breathe in the stalled crush. The third stood very still with a band flashing **912** and no idea where the manual bypass panel lived because the building had been designed not to need one in stories the city told about itself.

Mara thought of victim profiles that finished loading to numbers the easy stories couldn't absorb. Platinum guest. Platinum score. Wrong room for a wrong story. The rhyme wasn't proof. It was pressure on a habit she'd built her career inside.

The crowd panicked the way crowds panicked when optimization failed without admitting failure—compression, not riot. Tier bands visible on every wrist, invisible class suddenly legible: who got routed to the upper platform lip, who got held in the warm zone, who got told to wait four minutes and miss the slot that would not come again until afternoon. Scores didn't help. They **sorted**. They did not fix.

A routing overlay on Mara's tablet suggested alternate egress for **712+** bands while the lower platform still held bodies the map treated as delay rather than danger. Green icons. Wrong compassion. She watched a Gold-band man wave his wrist at a gate that wanted permission instead of a wrench and felt her trust strain at the hairline.

Somewhere below, a child cried with the flat outrage of heat and no air. A platform speaker offered *calm transit mode* and made half the crowd angrier because calm was also a product the city sold by the minute. Mara did not move toward the Focus volunteers. Moving would have looked like endorsement. She stayed at the rail and counted what the system counted wrong.

* * *

The fix came from the service corridor.

Low band on the wrist—**412 Bronze**, if Mara read the flicker correctly through sweat and bad light. A woman in maintenance coveralls, hair tied back with a strap that wasn't branded, dropped to her knees beside a panel the high-score volunteers had walked past twice because the UI didn't flag it. No Focus badge. No routing green. Just a body that knew where the city hid its honest parts.

She opened the panel with a key that wasn't digital. Mara heard the manual clunk through the noise—real metal, real resistance—and the crowd noise changed pitch as air began to move again.

The woman worked without performance. Checked a gauge with her thumb. Turned a valve half a degree, listened, turned it again. When a Focus volunteer leaned in to ask whether she needed **authorization**, she didn't look up.

"I need you to move so people can breathe," she said.

The volunteer moved.

Within two minutes the crush eased. Not solved—contained, the way Month Two problems were always contained if you didn't look at the substrate. Recovery chime followed, polite and satisfied. The overhead board refreshed: **INCIDENT CONTAINED · HVI FOCUS EFFECTIVE · NO FATALITIES.**

Mara watched the high-score volunteers accept the banner anyway because banners were how people who needed stories survived afternoons. One of them clapped the maintenance worker on the shoulder as if proximity to competence could be shared by contact. The worker wiped her hands on her coveralls and went back to invisible before anyone could ask her name for a feed.

Mara noted the band number anyway. Not for the file. For the notebook.

Ops wanted her statement before the feeds finished editing the afternoon into a success story. She gave them facts without adjectives—time of manual bypass, approximate recovery window, no bureau jurisdiction over maintenance keys—and watched a public recap tile assemble in the corner of her screen: smiling volunteers, green banner, the Spine **open for tomorrow**. The maintenance worker's face was not in it. Of course it wasn't. Invisible class was a feature until it saved you.

Chen's voice arrived in her earpiece before Mara reached the debrief alcove.

"Walk me through it," Deputy Director Marisa Chen said, dry as always, already reading the same fields from a desk that never smelled like a platform.

Mara looked at the empty space where the maintenance woman had been—no interview queue, no hero overlay, back to invisible the way low bands learned to be invisible when the story closed without them.

"HVI Focus deployed," Mara said. "Routing green. Manual bypass restored circulation. Bronze-band maintenance worker opened the panel. Focus volunteers didn't locate the valve."

Silence on the line. Chen choosing precision over comfort, the way she always did when Month One had offered *hardware stall* and *isolated incident* for a homicide that didn't fit its nouns.

"Stress event," Chen said finally. "Contained outcome. We'll note routing latency in the malfunction frame. Hardware-adjacent loop failure. Isolated incident."

*Malfunction frame.* The words arrived pre-warmed, the institutional equivalent of *within tolerance*—a shape the file could accept without asking why Platinum bands predicted assistance but not competence. Mara had heard the same appetite for closure before: close fast, keep feeds calm, treat pattern like embarrassment.

"The fix wasn't a malfunction," Mara said. "It was a person who knew where the panel was."

"Then we note both." Chen's tone didn't sharpen. It didn't need to. "Investigator, the public layer needs a story that keeps transit open tomorrow. You have embodiment drift in your queue and a Month Two caseload that doesn't benefit from philosophical detours."

Mara looked at the green banner on her tablet. **HVI FOCUS EFFECTIVE.** The maintenance worker's band had never appeared in the hero field. Chen wasn't wrong about the public layer. Chen was never wrong about the public layer. That was the job.

"I copy," she said.

She did not copy.

The debrief alcove smelled like coffee and policy. Two transit supervisors argued quietly about whether the loop failure should be filed as **hardware drift** or **crowd amplification**—two names for the same afternoon, neither of them *wrong hero*. Mara signed the liaison line and left before anyone could ask her to smile for a continuity clip.

* * *

Back at CIB-4 the air was filtered until dust looked compliant again. Mara sat at a desk that had never been assigned to ambition and opened her paper notebook—the pen in the spine, ink that could not be autosaved—because some details arrived better through the hand.

Her father had kept a ledger on the kitchen table long after the city told him paper was sentiment. *Numbers lie slower,* he used to say. Not wisdom. Habit dressed up to survive.

She wrote: *Platinum routing green. Valve manual. Bronze hands. Banner wrong hero.*

The wrong-case tab in the corner of her screen pulsed once—embodiment drift, merge flags, the file that passed verification and failed the world—then settled back into background weather. Month Two continued in the queue either way.

She tabbed a training overlay she hadn't closed from weeks ago, victim profile shape ghosting up uninvited: **934.** Platinum band. Near the top of the chart the city pretended wasn't a chart. *Colleague* in her handwriting from Month One. *Wrong room for a wrong story.*

Today's incident wasn't homicide. It wasn't even embodiment. It was a coolant loop and a crowd and a score that had sent the wrong people to the front because the front was where the city liked to photograph competence.

Something didn't fit.

Not the case only—the institution. The habit of trusting bands the way she trusted verification suites until kitchens proved otherwise. The habit of letting contained language close rooms before they admitted what had saved them. Cass had called it subscription once, in bad coffee and honest light. Mara had defended the bureau layer anyway. Today the layer had clapped the wrong shoulders and filed the right hands under malfunction.

She thought of private choices made against optimization—difficulty treated as treasure somewhere she couldn't see. She thought of witness statements that matched suite output until they didn't. Different rooms. Same shape: the map green while the world failed quietly enough to file.

Mara's fingers found the paper card in her jacket pocket without looking—address in the service corridor network, handwriting that said **Ember Hall**, Thursday, ask for Cass. She had told herself it was evidence retention. She had told herself the lie in the voice she used for *not relevant*. Thursday was tomorrow. Or had been tomorrow when Cass said it. Time had gotten slippery since Month Two started treating doubt like background weather.

Platinum logic had failed in public today. Not dramatically. Not Month Five dramatically. Just visibly enough that a banner could lie and everyone who needed transit open tomorrow could pretend they hadn't seen how.

She closed the notebook without closing the feeling.

The terminal hummed. The HVI column on a random profile widget still glowed green in the corner of her screen like a promise the world kept making to people it did not know how to save when the valves were real.

Mara did not call Cass. She did not file a philosophical detour. She wrote one more line—*hands before bands*—and checked the time.

Thursday was **tonight**. Ember Hall waited in the service corridor network like a question the bureau couldn't score.

The card stayed in her pocket.

She held the crack quietly and did not look up when the perfect city resumed—but her feet were already planning the route.
