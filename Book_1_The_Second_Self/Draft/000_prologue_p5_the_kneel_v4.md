"EVA window closing," the procedure voice said.

Adrian didn't move. Let the warning repeat. Let **4.8 billion** wait with him. Fair. They'd waited on his biometrics long enough.

Then he turned—not toward Farshore, toward the cameras, toward the invisible thread of every open channel routed through his nervous system without being with him in the dust—and he knelt.

Regolith clung to the white knee joints. Messy. Human. The sort of detail a drone would have edited out for a cleaner frame. Fabric creased. Servos whispered at the joint. Off-channel, protocol screamed—visor integrity, contamination risk, liability language that had never once stopped him when the moment required a **face** instead of a helmet. Rubbish. The world didn't need another helmet. It had worshipped helmets long enough.

He removed the visor with gloved hands.

Slower than necessary. Showmanship and sincerity braided—the way he always performed best when he wasn't sure which was which. Latches released. Seal broke with a soft pop the microphone carried with insulting clarity. He lifted the visor away.

Cold hit his cheeks through a face that wasn't in the room where his biological jaw clenched against a tremor no one on Mars could see.

Livestream tightened on skin. Chapped lips. Stubble on the Second's jaw—same stubble the cameras would have hated on Earth. Old acne line at the temple he'd never fixed because perfection was for products, not people. Breath fogging briefly in thin air. Blink. Ordinary. Therefore impossible.

Eyes. Brown. Tired. Slightly too wide. Old animal proof. Not a drone. Not an AI. Not a clever mirror. Him—or close enough the distinction would become theology later and nightmare later still.

*Please let this be enough,* he thought. Engineer's prayer. Not a messiah's.

Words he wanted were too small. Physics words. Link words. Gratitude and terror braided so tight he couldn't find the seam.

Cold on cheeks that were here and not here. Warmth on a body locked in a rig in Nevada while he left without moving. Not a message that finished a long commute—two correlated outcomes updating at once: suit thermal map, skin map, lattice beneath both that still lacked a polite word in English. A physicist would call it shared state. He called it feeling, because feeling was what made people pause their arguing and listen.

For a heartbeat neither location felt like home—just two rooms in the same wrong miracle.
