The witness intake arrived on a channel Mara had not used in years.

Not homicide. Not embodiment telemetry. **Memory integrity**—the bureau's polite name for when a human certainty arrived before the database agreed to carry it. **8853-M** sat below **SIG-MERGE-01** on her board like a smaller wrongness asking for a chair. Month Two. Week two. Part Two resources had widened the room without widening her sleep.

She did not think about the word **Impossible.** in the basement. She thought about coffee and the scuffed mug and the notebook that still held two cities' handwriting from a month that had ended on data.

The interview suite on CIB-4 was gray on purpose—no Veil bloom, no overlay hooks, verification terminals bolted to the table like furniture that expected argument. Mara set her tablet to record and opened the witness file.

**Tessa Rowe.** Twenty-nine. Tier-three clinic records clerk. Flagged by her employer's wellness layer after she reported a patient interaction the clinic's own intake system could not find. No criminal history. No Continuity violations. No reason to lie except the reason that mattered: she believed what she remembered.

Tessa entered with the stiff posture of someone who had been told her mind was a malfunction waiting for classification. Brown skin, clinic badge on a lanyard, hands wrapped around a paper cup of water she did not drink. Her eyes were clear. That was the first wrong thing.

"Investigator Voss," Mara said. "CIB Cognitive Anomalies. You asked for a bureau review."

"I asked for someone to stop telling me I'm inventing a Tuesday." Tessa's voice was level—not performative calm, the kind that came from repeating yourself to people who smiled professionally while closing tickets. "They said you'd be the one who listens when verification fails."

"I listen when the file doesn't match the witness," Mara said. "Start where it feels most real."

* * *

Tessa started with gloves.

"Nitrile," she said. "The cheap kind that tear if you flex wrong. **11:47 PM**, I was still on shift at **Mercy Tier Clinic 6**—late intake, guest paperwork for overnight Continuity monitoring. The guest signed **Alina Ward.** I remember the name because she laughed when I mispronounced it and said everyone did until they stopped apologizing."

Mara's pen stopped.

She did not look at the case tabs on her tablet. She did not say *I stood over that woman's body in a Platinum suite three weeks ago.* She said, "Describe the room."

"Exam bay three. White tile, one flickering panel in the corner they keep promising to replace. Guest chair—not medical, guest. She sat with her coat still on. She said she couldn't sleep because the building kept **correcting** around her." Tessa's fingers tightened on the cup. "She asked if records ever lied when people were still alive. I thought it was stress. I typed everything into the intake overlay. I watched her sign. I went home at **1:06 AM** and made tea I didn't finish."

Mara wrote *11:47 · bay three · coat on · correcting* and kept her face in the neutral register the job had trained into muscle.

"Thank you," she said. "We're going to run verification. That means memory coherence, location crosswalk, biometric tie-in. It isn't an accusation."

"It feels like one," Tessa said.

"I know."

Mara turned the tablet so Tessa could see the interface—clean, bureau gray, the Continuity Verification Suite built for a world where truth was supposed to be engineering.

**Phase one:** episodic recall alignment. Tessa closed her eyes. The room's speakers played nothing; the system read sync markers from her implant band and compared narrative cadence to stored physiological tells. Green bars climbed. **Coherence: 94%.**

**Phase two:** spatial reconstruction. Tessa walked the clerk through bay three with her hands—door left, chair angle, panel flicker at the northeast corner. The UI rendered a ghost layout and overlaid clinic blueprints. Match tolerance **within 2%**.

**Phase three:** identity tie-in. Guest name spoken aloud. Signature muscle memory from the overlay pad. **Alina Ward** resolved against public registry—Platinum band, compliance counsel, **deceased** flag active on a guest homicide Mara had not opened on this screen.

The verification suite did not flinch.

**OVERALL: PASS** — *Witness recall consistent with lived experience markers.*

Mara felt the result land in her chest the way results always landed when they confirmed what she wanted to believe about the world: with relief first, questions later.

Tessa opened her eyes. "So you believe me."

"The verification pass is step one," Mara said. "Step two is whether the world agrees."

* * *

The world did not agree.

Mara ran crosswalk herself. She no longer trusted a pass banner to close a room.

Clinic intake logs for **Mercy Tier 6** on the flagged night: **empty after 9:30 PM.** Security badge read for Tessa Rowe: **exit 9:41 PM.** Transit tap home: **10:08 PM.** Apartment building entry: **10:19 PM.** Biometric sleep onset estimate: **10:52 PM.**

No bay three guest. No **Alina Ward** signature event. No late intake overlay record—not deleted, not hidden, **never created.**

Building registry: **Exam bay three** existed. Northeast panel **replaced six months prior**—no flicker logged in maintenance.

Mara pulled Hush Spire timelines without naming them aloud. Alina Ward's body in **11-A** at **7:08 AM** dispatch. Guest asleep by **4:12 AM** per building systems. A clinic intake at **11:47 PM** the same night was not a scheduling conflict. It was an **impossibility** of a different species than three lines on a board—and somehow worse, because it wore a human face and passed every test designed to catch lies.

Tessa watched the color drain from the UI reflected in Mara's glasses.

"You see it," Tessa said. "You see I'm not—"

"I see the verification passed," Mara said. "I see the records don't match. Sit down. Please."

Tessa sat. The paper cup cracked slightly in her grip.

Mara tried procedure first because procedure was what she had. "Walk me through after you went home."

"Tea. Shower. I checked my messages—clinic schedule for the next day. I slept. I woke up thinking about her laugh. I came to work. The wellness layer flagged me because I asked my supervisor why the intake wasn't in the system and she said there had been no intake." Tessa's voice cracked on the last word, then steadied. "She showed me the logs. I showed her my memory. She sent me home. Then she sent me here."

"Did anyone suggest medical review?"

"They suggested I was tired." A bitter smile. "Everyone is tired. That doesn't make a person vanish from a chair."

Mara leaned forward. Compassion here meant treating a witness as data she couldn't yet parse. "I'm going to ask you again. You're sure of the time?"

**11:47 PM.** Tessa did not hesitate.

"You're sure of the name?"

**Alina Ward.**

"You're sure you typed the intake?"

"I watched the overlay accept it."

Mara had read enough post-incident reports to know what certainty looked like when it was manufactured. This was not that. This was the certainty that broke people because it felt more real than the room they were sitting in now.

The verification terminal chimed once—soft, green, still **PASS**—and Mara heard the thin hum again beneath the tone. She shut it down without comment. She wasn't ready for another noun.

"Tessa," she said quietly. "I believe you remember what you remember."

"Then why—"

"Because the records say otherwise. Because I can't explain it yet. Because my job is to hold both until one breaks." Mara kept her voice level. "That isn't the same as calling you wrong."

Tessa stared at her with the look of someone who had been holding two worlds in one skull for days and had been told, by every system she trusted, that the heavier world must be discarded.

"Why does everyone keep telling me I'm wrong?" she said.

The question was not dramatic. It was exhausted. It landed in the gray room with the weight of a thing that would outlive the interview.

Mara did not have an answer that would fit in a form field.

* * *

She filed what she could.

**8853-M** · witness **Tessa Rowe** · episodic recall **verification PASS** · facility/location/timeline **no supporting record** · guest identity references **8841-A victim (deceased)** — **unresolved conflict**.

She did not write *memory of a thing that never happened.* The file rejected poetry. She wrote: *Witness account inconsistent with institutional record. Cause unknown.*

In the margin of her notebook—not the official record, the one her father would have used for numbers that didn't add up—she wrote: *Passed. Failed. Same woman.*

Chen pinged her ear as she left the suite.

"Status on the memory flag?"

"Verification passes," Mara said. "Everything else doesn't."

"Sidebar language?"

Mara almost laughed. "They'll call it stress. Or clerical error. Or a wellness false positive."

"And your read?"

Mara walked the corridor toward the bank of elevators, **SIG-MERGE-01** still pulsing quietly on her tablet in the corner like a heartbeat the city refused to stethoscope. Tessa Rowe's question followed her without needing to be repeated.

"I don't have a read I can file," she said.

Chen was quiet a beat. "Keep it off feeds. Expand witness support. Do not tell her she's wrong on record."

"Understood."

"And Mara—" Chen's voice dropped half a register. "Part Two isn't only telemetry now. It's also this."

The elevator chimed. Mara stepped in with a case that passed verification and failed reality, and a witness who remembered a dead woman in a room the world insisted had never held her.

She did not feel afraid.

She felt the same lean as in the basement, in front of three lines: fascinated, unsettled, aware that proof could arrive as comfort and still clarify nothing.

The doors closed.

Month Two continued without asking whether continue was enough.
