The embodiment case had started as telemetry.

**8921-E** — guest sync drift in a mid-tier hotel, body present, Second reporting latency spikes that didn't match network logs. Routine Part Two work: compare bands, isolate interference, file a correction or a fraud flag and move on. The guest's band read **712 Silver**—respectable, not Platinum noise. His story was boring in the way innocent stories often were: business trip, link fatigue, wanted the bureau to confirm he wasn't cheating his employer's travel tier.

Mara had requested a cross-reference on prior Continuity counseling because drift often wore old grief as camouflage. The system returned three tangents and one name she hadn't typed.

**Adrian Vale.**

Not the public myth tier. Not the training montage she'd watched in Month One while **8841-A** pulsed in the corner of her screen. A restricted partition labeled **Early Research · Post-Farshore** — access flagged **sidebar approved** in Chen's voice from two days prior, the bureaucratic equivalent of *don't ask again on feeds.*

Mara stared at the approval line longer than it deserved. She had not finished typing the cross-reference before **sidebar approved** appeared—access moving faster than request, the way doors opened when something upstream wanted you inside without asking why.

Part Two had widened her reach without widening her explanations. Month Two. Week three. The coffee in her mug was cold. **8853-M** still sat below **SIG-MERGE-01** on her board like a wrongness that passed tests and failed the world. She did not open that file. She opened Vale's.

The secure archive terminal on CIB-4 was the same green glow as the verification suite two floors up—bureau honesty, no Veil bloom, fans humming the way servers hummed when they expected to outlive the humans reading them. Mara logged in with investigator clearance and told herself this was spillover, not obsession. Embodiment bled into identity bled into the city's founding ghost. The chain was professional enough to file.

Somewhere in the building a printer recovered from a jam with the soft apology of machines that still believed errors were temporary. Normal sounds. Normal day. The ordinary was always misleading when you knew where to click next.

The first subdirectory was **DMT Sessions · Internal.**

Mara's mouth tightened. She had read the public version of Vale's later years—the interviews, the tired charisma, the sentences that unsettled without explaining. DMT logs were not hero footage. They were lab notes with the polish removed.

She opened the earliest file.

Transcript fragments, not narrative. Timestamps. Dosage columns redacted to black bars. Subject **A.V.** — no surprise — voice tagged **degraded audio · recoverable 62%**. Clinical language throughout: *baseline stable · dissociation reported · no acute distress · session terminated per protocol.*

Mara read three entries without finding a thesis. Session 02: *subject reports receiver state prior to motor mapping*. Session 04: *visual cortex lag noted · subject insists perception leads registration · repeat*. Session 05: *no broadcast · internal only · do not attach to Farshore PR.*

No spectacle. No neon spirituality. The unsettling part was how ordinary the form was—the same template the bureau used for witness wellness reviews, as if the mind could be metered the way bandwidth could. One margin note, OCR-fuzzy: *if localization is post-hoc, embodiment cases are measuring echo*. Mara wrote *8921-E* in her notebook and drew a line through it. Speculation wasn't evidence. Speculation was how you ended up in sidebar language.

**8921-E** waited in a side tab. She didn't switch.

The second file cross-referenced **Mars · Field Notes · Sync Window 14**. Not the walk. Not *I can feel the cold.* A technician's memo about lag between proprioceptive feedback and visual overlay during early regolith tests—half a paragraph, dated three years before the public miracle, tone irritated in the way engineers got when physics refused to match the press release.

Another note, Vale's handwriting in scan: *Second reports regolith before feet confirm · check calibration or admit the map is wrong*. No myth in it. Only a man trying to make latency honest in a project that needed latency to disappear for shareholders.

Mara thought of the hero montage without reopening it: *The body is a node.* Vale on screen, haunted not broken, naming frontiers the world preferred to draw on maps. She scrolled past mission graphics and quote databases—the *greatest frontier* sentence lived elsewhere, filed under context, not proof. What she needed here was smaller—evidence that the hero had been a researcher first, asking questions the myth had later buried under applause.

The third subdirectory had no executive summary. That was the point.

**Signal · Preliminary · Restricted**

Field notes only. Abbreviations she didn't fully recognize. Waveform captures without labels, peaks annotated in handwriting the OCR couldn't clean: *not noise · repeat at 7.2 · subject reports "hum" · do not broadcast*. Dates clustered in the years after Farshore, before the public turned Vale into a verb for courage.

One file was a scan of notebook paper—equations half erased, prose intruding at the margins: *Receiver before receiver location. If consciousness is signal, brain is antenna, not author.* Another was an internal memo rejecting broadcast: *Speculative · reputational risk · table until embodiment stabilizes*. The table had never been cleared. It had been renamed **Signal** and buried under restricted access where only investigators with sidebar luck could stumble into it.

Mara opened a waveform with no legend. The peaks repeated at intervals that made her skin tighten—not because she understood them, because her body recognized pattern the way it recognized the verification chime before crosswalk failed. She did not attach meaning. Attaching meaning was how witnesses got told they were wrong.

Her finger hovered over **Session 07 · Audio Primary**. The cursor blinked. The fan hum matched something at the edge of her hearing—not the verification chime, not STATIC, just server air and the building pretending to be neutral.

She clicked play.

* * *

The headphones were bureau issue—flat, honest, no mood stabilization. Vale's voice arrived thin, as if recorded through equipment that had already decided wonder was a liability.

"...baseline holding. Visual field stable. I'm noting again that the receiver state feels prior to the—"

Static. A breath.

"—prior to the localization. The brain is mapping afterward. I keep trying to say that without sounding like I've lost—"

Pause. Paper, maybe. A chair. On the waveform, the spike looked almost polite.

"—What if consciousness is not produced by the brain?"

The question was not delivered as revelation. It was dropped mid-thought, clinical, tired—the way a witness asked why the room kept calling her memory a malfunction, except this voice belonged to a dead man the world still applauded on anniversaries.

The recording continued three seconds. Vale said *log that as hypothesis only* and the file degraded into noise.

Mara listened to the silence after stop longer than she meant to. The suite did not help. No overlay smoothed the gap. No assistance layer offered to reframe hypothesis as wellness risk. The green terminal glow made everything look like evidence whether it was or not.

Her finger found stop a beat late.

The terminal did not offer a PASS banner. There was nothing to verify—only a sentence that refused to behave like archival noise once you'd heard it.

She played the line again—not for proof, for tone. Still clinical. Still tired. Still unanswered by the three seconds that followed.

She tagged the clip **misfiled tangent · embodiment cross-ref** and typed a note in the margin she would never show a supervisor aloud: *Not relevant to 8921-E. Stress artifact. Post-mission cognitive load. Hypothesis only · closed.*

She said it once to the empty suite, voice level, performing competence for the room.

"Not relevant."

The word sounded reasonable. That was the problem. Reasonable was how institutions kept moving—file the tangent, keep the primary, don't let a dead hero's bad afternoon become a religion.

* * *

She closed the Vale partition the way she closed theater windows—context filed, myth returned to its case, professional distance restored on the surface.

The wrong feeling started before she stood— not nausea, not dread. **Recognition** without coordinates. The body remembering a lean it had learned in a basement in front of three lines, applied now to a waveform and a dead man's tired voice.

**8921-E** waited where she'd left it, patient and small. She resolved it in twenty minutes: band misconfiguration, overlay grief loop, guest compliant. Explanation attached. Correction routed. The kind of closure that made dashboards green and left the deeper wrongness untouched. Mara wrote the summary in bureau plain: *Latency spike · overlay grief loop · no fraud · guest compliant.* File complete. Next.

The queue scrolled forward with the misleading calm of work that still looked like the world made sense if you stayed inside the right folder. **8917-E**. **8933-E**. **8919-E**. Embodiment drift, embodiment drift, fraud flag dismissed—units she could argue with, forms that accepted her signature.

**8853-M** pulsed once in the corner. Unresolved. Verification pass, records fail—she did not open it. Some files were doors you kept closed until the hinge broke on its own schedule.

**SIG-MERGE-01** held its three lines without commentary.

Chen pinged optional—she let it go to message. *Archive access logged. Sidebar copy attached. Keep tangent off feeds.* No lecture. No Vale eulogy. Procedure at a distance.

Mara walked back to her desk through a corridor that smelled like policy and coffee. Colleagues passed with tablets and the neutral hurry of people whose wrong feelings had not yet learned their names. She nodded. They nodded. The institution continued—competent, pretty, insufficient.

She was not afraid. She was fascinated—the basement lean toward a sentence that arrived before evidence agreed to carry it.

*What if consciousness is not produced by the brain?*

The sentence did not accept *not relevant*. Mara filed the clip closed anyway. Drawers, not locks.

She did not write the line in the notebook. The notebook had learned which questions became cases and which became hauntings.

The next slot on her queue blinked: **Rawist contact · intel routine · off-grid edge · accept assignment?**

No name yet. Just a category—alternative community, protest adjacency, the kind of meeting Mara expected to endure before returning to telemetry that at least lied in units she could argue with.

She accepted.

The file icon for Vale's Signal research showed **closed** in green. The question did not close. It stayed in the room the way certain sentences did once you'd heard them in a voice that wasn't performing—occupying space without needing her permission.

Mara opened **8921-E**'s completion packet and signed her name where the system asked for certainty.

Outside the glass, New Manhattan kept its lanes open. The city performed continuity the way it always had—lanes, scores, polite sidebars drafting recoverable stories. Mara had no obligation to connect every wrongness on her board. She had a queue.

In the corridor two analysts laughed at something mild on a shared feed. Mara did not join. She went back to work and let the locked line sit unanswered in the green glow of a suite that had already decided what counted as finished thinking.

The fan hum faded when she left the archive terminal behind. She shut down the undertone without naming it. She wasn't ready for another noun.

The Rawist contact file loaded on her desk—no name yet, just a corridor address and a category the bureau called *friction*. Mara opened it before the suite could talk her out of curiosity.
