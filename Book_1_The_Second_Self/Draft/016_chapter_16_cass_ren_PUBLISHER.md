# Chapter 16

The assignment arrived with the politeness of work nobody important would fight over.

**Rawist contact · intel routine · off-grid edge.** Chen's note added one line: *Treat as community mapping, not infiltration. Sidebar if they ask about embodiment cases.* Month Two. Week three bleeding into four. Mara had accepted the slot in the archive terminal glow without imagining it would require a different kind of light.

She arrived early to the service corridor café because early was how investigators learned rooms before people did. The place sat half-outside the city's polish—past the tier where overlays defaulted to beautiful, where the menu was paper and the espresso machine sounded like it remembered friction. A flickering **OFFLINE** sign above the door apologized in three languages and meant it. Good light through dirty windows. Bad coffee that smelled honest.

Mara chose a table with sight lines to both exits and the alley mouth, habit not paranoia. She ordered nothing yet. Her tablet held a wrong case tab in the corner like background weather she wasn't paid to stare at today—embodiment drift, merge flags, the kind of file that passed verification and still felt untrue when she closed her eyes. The archive partition from yesterday was closed. The question from it was not. She filed that under *later* the way she filed most things that didn't fit forms.

She expected a performance—manifesto hair, debate club certainty, someone who would call her *bureau* like a curse. Rawists lived in sidebar language already: fringe, aesthetic refusal, the kind of movement that became a costume if you weren't careful. Mara had read the protest intel packets: flagged gatherings, offline pamphlets, aesthetic codes that looked like rebellion and sometimes were. She knew the type on paper.

The corridor outside smelled like hot metal and old rain—the city's honest underside, the place maintenance workers called home between shifts. Inside, the café had the stubborn clutter of a room that refused to be photographed for tier brochures. Chalkboard menu. Napkins that weren't compostable brand-story. A radio with a real dial that hissed between stations when nobody touched it.

The woman who walked in was not the type.

Thirty-something, sharp without trying, coat practical, hands empty. She paused inside the door the way people paused when they read a room before they read faces—not tactical, just practiced. Her eyes landed on Mara and a smile arrived that was funny before it was friendly.

"Investigator Voss," she said. "I'm Cass Ren. You're early. I like that."

"You weren't late."

"I was on time. There's a difference." Cass pulled out the chair across from Mara without asking, sat like the table belonged to both of them, and nodded at the flickering sign. "Coffee here's terrible. That's how you know it's real."

Mara did not smile. "You're the Rawist contact."

"I'm a person who agreed to talk to the bureau because my community is tired of being summarized by people who've never stood in an unfiltered rain." Cass's tone stayed level—calm, not soft—the voice of someone who had made peace with being disbelieved and hadn't let it make her cruel. "Your packet probably called me an organizer. Prophet optional. That's fine. I won't perform for the file either way."

Mara studied her. No performance extremist. No costume. Just a woman who looked like she slept enough and still carried anger like a tool she knew how to use. The kind of person who would be dangerous in a meeting if danger meant clarity instead of volume.

Two tables over, an older man read a paper book without augmentation—real spine, real pages, the luxury of slowness. A teenager at the counter argued quietly with a friend about whether rain on the window counted as *content*. Nobody looked like they were preparing a manifesto. They looked like people who had chosen a room the brochure would never photograph.

"Why meet here?" Mara asked.

"Because half my people won't enter a tier with default Veil bloom. Because you needed to see a room the overlay doesn't own before you decided what we are." Cass glanced at the window. "Also because the coffee is bad. I trust people who drink bad coffee on purpose more than people who pretend it's fine."

* * *

They talked the way people talked when both sides knew the first ten minutes were audition.

Mara opened with procedure—community mapping, protest adjacency, no warrant today, no obligation to share names. Cass listened without interrupting, which was its own kind of confidence. When Mara finished, Cass said, "Good. Now I'll tell you what you actually came for."

"I came for intel."

"You came because your queue handed you a margin case and you're curious whether we're dangerous or ridiculous." Cass leaned back. "We're neither, mostly. We're people who got tired of renting reality by the month."

Mara felt her shoulders tighten—the defensive posture she wore when identity dressed as professionalism. "The Veil isn't a rental agreement."

"No?" Cass's eyebrows lifted. "Who maintains it? Who bills for it? Who tells you when you're allowed to see a crack in the glass?"

"The city. Continuity infrastructure. Elected tiers, not—"

"Subscription tiers." Cass said it lightly, like correcting a mislabeled file. "You defend the system the way my uncle defended cable packages in the before-times—because switching felt like losing, even when the feed was garbage."

"Continuity infrastructure saves lives."

"So does clean water. So does anesthesia. So does a friend who tells you the truth when the room is pretty." Cass leaned forward. "I'm not anti-comfort. I'm anti-confusing comfort with **contact**."

Mara had walked the Veiled City on Chen's day off. She remembered enjoying the ride without forgetting the overlay existed—the frost on treated glass, the meadow horizon where another tower's flank lived, the way beauty arrived on schedule and made you forget to ask who wrote the schedule. She did not recite that memory aloud. "I've seen people happy in optimized districts."

"Happy is not the same as informed."

"I didn't say it was."

"You said it like a rebuttal." Cass's mouth twitched—not quite a smile. "That's bureau reflex. Comfort enters the record as evidence of system health."

"I believe you. Happiness is real. So is the invoice." Cass tapped the paper menu. "This café doesn't polish its steam. Half the customers hate that. The other half come here so their mouths remember something the overlay can't autocorrect."

"That's aesthetic preference. Not epistemology."

"It's both." Cass's voice dropped half a register—not angry, **protective**, the way you spoke when you were defending people who couldn't afford to be called irrational on feeds. "You investigate embodiment drift. You trust verification suites. You file tangents when dead heroes ask questions your institution can't answer. I'm not saying you're stupid. I'm saying you're **loyal to a product** that calls itself truth."

Mara wanted to say *that's not fair*. Fair was a word the bureau used when it needed witnesses to calm down. She thought of green terminal glow, witness statements that matched suite output until they didn't, the quiet professional shame of being right on paper and wrong in a kitchen. She said, "Verification prevents fraud."

"Verification prevents **embarrassment** for institutions that can't afford to be wrong in public." Cass's eyes didn't blink often. "When your suite passes and the world fails, what do you call that?"

Mara did not answer. The question belonged to a file she kept closed.

"You investigate bodies in two places," Cass continued, quieter. "You trust maps that update after the fact. You call it procedure. My people call it **lag**—the delay between what happened and what the city agrees to remember."

"My job is to find truth, not—"

"Your job is to find **reportable** truth." Cass sat back. "Different species."

Mara's cup had arrived at some point—she didn't remember ordering. Her grip was tighter than the coffee deserved.

"I live in the same city you do."

"You live in the bureau layer of it." Cass held her gaze. "You live where PASS banners close rooms. Where closed files mean finished thinking. Where *not relevant* is how smart people stay employed."

The line landed too close to the archive terminal. Mara did not flinch on purpose; she flinched anyway.

"You don't know me."

"I know your job." Cass's hands stayed still on the table. "I used to work upstream of you. Not your floor. Earlier in the pipe. I'll leave it there unless you want a lecture you'll dismiss."

Mara filed the claim without confirming it. "You're suggesting I can't see reality."

"I'm suggesting you see **a curated feed with investigative permissions**." Cass did not smile now. "You don't live in reality. You live in a subscription."

The café went quiet the way rooms did when a sentence was too accurate to argue with immediately. Espresso hissed. Somewhere in the alley a delivery drone lost signal and corrected with a sound like annoyance. No overlay smoothed it.

Mara heard herself start the institutional defense—Continuity saves lives, grief management, the Veil prevents panic—and heard how much of it sounded like a terms-of-service update. She stopped mid-paragraph.

Cass didn't gloat. That was worse. An extremist would have pushed. Cass only refilled the silence the way you refilled a glass for someone you expected to stay at the table longer than they planned.

"You think I'm recruiting you," Cass said. "I'm not. I'm describing the water you're swimming in so you stop calling it air."

Mara's jaw worked once. "My job keeps people from drowning in rumor."

"Your job keeps the pool **labeled**." Cass tapped the table between them—wood scarred by real cups, not composite that healed overnight. "I'm not asking you to quit. I'm asking you to notice the invoice."

Cass waited. Patient in the way of someone who didn't need to win today—only to plant.

"That's insulting," Mara said finally.

"It's accurate." Cass's smile returned, smaller. "You'll think about it on the walk back. You won't admit I was funny. You'll admit I was **sharp**. Different file."

Mara was irritated. Mara was, against her will, **impressed**—the way she'd been impressed by a dead man's tired question and hated that the impression had no case number.

* * *

Cass did not push when the silence ran out.

She slid a card across the table—paper, offline printed, an address in the service corridor network and a name handwritten beneath: **Ember Hall**. No manifesto on the back. Just *Thursday · community · ask for Cass*.

"We gather when the city pretends it's finished updating," she said. "Food. Argument. People who want their children to feel rain without a comfort slider. You're invited. Not as a spy—as a person who's starting to notice the subscription."

Mara looked at the card. "I'm not joining a Rawist cell."

"No one's asking you to join anything today." Cass stood, left cash for both coffees—real bills, inconvenient denomination. "I'm asking you to stop pretending your doubt is private. It shows."

"I have cases."

"You have open tabs you pretend are closed." Cass said it without triumph, almost gently. "I'm not your source. I'm your mirror with worse manners."

Mara should have corrected her. She should have stood, should have taken the card only as evidence. She did stand. She did take the card.

"Not now," she said. "I can't— not now."

"Thursday," Cass said. "Or not. The address works either way."

Cass left through the alley mouth without looking back. No chase. No hard sell. Respect in the decline, which was worse—because it meant Cass believed Mara might come anyway.

Mara stayed long enough to finish the bad coffee. It tasted like burnt beans and honesty. The word **subscription** sat in her mouth like a flavor the overlay couldn't autocorrect. She thought of optimized rain on glass. Closed files. PASS banners. The Veil on a day off, beautiful and heavy in her bag beside tabs she refused to close.

She watched the café after Cass left—the paper book reader turning a page with his thumb, the teenager laughing at something unpolished on the window. Normal life, if normal meant choosing friction on purpose. Mara had spent Month Two learning that friction could mean fraud or truth and the suite couldn't always tell which. Cass had not offered a new suite. She had offered a mirror with worse manners and better lighting.

She was irritated. She was impressed. Both true. Neither admissible.

She walked back toward CIB-4 through a corridor where the city's hum returned by degrees—transit perfect, weather mild, strangers' faces softened at the edges. A vendor's overlay stuttered once and recovered before most pedestrians noticed; Mara noticed because she couldn't stop noticing now, and hated Cass for that too.

The skyline at distance looked solved, a product photograph of continuity. In her pocket the card pressed against her badge like a second credential she hadn't applied for. She could feel the bureau layer reasserting itself block by block—transit schedules smoothing, faces defaulting to pleasant, the hum of infrastructure that sounded like reassurance if you didn't listen for the lag.

On a wall near the tier lip, half-hidden under a maintenance poster, someone had tagged a small flame in chalk—**EMBER** in letters the cleanup drones hadn't prioritized yet. Seed, not event. Fire in the name, not on the page. Mara did not photograph it. Evidence had limits. Some things you kept because throwing them away would mean admitting why you'd picked them up.

Mara did not throw the card away. She told herself that was evidence retention. She told herself the lie in the voice she used for *not relevant*.

**Subscription.**

The word followed her into the elevator, into the green glow of her desk, into the ordinary queue that scrolled embodiment drift and fraud flags like the world still made sense if you stayed inside the right folder.

She opened the next assignment and did not look up when the perfect city continued outside the glass.

Month Two continued without asking whether continue was enough.
