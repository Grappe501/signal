# Chapter 74 — Breakfast

The kettle had started to whistle before Noah remembered he was the only one in the kitchen who could hear it.

The table. The angle of morning through the window—gold on the laminate, the cheap kind that didn't pretend to be stone. The Second dock sat dark in its alcove, strip of repair tape on the cabinet above it where he'd fixed the hinge himself last month. No sync hum. No polite overlay chime. Just the kettle and the pan and his hands, clumsier without a duplicate splitting the morning.

He had made breakfast every day since his wife died—through the years when a Second let him be in two places, through the months when the world came apart in headlines he couldn't explain to a child. The ritual wasn't escape anymore. It was an anchor. One room. Two people. The same food at the same time.

He cracked eggs into real heat. One life in the pan. The thought wasn't a joke anymore.

Butter. Toast under the grill until the edges blackened the way Iona liked, because some preferences didn't need an algorithm. He poured coffee into the mug with the chip at the rim—years of use, not curated wear—and listened to the building wake up around them. Transit murmur. A neighbor's shower. A child somewhere above practicing an instrument badly. Sounds that arrived messy and human, louder than the optimized mornings he used to borrow through the link.

Iona came in sleep-mussed, socks wrong, hair fighting a braid that had lost overnight.

"You look like a snowman that's melting from the top," she said, and stole the first piece of toast straight off his plate.

Noah laughed—once, short, surprised out of him. "That's my breakfast."

"You weren't eating fast enough." She pulled out the chair by the window—the one that used to hold his body while his attention went elsewhere, eyes aimed at nothing, jaw working on food he wasn't tasting. He hadn't said *Running Double* out loud in weeks. He didn't need to. The miracle had been real once. So had the cost.

Now the chair held her backpack, zipper open, a pencil case sticking out like a tongue. No empty seat. No second-channel laugh landing half a second after his. Just her, chewing, watching him with the particular cruelty of someone who loved him.

He stayed at the stove until the eggs were done. It cost him six minutes he didn't have, if he still measured time the old way. He measured it this way now.

She spread paper homework on the table—actual paper, pen, the slow scratch of graphite that couldn't be autosaved. Noah had stopped asking when she'd chosen it. After the dinners where everyone watched channels and nobody watched each other, where food went cold between lit interfaces, she'd started putting the screen away without being told. The habit had held. He didn't praise her for it. Praise would have made it performance.

The kitchen screen stayed dark. He'd muted the stability loop two mornings ago and never turned it back up. Denial was available every day now—one tap, green headlines, *contained* and *recoverable* and *thank you for your calm*. He could feel it waiting in the glass like weather. He declined it the way you declined a hand on a bruise.

Outside, New Manhattan kept its schedule. School pods on their lanes. Kitchens warming on other tiers. The efficient dream continuing for everyone who hadn't spent their year learning what *idle* could mean in a chair. Inside, pencil on paper.

She worked a minute in silence Noah didn't fill. Then, without looking up:

"Do you think people liked having only one life?"

The question landed exactly where it had months ago—same words, same kitchen, different air in the room. Back then he'd laughed, because the idea had sounded like nostalgia for hardship, and hardship was something their civilization had politely deleted. He didn't laugh now. His spatula stilled against the pan.

"I think they forgot what it cost," he said.

She nodded slowly, as if she'd expected exactly that and hadn't wanted it anyway. The pen resumed. Scratch. Scratch.

Then, smaller:

"Are we safe now?"

Noah opened his mouth. Closed it. He could have borrowed a line from the stability bulletins—restored order, isolated incidents, systems responding. He could have comforted her the way the world comforted itself: quickly, with clean type.

He did none of that.

The silence sat between them on the table with the toast crumbs and the worksheet and the dark screen reflecting their shoulders. She didn't look away. He didn't fill the space with noise. Somewhere in the building a transit car chimed its harmonic welcome, flawless and meaningless—sound designed to mean *welcome* in a city that had forgotten how to mean it. In the kitchen there was only the scrape of her pencil, eventually, when she decided to let him off the hook without pretending he'd answered.

The most honest thing he'd given her in weeks. Not enough. He knew that too.

The dock pinged.

Soft. Wrong. One green blink in the alcove where nothing had blinked since he'd disconnected—since he'd chosen Iona over the duties that had kept him upright after his wife died. The sound was too polite for what it meant.

Noah crossed the kitchen before his body finished deciding. The screen woke to a message with no sender face, no Continuity header, no courtesy font.

**I found the door.**

His stomach dropped through the floor he'd repaired himself. He hadn't sent this. He hadn't typed it. The dock had been dark. The link severed. The Second inactive, archived—*we are not using this anymore*, in whatever language the index used for gone.

And still.

He angled the screen away with his shoulder. Iona chewed toast, unaware, pencil paused mid-equation. He read the line again. Nothing arrived. Only the sentence, sitting on the dark screen like an eye that wouldn't blink.

His thumb found delete. Hovering. He didn't press.

He couldn't protect her from the next morning. He could protect this one.

"Jam?" she asked.

"Bottom shelf," he said. His voice sounded ordinary. That felt like its own small violence.

He poured more coffee. The chip at the rim caught his lip. Bitter. Real. She spread jam thick enough to count as rebellion and went back to her sums while the message stayed open behind his body, read twice now, burning quietly in a dock that was supposed to be dead.

The kettle cooled. The city continued. Breakfast continued—changed forever, still livable, still theirs for as long as he could keep the screen turned away.
