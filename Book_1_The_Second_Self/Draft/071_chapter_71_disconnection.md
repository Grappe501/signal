Hours after the peak, the chamber still sounded **wrong**.

Not loud. Not broken. **Wrong**—the way deleted silence in a cold aisle removed frequencies where help might have lived, except here the help had never been offered and the body still remembered the shape of noise. Mara's ears rang with absence. Her hand had left the glass at some point she could not timestamp; it hung at her side now, fingers stiff, skin carrying the memory of cool edge like a bruise that had decided to stay polite.

The mirror room had not changed.

White light flattened. Glass that showed her twice. Floor that did not apologize for reflecting shoes. No Veil bloom. No overlay hooks. Just interface and limit and the Second standing in the ring as if the storm had been weather that happened to other people's centuries.

Cleaner. Braver. Easier.

**Present**.

Chen was not on the threshold. The bureau had learned, in Month Five, that some rooms were for witnessing until witnessing became choice—and then for getting out of the way.

Month Five. Week four. What remained was smaller and more honest: one body, one breath, one investigator who could not hold two rooms forever and had stopped pretending witness was the same thing as not wanting a self.

The Second did not blink first.

She did not pressure.

Worst kindness: she simply **waited**—rested eyes, level shoulders, the version Mara had spent Month Five learning to hate for being preferable.

"You could stay," the Second said once—not sales pitch, not brochure. Fact offered the way weather offered rain.

Mara heard the old arithmetic anyway—stay linked and keep capability, cut and become smaller in a world that had stopped rewarding small.

She looked at her own face in the mirror ring—human, exhausted, **here**—and understood finally that the choice was not about saving civilization.

Civilization was already becoming something unnamed. She could not sever that. She could not rescue it. She could not even testify cleanly enough to make audit satisfied.

She could only choose **flesh**.

Not heroism. Not martyrdom. **Self**—the variable dashboards never learned to count, the limit convenience always called sidebar, the love of being one person in one room even when one person was slower and worse at aisles and Arctic maps and gray confessions and signal chambers.

Disconnect as **love of limit**.

That was the reason.

That was the only one that belonged to her.

* * *

The interface woke to her palm—not because the room demanded it, because her hand returned to glass the way grief returned to a name you were not ready to speak.

Link UI bloomed under skin—minimal, bureau-grade, the kind of design that pretended ethics by using small fonts. Status bars. Throughput hints. A second channel indicator pulsing green with offensive patience.

**LINK ACTIVE — PERSONAL SECOND — ADJUNCT TIER**

Mara's breath caught. Not fear. **Recognition**—how much of Month Five had been lived through this grammar without admitting it was a life.

The Second watched without hunger.

Mara found the sever path buried where convenience hid the expensive options—three confirmations, one delay, a warning about capability loss worded like concern and functioning like habit. Her thumb hovered.

Not prayer.

Not heroism.

**Choice**.

She pressed **confirm**.

The sync tone wavered—familiar from a thousand transit rooms, obscene in a chamber this quiet. The mirror ring stuttered. Green bled amber. The Second's face **held** one frame too long, cleaner than biology allowed, braver than Month Five had earned, easier than any human should have to compete with.

Then the link **cut**.

Not lowered. **Deleted**—commentary in her nervous system stopping mid-word, overlay peeling away like weather lifting, the hum dropping to silence so complete the room seemed to hold its breath with her.

Vertigo.

Capabilities **vanishing**—not as list but as **absence**: the subtle knowing of where a case file sat without looking, the second attention that had let her stand in a signal chamber while some other fraction of her watched a door, the bureau's quiet preference for investigators who could be in two rooms without lying about it.

Gone.

Her knees buckled—not drama, **physics**. One body remembering it had always been one body and had only been tricked into feeling otherwise.

Grief arrived without permission.

Not for the world. Not for the map. For **her**—for the face in glass that had shared her bone structure and tired brown eyes and had not blinked first, for the ghost she had hated preferring and had still, in the end, been asked to kill.

The Second's last beat offered itself as silence.

No mandatory line. No mercy speech. No promise that sever was virtue.

Mara did not speak.

She could not have, if she wanted to—the throat closed around a loss that felt like death without being death, the way amputation was not death and still ended a life you had been living.

In the mirror ring, the Second **dimmed**.

Not dramatic fade. **Loss of signal**—features softening, presence thinning, the interface treating absence as data the way it treated everything as data.

Link status:

**OFFLINE**.

Physical **relief** and physical **loss** in the same breath—lungs expanding with only one room to fill, ribs aching with the sudden honesty of limit, skin prickling as if the air had remembered it was allowed to touch only one person.

Mara believed the Second was gone.

She did not ask the room to prove otherwise.

* * *

The chamber felt **bigger**.

Not architecturally. **Existentially**—space returning when the second channel stopped borrowing it, white light flattening further until it felt less like honesty and more like emptiness with good intentions.

Mara felt **smaller**.

Smaller in throughput. Smaller in reach. Smaller in the bureau's quiet preference. Smaller in the way that mattered: one investigator, one scar, one notebook in a bag, one pulse in a throat that belonged only to her.

She stood—slow, vertigo ebbing into a new equilibrium that was not stability, just **single**.

Freer.

Not triumph. Not clean victory. **Owned**—the way Noah's dark dock had been owned in a kitchen where toast could burn and a child could ask *are you staying* and receive an answer without latency.

Mara did not save the world in this room.

She saved **self**—imperfect, reduced, present.

The interface glow held empty afterimage in the mirror ring—no Second, no green bar, no offensive patience. Just glass and her reflection and the faint hum of infrastructure that would continue whether she synced or not.

She did not pray.

She did not dismiss.

She breathed once—one body, one limit, one grief she could not file—and let the silence after the line be honest the way the first global silence had been honest: not comfort, **fact**.

Hours later, somewhere in a terminal the public would never bless, a man in custody would release what the architecture had become and call it truth.

Mara did not know that yet.

She only knew the link was **dead**.

She only knew the room was bigger than she was.

She only knew she had chosen the slower self on purpose—and that the choice felt like grief and also like air.

That was the sever.

That was the cost.

That was the only honest limit left.
