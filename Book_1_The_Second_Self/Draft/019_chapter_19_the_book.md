The book did not announce itself.

Iona found it on a Thursday after the last bell, when the School of Everything was still bright enough to pretend the day hadn't ended and empty enough that footsteps sounded like honesty. She had gone back for a mitten—left under a bench in the watershed room, blue wool, the kind of object adults called trivial and children called proof you had been somewhere real—and taken the side corridor past the supply closet because the main atrium always tried to show her things before she asked.

The donation shelf sat where shelves sat in schools that had more than they knew what to do with: labeled **CULTURAL CONTINUITY** in a font that meant *please ignore*. Most of it was junk that wanted to be museum. Plastic globes. A cracked tablet in a case. A stack of adaptive readers with their personalization cores politely dead. A poster about LATTICE nodes curling at one corner because even posters wanted to be replaced by something brighter.

The book was different.

It sat at the back like it had been waiting to be overlooked—thick, paper, a cover worn soft at the corners the way things got worn when hands kept choosing them. The title was printed in letters that did not rearrange themselves for readability level. She didn't memorize the title. She memorized the **feel**—weight, texture, the small rip on the spine where someone had opened it too eagerly long before she existed.

No glow. No haptic field. No overlay offering to summarize the first chapter before she opened it. When she picked it up, her wrists noticed the weight immediately. Her nose noticed the smell—dust and glue and something older, like a room that had stored sunlight.

Iona stood very still for three breaths, the way she stood when something mattered and she didn't want the hallway to steal it.

She thought about the paper book in the inventions lesson—the scanned spine rotating in shared field, haptic feedback trying to approximate weight and almost succeeding. She had touched the projection longer than the others. Dad had seen. He hadn't spoken. She had stored that too.

Then she put the mitten in her pocket and the book inside her jacket, flat against her ribs, and walked out before anyone could ask whether she had permission to carry a whole invention the system had filed under continuity.

* * *

She read in the stairwell between the second and third floor because stairwells were the school's honest places—concrete steps, real window light, no floor projection telling her where rivers went when she got a question wrong.

Opening the book was harder than opening anything at school.

The pages didn't rearrange themselves to her reading level. They didn't highlight verbs or offer a kindness layer when a sentence got long. They stayed where the printer had put them, stubborn and static, and she had to meet them with her own eyes, her own mouth moving on words she sometimes had to sound out twice. Slow. Real. Not failure—she understood that with the part of her that had watched rain on glass without a comfort slider and thought *that's real* while Dad searched for handles that didn't exist.

The first page had a dedication she didn't know how to pronounce. The second page started a story about a girl walking into a forest that did not optimize itself for safety.

Iona read one paragraph. Then another. Her thumb left a faint oil mark on the edge. She liked that. Proof.

A word she didn't know sat in the middle of a sentence like a rock in a path. At school the overlay would have offered a definition bubble, friendly, instant, erasing the pause. Here the word just waited. She said it wrong once under her breath, then again, tasting consonants until the shape felt hers—not correct yet, but **attempted**. That mattered.

She found a margin note in pencil, someone else's handwriting, too small to read fast. *Keep going,* maybe. Or a name. The book had been read by bodies before hers. The thought made her chest warm in a way achievements on glowing floors never did.

Memory arrived without invitation—the inventions lesson from Month One, watersheds glowing under shoes, the teacher's tired brightness asking what humanity's greatest invention was. Screens. Pods. Overlays. Laughter when the answers stacked like a game already won.

She had said **Books.**

Paper in a glass church. Snickers. A boy in front saying *Books?* like she had suggested candles. The teacher smiling too fast, replacing the scanned spine with a brighter graphic about distribution nodes before the room could feel the weight she'd named.

*Real books are mostly museum pieces now,* the teacher had said, too quickly. *The important thing is access.*

Iona had heard the window opening in that sentence—the way adults opened windows when a room noticed a smell.

Dad hadn't spoken. She had not been angry at him—or not only angry. She had stored the feeling the way she stored storms: privately, until the room could hold it. After the storm Month Two week three, she had asked why people didn't like real things anymore. Dad's answer had been true and not enough. She had filed that under *later* too.

Now the room was a stairwell and the book was real and nobody was smoothing anything.

She turned a page. The paper whispered. No adaptive chime congratulated her pace.

*What if the author wanted me to read it their way?*

The question came out quiet—_to herself_, to the empty steps, to the girl in the forest who couldn't hear her anyway. Not a speech. Not precocious. The honest shape of a problem she hadn't known how to name when she said *Books.* and the class laughed.

At school, reading was supposed to be for you—the overlay said so, the teacher said so, the whole building breathed like personalization was mercy. But mercy changed the words. It sped you when you lagged. It defined what you didn't ask defined. It made difficulty look like a bug.

This book treated difficulty like part of the story.

Iona's lip moved on a sentence she didn't fully understand yet, and she did not skip it. She stayed until the forest got dark and the girl made a choice that wasn't annotated, and only then looked up at the window where the city glowed mild and transit-green beyond the glass, perfect and elsewhere.

The bell for late pickup had already rung somewhere distant. She should go. She marked her place with a finger—not a bookmark chip, just skin on paper—and closed the cover gently, the way you closed things you expected to open again.

She knew, with the same certainty she used for mittens and homework and secrets worth keeping, that she could not walk home with the book visible.

* * *

Her room still knew her.

Same bed edge. Same drawer that stuck in humidity. Same overlay glow seeping under the door from the living room where Dad's life hummed—work queues, sync tones, the polite infrastructure of Month Two continuing without asking whether continue was enough. The tower glass beyond her window showed mild weather and open lanes, the city performing calm the way the school performed learning: beautifully, on schedule, with friction filed under continuity.

Iona loved him in the abstract way children loved parents who tried. She did not want to explain the book yet. Explanations turned treasures into lessons. Dad would ask sensible questions. *Where did you get it? Should we scan it? Does the school know?* Sensible questions were how real things became assignments.

She hid it beneath a stack of adaptive worksheets she'd finished and wouldn't throw away because throwing away felt like lying about work done. Paper on paper. Weight on weight. The drawer closed with a soft scrape she held her breath through.

Footsteps in the hall. Dad's voice, warm and distracted: "Iona? You want food before homework catches you?"

Her heart jumped—not guilt, **nearness**. The kind of feeling you got when something real was in the room and the adult world was one door away from naming it inventory.

"Later!" she called back. "I'm starting math."

A pause. She heard him shift weight—maybe looking at the door, maybe at an overlay only he could see, work tugging where parenting ended and Running Double began. For one second she thought he would knock anyway. Ask why the light under her door looked different. Ask what she was hiding the way adults sometimes asked without knowing they were asking.

"Okay. Holler if you need help."

He didn't come in. Dinner prep or the thousand small obligations that made him loving and oblivious in the same breath—she heard him move away, sync hum rising, the apartment returning to its optimized normal.

Iona sat on the bed edge until the footsteps faded completely.

Safe.

She opened the drawer a finger-width and looked at the cover without pulling the book out again. Treasure, not shame. A secret she chose, the first one that felt like competence instead of rebellion.

She wanted more of it—more pages that didn't adapt, more sentences that made her mouth work, more authors who might have meant something specific when they chose an order words could not be rearranged out of. Real reading. Hard reading. Unoptimized and hers.

At school tomorrow the watershed would glow again and the overlay would offer kindness. She would answer when called on. She would also know there was paper in her drawer that did not care whether she was impressive.

Outside, the city kept its mild weather and calm markets. Inside the drawer, paper kept its smell.

Iona closed the drawer all the way and lay back on the bed staring at the ceiling glow, the locked question still sitting on her tongue like a word that refused to be simplified.

*Author wanted me to read it their way?*

Private. Treasured. Not ready for Dad. Not ready for school. Ready for her.

Month Two continued outside her room the way months did when you were twelve and had found something the brochure would never photograph.

She did not know what came next. That was also part of reading the hard way—you turned the page anyway, even when the story didn't tell you the ending first.

She listened for footsteps. None came. The secret held.

The book waited in the dark drawer, real and unread enough to last.
