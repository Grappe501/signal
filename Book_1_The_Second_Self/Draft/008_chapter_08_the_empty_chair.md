The apartment had learned to host.

Noah had moved the table closer to the window for the occasion—not for the view, which was the same optimized hush as always, but because tier relatives liked light when they visited, and light made them feel the evening was going well. He set chargers he almost never used, wine that had been a gift, napkins folded the way his mother had taught him before the city decided efficiency was a moral position. The laminate caught gold from the overhead panel, the cheap kind that didn't pretend to be stone. Morning's gold still lived in that surface. Tonight it tried to look like ceremony.

Iona Vale helped without being asked—plates aligned, water glasses filled, the quiet competence of a twelve-year-old who watched adults for a living now. She had been quiet since the school day, not sullen, just storing, and Noah had learned not to pry when she wore that particular stillness.

The Second dock hummed in its alcove down the hall, polite sync tone confirming link strength. Noah had muted his overlay for hosting. A host should be present, or should perform present convincingly enough that nobody had to feel embarrassed for him.

The door chimed. Tier relatives arrived in the order the city preferred: smiles first, coats second, compliments about how much Iona had grown third.

Aunt Ren from two tiers up—gold band stable, voice warm. Cousin Dae with a partner Noah could never remember the name of because it changed with the season. Pleasant people. Pleasant was a technology too.

"Smells wonderful," Ren said, which was not true yet; the main dish was still finishing. Noah accepted the lie the way you accepted weather.

They settled into the dining area with the easy frictionlessness of family that did not live together but still believed it shared a story. Conversation began in fragments—work schedules, transit delays praised as minor, a cousin's promotion rendered in the soft language of *recoverable*. Noah poured wine. Iona took her seat where she always did, not at the children's edge tonight but one chair in from it, watching.

The table was full.

It was also not.

* * *

Dinner progressed the way optimized dinners did: food arriving on time, voices overlapping, assistance layers suggesting topics when silence threatened. Noah carved, passed, refilled glasses. Host competence. Hairline strain beneath it he hoped read as fatigue rather than fear.

Halfway through the second course, Ren laughed at something Dae's partner said—or appeared to laugh. The sound arrived a half-second after the partner's punchline, the tell Noah had learned to recognize in himself: overlay latency, reaction pulled from a channel the room couldn't see. Dae nodded along a beat late, eyes glass-bright for a moment, then refocused on the plate.

Bodies here. Minds elsewhere. Not absent the way absence used to mean—still chewing, still nodding, still performing the choreography of togetherness—but **elsewhere** in the precise way Running Double had made normal.

Iona did not laugh on the delayed beat. She watched Ren's face the way she had watched the class after *Books*—measuring the gap between what was said and what was meant.

Noah's thumb found the rim of his water glass. No chip there. He missed the chip anyway.

Conversation splintered again: school pods, allocation grids, a story about Dubai's sunset show that made Noah think of half-seconds people chose to forget. The city had compartments. Family dinner was one of the better ones.

Then Ren lifted her glass.

"To family," she said, the oldest toast, the one that required least specificity.

Glasses rose. Noah rose. Iona lifted her juice with deliberate care. Dae and partner smiled. The circle completed itself—

—and left one place untouched.

The empty chair sat at Noah's right hand, setting complete: plate, napkin, fork aligned with the others, wine poured and untouched. A chair for someone who was not coming down the hall, not stepping in from the dock, not surfacing from a link with a half-second delay. A chair the table had kept setting since the first year after his wife died because tier relatives had decided, without a meeting, that removing it would be worse than seeing it.

Nobody named her.

Nobody named the empty.

They drank as if the circle were whole.

Noah drank because not drinking would have been a speech.

* * *

Absence in presence was not a new idea. It was a **practice**—politeness trained into muscle until muscle forgot it was grief.

He looked at the chair the way you looked at a scar you had promised not to touch in public. The wine caught light. The fork reflected the panel glow. Everything at that place setting was real except the person.

Iona's eyes found his across the table. Not accusing. Not asking him to fix it. Just **seeing** whether he saw.

He could have said her name. Could have said *We don't have to keep the setting.* Could have broken the social contract with a sentence small enough to sound like kindness. The room would have smoothed it—assistance layer, mood stabilization, Ren murmuring *Of course, whatever you're comfortable with*—and the evening would have continued on a new track that still avoided the truth.

Noah closed his mouth.

Silence was not agreement. Silence was the tax he paid for keeping Iona inside a family that still believed stability was love.

Ren was already talking again—something about a colleague's Second handling a deposition while the colleague attended a child's recital, admiration and unease braided together in the same tone people used for miracles they didn't want to examine. Dae laughed on schedule this time, or close enough. The empty chair stayed where it was, still as furniture, obscene in its completeness.

Noah cut another slice he did not want and passed the dish.

* * *

Dinner continued.

That was the horror—not a reveal, not a sting, just **continuation**. Dessert. Compliments. A discussion of whether Iona should advance to the accelerated stream next term, as if streams were the only currents that mattered. Iona answered in short sentences. Noah cleared plates while his overlay tried to ping him back to work and he ignored it with the stubbornness of a man who suspected that if he opened the grids now he would never fully return to the room.

Cleanup was ordinary: water hot, cloth moving, stacked ceramics clicking. The empty chair's plate came away untouched, wine poured down the sink without comment because waste was less frightening than naming why the wine had been poured at all. Iona dried forks. Ren offered to help and mostly supervised.

When the relatives left, the apartment felt larger and thinner.

Noah stood at the table with one chair still pulled out, setting cleared except the napkin folded where a hand might have rested. The Second dock hummed down the hall—link available, channel open, the city's offer to split yourself when one room was too loud for grief or too quiet for fear.

Iona paused in the kitchen doorway.

"Dad?"

"Yeah?"

She looked at the chair. Looked at him. Did not ask.

"Goodnight," she said, and went to her room with socks wrong and braid loose, twelve and already learning which absences adults would not speak into existence.

Noah turned off the overhead panel. The chair remained visible in window light from the city—gold on laminate, cheap and honest, holding space for a person the family had agreed to love by **not rearranging the furniture**.

He could have put the chair back. Could have broken the ritual in private.

He didn't.

The sync tone pulsed once, steady, asking nothing and everything. New Manhattan kept its lanes open beyond the glass. Somewhere investigators were widening cases; somewhere a child had said *Books* and been laughed at and had not bent.

Noah washed the last glass and set it to dry.

The empty chair stayed where it was.

The apartment agreed to continue without asking whether continue was enough.

He said nothing.

The city did not correct the sentence.
