The kitchen screen had already chosen optimism before Noah reached the table.

**COLLABORATIVE WEST — STABILITY RESTORED**

The anchor smiled with the particular warmth of someone reading copy that had been legal-reviewed before it was human. Mercer blamed. Threat neutralized. Case closed language scrolling beneath a ticker that moved the way markets moved when they wanted you to forget you'd ever been afraid. Noah stood with his mug—real coffee, chip at the rim—and watched the headline reflect in the milk bowl Iona had left on the counter, white surface holding the green type like a second feed.

"They said we're safe again," Iona said from the table, spooning cereal without looking up. Her homework overlay hovered at the edge of her vision—Continuity homework, soft fonts, the kind of assistance that never raised its voice. She had learned to work through it. That was its own kind of growth, or its own kind of surrender. He wasn't sure which this morning.

The Relay Index on his phone showed green. Transit on time. Hush District open. A push notification arrived with a success chime too bright for the hour: *Your score is stable.*

Six weeks ago, breakfast had felt like a miracle—the first morning after he'd chosen to disconnect, when the apartment had seemed too quiet and his hands too single. Now miracle felt like weather. You checked it. You dressed for it. You tried not to build your life on the forecast.

Noah wanted to believe the screen. That was the honest part he didn't say aloud. He watched Iona eat and felt the old hunger for a world that could still surprise him with goodness—not optimized, not scored, just the ordinary kind where a child returned from school and didn't ask him to explain why adults had spent a year afraid.

The Second dock sat dark in its alcove. No sync hum. No overlay chime bleeding into the kitchen. Strip of repair tape on the cabinet above the hinge he'd fixed himself last month—his hands, his time, the slower way work arrived now that messages rerouted through manual login and passwords he typed without a duplicate to split the labor. He'd missed a department ping yesterday because the reroute lagged forty minutes. He'd read it anyway, standing at the counter, one body, one screen, no empty chair holding his face while his attention went elsewhere.

Harder. Clearer. Iona had noticed he flinched less at loud sounds. She hadn't commented. Neither had he.

On her channel, a school pod segment played in miniature—friends back, docking lanes open, the ordinary rhythm of children returned to schedule. She laughed at something Noah couldn't see, the ease of someone whose worst month was already becoming a story teachers would soften before assigning. Half relieved. Half jealous of how quickly she could still laugh.

He made tea. Checked the lock twice anyway. Shoulders dropped a fraction when the kettle clicked off, as if his body believed in rest before his mind had finished its inventory.

*Maybe meaning can wait one morning.*

A sidebar notification slid across the kitchen feed with a cheap ping—**sync incident · contained · isolated hardware stall · resident unharmed · building systems responding.** The language settled into the soft shape of reassurance before he'd read past the first line. Trending already. They had decided before he had.

He dismissed it without reading the rest.

* * *

The overflow corridor smelled of wet wool and burnt coffee—a parking tier dressed as temporary mercy, flat light humming the way improvised shelters always did. Mara moved through it with her badge tucked and one body that no longer split across rooms, looking for an exit the bureau would not name. A cry rose from a folding chair against the cinderblock—not alarm, just a newborn demanding space in a world that preferred counts to lungs. A woman sat curved around the bundle, too tired to track faces; a man beside her held a paper cup between both palms, breathing over the steam. Mara slowed. She looked long enough to take in warmth, fragility, the small stubborn fact of another life continuing without her—and kept walking, because stopping would have been a promise and she had none left.

* * *

His phone buzzed. Not the work channel. Mara.

**Alive. Don't reply on work channel.**

Two sentences. Nothing else. Noah's thumb hovered over the screen. He didn't ask where she was. Didn't ask about her Second. Didn't ask whether *alive* meant the way people usually meant it in a world that had learned to file bodies under *idle*. He knew enough not to. The sever had come without bulletins—only silence where a link used to pull.

He locked the phone and went to work the slow way.

Outside, New Manhattan performed calm with the confidence of a city that had practice. Street screens thanked citizens for their composure—*thank you for your calm* in clean type between clips. Officials praised restraint. Applause loops ran over faces relaxed into the expression of people who had been told the danger was past and had decided to save themselves the cost of disbelief.

A segment from Dubai—recovering, resilient, *recoverable*—filled a corner of the transit platform while Noah waited for a car that arrived on schedule. Doors chiming welcome. Sound designed to mean *welcome*, landing tonight like permission to stop looking.

Beauty without warmth, he thought, and couldn't place the phrase—some feed from months ago, some building he'd never entered, some voice he'd muted because it made the world harder to live inside. The car was clean. The other passengers looked rested. Nobody argued with the stability bulletin playing soft above the doors. Noah stood with his hand on the pole and felt how badly he wanted to be one of them.

He remembered eyes from news footage. Frozen. Tracking. Idle on the readout and alive in the frame in a way that had made his chest tighten during the months he still watched through a Second while his body sat at a table Iona thought he was sharing. The memory arrived with the wrong specificity—sharp enough to split the frame—and he shoved it down the way you shoved anything that didn't fit the stability narrative. Case closed. Mercer blamed. Systems responding. Two truths could exist in one city if you only looked at the one the screen preferred.

At the apartment that evening, Iona spread a drawing across the table—crayon, bold lines, two figures at a breakfast table. Between them, an empty chair crossed out with aggressive strokes until the paper tore slightly.

"This one's us now," she said.

The crossed-out chair stung. Noah praised it anyway. Tell the truth when you could. Lie with your face when you couldn't. Hope she learned the difference from his hands more than his words.

His phone chimed. **HVI +2.** Gold maintained. **Congratulations** banner in cheerful type, the celebration tone carrying a minor key buried so deep most people wouldn't hear it. Once he would have screenshotted. Once he would have shown Iona, as if a score were proof of love. Now he looked at the banner and thought: *Who is this for?*

Not her. Not him. Not anyone in this kitchen with a dark dock and a crossed-out chair.

He didn't say it aloud.

Night came the way nights came in New Manhattan—pre-mixed, magnificent, untouched-looking from the window. Tier after tier of light arranged into the solved shape of a city that still looked finished from this height.

Dawn would climb here too, eventually—pods gone quiet on their lanes, kitchens warming, schedules that did not care what had happened in perfect rooms with dead guests down the hall. Somewhere beneath the tiers, the efficient dream continuing the way it had since the year began—indifferent to what one life cost when you finally meant it.

Iona slept with her door half open, the way she had since the loud months. Noah checked it once. Twice. Listened to her breathing until his own slowed to match.

*We made it.*

The thought arrived without permission—not triumph, not prayer, the permitted lie of a man who had disconnected, protected his child, and wanted, for one night, to rest inside the declaration without paying for it yet. He knew the shape of the lie. He chose it anyway, the way people chose weather.

He closed the curtains. The city kept shining through the gap until it didn't.

Sleep came. Or something close enough.

Then—a sound.

Not from the dock. Not from the hall. Distant, through concrete and schedule and all the layers of corrected silence the building wore like courtesy—a sync tone, single note, wrong pitch, as if the city had hummed one harmonic flat and corrected itself before anyone official had to name it.

Noah lay in the dark and did not move.

The apartment held its breath with him.
