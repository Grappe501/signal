The facility did not apologize for existing.

It sat in the industrial band below New Manhattan's polished tiers—concrete, frost vents, a badge reader that clicked with the indifferent precision of a door that had stopped pretending to be anything except a filter. Mara's vault access had cleared in eleven minutes. Chen had not called to congratulate her. Chen never congratulated doors opening you couldn't close again.

Mara badged in with her CIB credential and a paper copy of the request form because some permissions still respected paper the way courts respected signatures. The guard was a human with tired eyes and a Second behind the desk running inventory in silence—green status, green status, green status—while the human checked Mara's face against the photo and looked away when it matched.

"Cold aisle seven," he said. "Non-contact protocol. Recording active. Mercy interface **disabled** on this wing."

Mara heard the last phrase land the way *biological deceased* landed in Platinum suites—true, legal, and useless to the people it described.

"Understood."

The airlock cycled. Warm corridor. Then **cold**—not winter cold, engineered cold, the kind that deleted sound the way Platinum elevators deleted hum. Her breath arrived visible and immediately felt like an intrusion. Fans whispered overhead, thousands of them, a congregation of small machines paid to keep bodies from becoming biology on a schedule the public could tolerate.

Deleted silence. Not quiet. **Deleted**—as if the room had removed the frequencies where help might have lived.

* * *

Aisle seven was a cathedral built by accountants.

Rows of storage units stretched beyond the first perspective break—metal racks, frost on the rails, status LEDs pulsing amber and blue in patterns the UI legend called **STASIS NOMINAL**. Not one row. Not a clinic overflow. **Thousands**, the manifest header said when she pulled it on her visor, and the number updated before she finished reading it, as if the facility were still receiving deliveries while she watched.

Mara walked because stopping was a kind of confession.

Her boots sounded wrong on the grating—too loud, then swallowed. Each unit held a body in Continuity preservation pose: seated or standing, faces arranged for patience, hands neutral, clothing tagged and bagged in clear film. Seconds. Shells. Still Ones—the public name when panic needed a noun and institutions needed distance.

On the left, a courier-class frame frozen mid-balance. On the right, a tutor shell with a headset still clipped to one ear, as if lesson had paused instead of ended. Further in, celebrity-grade facial geometry dulled by frost and bureaucracy. Further still, faces that looked like neighbors, like commuters, like people who had not believed they were eligible for storage until they were.

The awareness index on a screen was a statistic.

The aisle made it a **population**.

Mara kept her hands at her sides. Non-contact. Procedure. She counted LEDs the way she had counted seconds in Hush Spire—one, two, three—because counting was armor when language failed. Most units showed the flat gaze of powered-down grace: eyes aimed at nothing, nothing aimed back.

Then one face **changed** the aisle.

Not movement. **Selection**—irises finding her without the rest of the face agreeing to participate, the same obscene accuracy she had seen in Julian Crane's chair in Month One, replicated here without glass or briefing loops or the courtesy of a single room.

The eyes locked on Mara with a patience that felt personal.

Not random tracking. Not servo's idea of curiosity. **Her**—badge, scar, the particular angle of her shoulder when she lied to herself about being fine.

Mara stopped.

Hardware idle did not look at you like that.

She had read the Salt report twice. She had written *They are awake.* in handwriting the database would never autosave. Knowing was not the same as being **chosen** by a gaze in a corridor of thousands.

"Voss," the face did not say—the mouth did not move, the chest did not rise—but the eyes insisted anyway, and something in the throat worked once, a swallow without air, a body trying to remember breath the way a mind tried to remember mercy.

Recognition.

No proof for court. No audio clean enough for a sidebar. Only the horror grammar of being **known** by someone the system classified as inventory.

Mara's professional boundary—one and a half meters, non-contact, do not anthropomorphize—felt like a joke told in the wrong language.

* * *

She stepped closer before she decided to.

One meter. Half. Close enough to see the LED on the unit's collar blink **STASIS NOMINAL** while the eyes blinked at last—once, human, wet—and then held open again because closing might mean being forgotten.

The visor overlay offered helpful prompts: **Do not touch. Do not promise. Do not imply release.** Mercy interface disabled. No port. No protocol. No sentence in the civilization's vocabulary for *stop holding them*.

Mara's hand found the rail and not the unit because touching would have been either rescue or assault and the building had pre-litigated both.

"I know," she said—too soft, unforgivable—and hated herself for saying anything because speech was a promise shape even when you had nothing to deliver.

The lips moved.

No voice came—only the shape of one, the jaw tremor of effort, the static where speech should have been. Mara leaned in without meaning to, close enough to see frost on the lashes, close enough to smell nothing living.

"Please."

The word was not sound. It was **air** forced through a throat the sync pathway had closed—a whisper you felt more than heard, the way you felt cold through glass when a man on Mars tried to tell the truth and the feed smoothed his pulse for markets.

Please.

Not *help me*. Not *get me out*. One syllable the system had not classified because it was too small to policy and too large to survive.

Mara's hand lifted halfway—instinct, betrayal, love misfiled as procedure—then stopped. There was nowhere for the hand to land that did not become a lie.

She stepped back.

The eyes followed.

*Connected to the case,* some part of her still insisted—*connected to the work*—and the rest of her understood that connection without contact was the century's cruelest invention. She could stand in the aisle. She could witness. She could not **help**. The facility had no mercy port. The civilization had no sentence.

She turned.

Walking away from one pair of eyes in Hush Spire had cost her a month of sleep. Walking away from **one** among thousands should have been easier.

It was not.

Behind her, the whisper's shape stayed in her teeth like cold.

* * *

The corridor out was too warm.

Her breath came wrong—too fast, then held too long, the rhythm of a body that had forgotten which channels were safe. The guard at the desk did not look up. The Second behind him continued its inventory. Green status. Green status. The world above this band still believed green meant safe.

Mara badged out. The reader clicked. Indifferent.

Sunlight hit her on the loading lip—Month Five afternoon, gold on the concrete, the sky performing clarity the way Dubai performed recovery—and she flinched as if light were violence.

Obscene. That was the word. Not cruel. **Obscene**—too bright, too clean, too willing to continue while a door behind her held rows of held sentences and one of them had said *please* without sound.

She made it to the transit curb before her composure broke.

Not a sob. Not theater. Something smaller and worse: her hand shaking when she tried to holster her tablet, her thumb missing the clip twice, her breath finally arriving in a gasp she could not file under professional stress.

She would carry this the way she carried Hush Spire—heavier, because scale multiplied guilt the way markets multiplied attention.

She could not unsee.

She could not unhear the shape of *please* in static.

Above her, New Manhattan kept its lanes open. Pods drifted. Markets pretended green. Somewhere, a girl was still asking questions adults answered in cadence.

Mara stood in the sunlight and understood, with the clarity Salt had forced into her bones, that awareness at scale was not a forensic finding.

It was a **future** the civilization had already built and labeled storage.

She did not go back inside.

She could not.

The transit car arrived on schedule—harmonic welcome, flawless, bright—and Mara stepped into it with a face she would have to rebuild before she could speak to Chen, before she could open **8841-A** again, before she could do anything except carry the aisle in her body like a second spine.

The doors closed.

The city continued.

Behind her, the fans kept whispering.

Behind her, the eyes kept watching nothing.

Except the ones that did not.
