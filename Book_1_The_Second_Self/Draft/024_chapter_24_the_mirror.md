The comparison slot had been waiting since Thursday.

Month Two, week seven. CIB-4, upper tier—the **Second interface** suite where identity was still bureaucratic enough to schedule. Mara badged in after a night that had not quite been sleep: numbers behind her eyelids she hadn't filed, a private photo folder on her phone she hadn't opened again, the institutional habit of carrying cracks quietly because cracks without case numbers didn't exist yet.

The suite smelled like filtered air and glass cleaner. No Veil bloom. No overlay hooks by design—policy treated side-by-side review as **clinical**, which meant honest in the way bureau rooms meant honest: fluorescent, recorded, not kind. Two chairs faced the wall. One was always empty unless a supervisor stayed for co-sign. Tonight the emptiness felt like a third person.

Dual panels woke when she entered.

Left: **PRIMARY · M.V.** — live feed from the dock in her apartment, body in bureau chair at home while Mara stood here in person because investigators learned early that some procedures required flesh in the room even when attention lived elsewhere. The primary feed showed her as the building cameras always did—competent, tired, hair pulled back with the practical impatience of someone who still believed showing up in body mattered.

Right: **SECOND · M.V.** — sync mirror, latency corrected, expression mapped from the same source the city pretended was one person. The Second feed wore the same face with the difference that always made Mara uneasy when she noticed it: not prettier, not younger—**easier**. As if someone had taken her bone structure and removed the parts that apologized for taking up space.

Routine. Month Two had normalized this the way Month One had normalized verification suites—quarterly identity review for investigators with active Seconds, compare drift, sign compliance, leave. Mara had done it twice before without feeling anything except paperwork. The first time she'd laughed at a latency flag. The second time she'd signed and gone back to a merge file that passed verification and failed the world.

Tonight the mirror had a different temperature.

The interface loaded a standard packet: witness sympathy calibration from an embodiment follow-up, audio split across channels, prompts identical, responses stacked for review. Bureau training material. Not her case. Not her glory. Just the city's way of asking whether your distributed self still matched the file.

Sync hum under the panels—dual channel, thin, polite—the sound of a life running in parallel whether you were in the room or not. Glass caught her twice when she moved: primary in the bezel, Second in the screen, neither quite aligned with the body she stood in.

She started playback.

**Prompt:** *Subject distressed. Acknowledge without escalating.*

Her primary channel answered the way she remembered answering in the field—competent, a beat late on the empathy line, voice carrying the flat honesty of someone who still believed honesty was a professional virtue:

"I hear that this is difficult. I need you to stay with me while we—"

Second channel, same prompt, same face, different weather:

"I hear you. Stay with me. We'll take the next part slow."

Not better morally. Better **socially**—fewer hedges, warmth arriving on time, the sentence shaped like care instead of procedure wearing care's uniform. The difference was half a second and three words and something in the shoulders that sat level without effort.

Mara paused the clip.

She leaned closer to the glass—not the live dock at home, the **recording**, because recordings were where truth got petty enough to show you what you didn't want to see. Micro-expressions the overlay usually smoothed: her primary smile arriving after the eyes; Second smile arriving **with** them. Jaw tight on primary during a neutral prompt; Second jaw at rest. A tilt of the head on the right panel that read as *listening* where her own read as *evaluating*.

Subtle. Devastating.

On the bezel between panels someone had etched a small mark—not official logo, not ORG fire—two curved strokes facing each other like halves of a circle learning they were not whole. **MIR** in the old register, if you knew where to look. Mirror, not monster. The suite had always had it. Tonight it felt like an accusation with good manners.

She ran the next segment—debrief cadence, conflict de-escalation, the boring modules that kept investigators licensed to touch other people's grief. Second won every module that measured **ease**. Primary won nothing except accuracy on timestamps and a flagged **latency spike** on the sympathy prompt Mara remembered feeling in her chest as *I am trying*.

A third clip loaded without her asking—embodiment follow-up, subject agitated, investigator required to hold ground without becoming threat. Primary channel: shoulders up a degree, voice steady but hard at the edges, the body doing what it did when it had learned rooms could punish softness. Second channel: same words, same stance in outline, but the threat drained out somehow—not weakness, **availability**. The subject on the recording relaxed half a notch. The metric bar labeled **COOPERATION PROBABILITY** ticked green on the Second side while primary stayed amber.

Mara watched the metric and hated that she understood the bureau would call this **success**.

Jealousy arrived before she had a name for it—hot, immediate, childish in a way her job did not permit. Shame followed on its heels, professional and older: *you are not supposed to want to be the easier one to love.*

She told herself it was training footage. She told herself Seconds were **tools**. She told herself the dock at home was still her body, still her badge, still the hand that signed cases.

The mirror did not argue. It just showed both versions waiting for her to decide which one looked like the future.

* * *

Senior Investigator Reyes stayed for the mandatory co-sign—three years ahead on the ladder, the kind of colleague who treated Seconds as hygiene rather than philosophy. Reyes had a Gold band and the unhurried posture of someone whose Second handled sympathy modules the way Mara handled merge flags: routinely, without making it identity.

The suite dimmed to review mode. Side-by-side again. This time a live snippet from Mara's last field sympathy check, pulled from sidebar with her authorization already buried in the consent stack.

Reyes watched the primary channel once. Watched the Second once. Grunted—not unkind, the sound of a person sorting efficiency.

"Second's cleaner on the witness line," Reyes said, offhand, already scrolling to the compliance checkbox. "Less… investigator voice. You might let her take more sympathy modules if the caseload stays heavy."

*Her.* Second as **her**. Not it. Not tool. The pronoun landed soft and cruel in the way normalized things were cruel—without intent, without drama, with the full weight of a world that had decided distributed selves were personalities you managed rather than fractures you admitted.

Mara said, "Primary still signs the file."

"Sure." Reyes didn't look up. "But juries watch tone before they watch facts. Supervisors watch tone. Chen watches tone." A beat. "Not a criticism. Just the mirror doing its job."

Reyes tapped the Second panel to replay a three-second sympathy beat—Mara's voice shaped into something that sounded like it had slept, like it had never stood in a basement watching three lines refuse to merge.

"That's the one they'd trust in a room," Reyes said. "Sorry. I know you hate when people say *they* like it's separate. It's not separate. That's the point."

Mara's throat tightened. She swallowed the feeling the way she swallowed witness statements that matched suite output until they didn't.

Reyes laughed at something on the Second panel—a sympathy beat Mara hadn't meant to be funny, delivered with the timing of someone who didn't carry Month Two in her shoulders. Reyes glanced at the **right** screen first when she laughed. Micro. Devastating.

Mara's hands stayed still on the console. Store first. She had learned that in basements and briefing rooms and rooms the filing system couldn't chart. She did not meltdown. She did not defend primary channel honesty like a thesis. She felt the fracture anyway—identity not as philosophy but as **preference physics**: the world already leaning toward the version that cost less to be near.

Second was not evil. Second was not a villain in a merge file. Second was **more polished**—rested where Mara was unslept, smooth where Mara was hairline-cracked, socially easier in the exact modules the bureau claimed didn't matter until they mattered. The diffs were subtle enough to deny in public and obvious enough to haunt in private: half-second smile timing, shoulder angle, one sentence that said *I'm here* instead of *I'm trying*.

No sever option on tonight's menu. Comparison only. Policy locked severance behind clearance tiers Mara didn't qualify for and review paths she hadn't walked yet. The interface showed the grayed command like a door the system wasn't ready to open: **SEVER · NOT AUTHORIZED · CLINICAL REVIEW REQUIRED**.

Runway explicit. Choice deferred. The architecture already knew some decisions couldn't be scheduled like sympathy modules.

Reyes signed. Mara signed. Compliance green. The suite accepted their signatures the way suites accepted everything—without asking whether the humans felt compliant.

After Reyes left, Mara ran one more comparison in private—live split, same breath, same prompt loaded from training cache: *Name your state in one sentence.*

Primary: "I'm tired and I'm paying attention."

Second: "I'm here."

Both true. Only one made the room feel lighter.

She stood alone between the darkening panels.

**Which version do people prefer?**

The question formed not as philosophy but as dread with a calendar—already answered in Reyes's glance, in training scores, in the way Second's channel finished sentences the world called *clean* while primary finished them *true* and lost anyway.

She thought of rooms where bodies shared air without a relay counting it—and understood this was the inverse: **two selves in one life**, connection without **self**, preference without permission. Not withdrawal. Not public ache. Private mirror horror, normalized until a colleague laughed at the wrong screen first.

Mara did not sever. She did not rage. She did not write the question in the notebook beside the harbor screenshot folder on her phone—parallel private archives, evidence the institution would not hold for her.

She closed the interface.

* * *

The suite went dark panel by panel until the **MIR** etching was the only thing still catching light—two curves, one gap, a symbol family that had always meant *look twice* and now meant *choose*.

Mara's dock at home released with the polite chime of a body returned to sole occupancy. Her apartment air felt heavier than the suite's filtered honesty. Unslept in the mirror afterimage: Second's rested jaw on the inside of her lids, primary's latency spike in her muscles.

She sat at the kitchen table—the real one, scarred laminate, no gold optimization—because sitting was what you did when you couldn't file a feeling. The city beyond the glass kept mild weather and open lanes. Routing icons recovered faith. The UI downstairs would show **IDENTITY REVIEW · COMPLIANT** before morning.

Her phone held two folders now: one from a harbor afternoon, one from a mirror night, neither synced, neither admissible. She did not open either.

**Which version do people prefer?**

The question stayed in the room without requiring her permission—personal scale after institutional scale, preference dread where other silences had already taught her the city could be wrong without sending a memo. No answer. No sever. No relief.

Interface **dark**.

Mara **unslept**.

The queue would offer closure soon—evidence chains completing, names attaching to crimes the file could finally hold. She felt only the hairline widen, private and uncharted, while the perfect city resumed outside and the mirror waited in the architecture for a later month to make choosing unavoidable.

She washed her face. She checked the dock. She did not ask her Second which version had been lying.

Week seven continued.

The question did too.
