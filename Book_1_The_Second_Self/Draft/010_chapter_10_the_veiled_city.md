Chen called it a day off the way Chen called everything—policy dressed as mercy.

"You've had two cities in ten days," she said. "Pattern language stays in draft until I can defend it. Take twenty-four hours where nobody dies on your shift."

Mara looked at the desk. **8841-A** and **8847-C** sat open in the gray interface like teeth she couldn't stop touching with her tongue. The notebook beside them held two handwritings of wrong shape. Port Meridian's harbor smell still lived in her jacket lining though the filters had scrubbed the transit car clean.

"I'm still investigating," she said.

"You're still breathing," Chen replied. "That's the bar today."

Mara did not argue. She changed into street clothes—real cotton, no bureau cut—and left the mug on the desk because taking it felt like admitting she might come back before morning. The cases stayed open on the tablet in her bag. Not a montage. Not a vow. Just tabs she refused to close because closed was how departments learned to forget.

Outside, New Manhattan was already wearing its Veil.

She had grown up before the overlay pass made beauty default, when mornings arrived loud and unedited and you learned which corners the city hid on purpose. Now the Spine rolled gold at the brow whether the sun earned it or not. Transit glass softened smog into distance. Strangers' faces arrived with the friction filed down—not lies, exactly; more like kindness applied at scale.

Mara told herself she did not need to hate it to stay honest.

* * *

The shopping tier was a lesson in why people defended the Veil at dinner parties.

She bought nothing important—a replacement filter for the bureau mug, fruit she could have had delivered, a scarf because the color pleased her when the overlay made the weave look like weather she remembered from childhood trips upstate. On the transit lip between tiers, the car's interior bloomed: frost on the window where there was only treated glass, a meadow horizon where there was only another tower's flank. The illusion was not subtle. It was not meant to be. It was meant to make the commute feel like a gift instead of a schedule.

Mara laughed once at a shared overlay joke—a cartoon pod mascot bowing to the crowd—and felt the laugh arrive real in her chest. That was the seduction: not that you forgot the overlay existed, but that you stopped resenting it long enough to enjoy the ride.

Entertainment district next, because Chen's day off did not come with instructions and Mara had never learned leisure as a skill. A street loop ran preview clips of tonight's lattice shows—water arcs, responsive facades, the kind of synchronized beauty Dubai exported and New Manhattan improved with better bandwidth. Mara watched from a public bench while faces around her lifted in unison. A child on a Second's shoulders pointed at colors the child couldn't name yet. An elder couple held hands without looking at each other, trusting the overlay to carry the wonder so their bodies could rest.

For twenty minutes she almost forgot **8841-A**.

Not erased. Deferred—the way you deferred hunger when someone set food in front of you and you were allowed to eat. The case sat in her bag like a stone in a pocket. She felt its weight when she shifted. She chose not to shift.

Food came from a stall that advertised *raw option available* in small type almost no one selected. Mara selected it anyway out of habit, then let the Veil sweeten the plate when the vendor smiled—the tomatoes brighter, the bread steam more cinematic, the stranger beside her at the counter turning a complaint about transit delay into a joke that landed soft enough to share. Artificial togetherness. She had spent a week inside rooms where records clarified nothing; here, for one meal, the city performed being kind on purpose, and her shoulders dropped a centimeter she hadn't known they were holding.

The stranger nodded at her exit. "Good day for it."

"Yeah," Mara said. "Good day."

Smoother than raw. She noticed the smoothness and did not punish it. Honest cops were allowed one afternoon.

* * *

Late afternoon pulled her toward the Spine's lower seam where maintenance still showed through if you knew where to look.

Mara told herself she was walking off lunch. The truth was simpler: investigators who couldn't stop investigating looked at edges. A scaffold strip ran along the pedestrian deck—real concrete, unbuffered, the gray the city used when it wasn't performing. For one step the Veil thinned and she saw the seam: overlay bloom on the left, bare wall on the right, the join imperfect enough to feel human.

She glanced at it the way you glanced at a scab.

The transit bell chimed its harmonic welcome three tiers up. Audio sweetened. Crowd density nominal. Then—

A flicker.

One frame. Maybe two. The promenade overlay stuttered—gold to flat gray to gold—while the audio bed hiccupped beneath the bell, a syllable of static swallowed before her blink finished. On a passerby's wrist, a sync icon jumped **IDLE** to **—** to **IDLE** again.

Mara stopped walking.

The Veil resumed. Seamless. Smiling. The stranger who had hiccupped didn't notice; their wrist continued its steady pulse. The city kept its lanes open. Beauty returned on schedule as if the schedule had never broken.

She stood on the scaffold strip long enough to count her heartbeats. Two. Three. Nothing else failed. No collapse. No scream. No second crime. Just a micro-tear in the world's makeup, corrected before most people would have had language for it.

Fatigue, she thought. Bandwidth load. The kind of thing that happened when a city worked too hard to be beautiful.

She did not call Chen. Did not flag a report. Did not open the cases in her bag on the public deck and ruin the afternoon she had been given. She filed the moment the way she filed half the things she noticed—in the notebook later, if it still mattered; in her body now, as a small cold point behind the scar.

She blinked. The overlay held. The promenade below still looked like a gift.

Mara kept walking.

* * *

Home arrived as evening light the Veil made forgiving.

Her apartment was smaller than Hush Spire and less curated than Noah's tier—CIB pay, honest walls, a window that showed the solved skyline without improving it. She liked that about the glass. Some surfaces should stay rude.

She set the scarf on the chair. Made coffee she didn't need. Sat at the kitchen table where her father would have kept a ledger and opened the tablet.

**8841-A.** Alina Ward. Platinum wrong-shape. Eyes that tracked.

**8847-C.** Renata Solis. Gold guest. Owner elsewhere. Same law intact on paper.

Pattern request still in draft. Crane cross-ref inconclusive. Mercer's timestamps still whispering sanitize-before-flag without becoming theory. The hum at the edge of hearing from Meridian had not followed her inside—or it had, and the apartment filters were better at lying.

She read until her eyes burned, not because the files had changed, but because open was how she kept the world from closing cases in her absence.

The flicker did not get a paragraph in the notebook. She thought about adding it. Dismissed the thought the way you dismissed a wrong weather ping when the sky still looked fine.

Outside, New Manhattan performed correctly—lit tiers, soft transit, strangers smiling at overlays that made their lives easier to live inside. An empty chair somewhere.

Mara had twenty-four hours where nobody died on her shift.

She used six of them to remember the city could feel kind.

The cases stayed open on the table glow. She did not close them before sleep.

The efficient dream continued—for everyone who had not spent their month inside rooms where the record clarified nothing—and somewhere beyond her window, in a place without smart systems and without performance, a graph was about to gain a third peak she would not be told about yet.

She saved the files. Left the tabs active. Turned off the lamp.

The city kept its beauty on through the night.
