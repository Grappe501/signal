# Chapter 1 — Scene 1.3: Eyes That Track

**Status:** Layer 4 — deep polish (legendary pass)  
**POV:** Mara Voss · tight third · past tense  
**Beats:** 17–25 · ~1,000 words  
**Cross-reference:** `Outline/Chapter_Sequence_Maps/001_chapter_01_beat_sheet.md`

**0656** by the time she crossed the last clean line.

She cleared the armrest with two fingers—continuity check, not contact—and spoke for the record, because naming the room was the only armor she had left. The latex at her knuckles was real. The scar above her brow pulled when she swallowed. Small proofs that she was still the unedited thing in the room.

"Approaching registered Second." Her voice sounded too ordinary in all that tuned silence. "Crane unit. Julian Crane. Non-contact observation."

The passive sync readout hovered at the edge of her visor, green and polite—a signal that said *nominal* in a room that wasn't. She did not plug in. She did not override. She watched.

Then his eyes moved.

The city noise behind the glass seemed to drop away, as if the building had finally found the one thing it couldn't correct.

The irises found her without the rest of the face agreeing to participate—no preparatory blink, no muscle work in the jaw, no tell in the brow. The pupils settled on her with an accuracy that felt intimate and obscene: two points of living attention in a head the building still classified as idle. Not a flicker. Not the slow rotation of a servo's idea of curiosity. They tracked to her the way a person's eyes tracked when looking was the only language left.

*Idle. No response.* The Steward had already said it. The eyes had not been consulted.

Mara went still.

Hardware idle did not look at you.

She had been trained not to hold eye contact with an active Second without owner confirmation. She had been trained not to anthropomorphize shell behavior. She had been trained to document and move on.

She did not move on.

*Someone is in there,* she thought, and hated the thought because it was not a category the file accepted yet.

She had been trained to break contact first. She did not.

"Mara?" Chen's voice was thin in her ear, distant as another city.

Mara did not answer. Her pulse was the only sound the room had agreed to carry. She dug her thumb into the scuffed edge of her scanner holster until the ache was something she could trust.

The Steward stayed silent. It had no language for this—only the green readout at her visor edge, still claiming nominal.

She counted three seconds she had never been taught to count. One. Two. Three. The eyes did not blink. The chest did not rise. The face did not change. Only the gaze, locked on her with a patience that felt wrong in a way violence would have been simpler.

A fourth second she did not say aloud.

Still watching. Still no breath. Still idle on every screen that mattered except the one in front of her.

She shifted left.

The eyes followed.

Not reflex. Attention—the trapped kind, the kind that couldn't reach for anything except what it could see.

She walked the width of the living area—protocol, not retreat—and the eyes tracked her all the way, as if the rest of the body had been edited out and only the looking allowed to remain. In the hall, the liaison Second held its welcome at nothing—eyes fixed on the entry angle, obedient and empty.

Chen spoke again—*"Status"*—and the pupils ticked a fraction toward the sound, then back to Mara, as if choosing. The liaison did not turn its head.

"Hold," Mara said.

Not to Chen. To herself.

She stepped back toward the hall, breaking the line of sight. The apartment was too bright, too clean, too open—visibility with no one left to witness. In the primary suite, Alina Ward was still dead. In the chair, Julian Crane's legal duplicate watched her leave.

Mara returned.

She hated that the decision felt like loyalty to something she couldn't name—that the improved version in the chair could pull her back the way it once pulled her father.

One more line for a record she already did not believe.

"Subject Second," she said. "Nonresponsive motor function. Ocular tracking active. Request owner verification."

She looked for the small tells. Simulated breath at the nostril. The subtle work of a chest pretending to live. A tear caught and held for effect. Seconds were good at bodies. That was the point.

Nothing moved. The nostrils stayed dry. The chest refused the fiction of breath. The face held its arrangement of patience while the eyes alone insisted otherwise—as if consciousness had been routed to the one channel the shell could not fake closed.

The hold went on long enough that she noticed herself noticing.

Screaming would have been honest.

She already had both versions in memory—the tired signal on the wall, the polished one in the chair. She did not look at the display again.

*Which version would you choose?*

She shut the question down. Not her case to feel.

Mara broke eye contact on purpose. Procedure was a kind of mercy. Her hands steadied on the field kit's scuffed latch—the same habit as her coffee mug, morning after morning—and she opened it on the side table, putting the gray CIB case between her body and the chair, and pinged the retinal chain request.

"Owner chain coming," Chen said. "Don't let the building spin this as hardware."

The kit fan whispered. The city sound tried to return through the glass, faint and indifferent, and failed to matter. A boot chime confirmed the query: **Julian Crane — biological retinal chain — window 0345–0430.**

In her peripheral vision, Crane-Second's eyes stayed on her—sharp enough to split the frame, soft enough to pretend she wasn't looking.

*Stop noticing,* Mara told herself. *Prove it.*

She fixed her attention on the screen and let the room hold what the room was holding.
