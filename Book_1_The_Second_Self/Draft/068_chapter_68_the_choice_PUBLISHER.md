# Chapter 68

The table caught the morning the way it always had—gold on the laminate, the cheap kind that didn't pretend to be stone—but the wall tile did not agree with the room.

**CONVERGENCE ADVISORY — STAY CALM**

Beneath it, smaller type the city used when it wanted parents to keep sending children to pods: *Localized sync variance · infrastructure responding · no action required for residents.*

No action required. The phrase arrived pre-chewed, like **contained** had in Week one.

Month Five. Week four. Through the window, New Manhattan kept its lanes open because lanes were what the city did when it could not keep its **promises**. Pods drifted toward docking slots. Kitchens woke on other tiers. Recovery kept playing on feeds as if recovery were a genre.

Noah poured coffee into the mug with the chip at the rim and listened to the Second dock hum in its alcove down the hall—sync tone polite as a door held for you, LED strip pulsing along the cabinet edge. Link strength **nominal**, the overlay insisted, while the tile insisted something else.

He had not slept through the night. Not since Week two, when a child's question had learned to sit in the kitchen like a second guest. Not since Week three, when denial had stopped feeling like parenting and started feeling like **schedule**.

Iona came in before the toast finished—sleep-mussed, socks wrong, hair fighting the braid that had lost overnight. She did not steal bread first. She stood in the doorway and watched the tile the way she had watched experts in Week one, before she had learned which screens lied politely.

"Dad," she said.

"Morning." He kept his voice level the way adults kept voices level when children were watching for cracks.

"Something happened in the building last night." Not a question. Twelve years old and already fluent in the grammar of *variance* and *responding*. "Pod wall said *stall*. My friend Lena's mom said *hardware*. The tile says *no action*."

Noah flipped the toast too late. Edges blackened past preference. "Building systems glitch sometimes."

"That's what adults say when they're scared."

The line landed—not his locked question from Week one, but close enough to bruise.

"Coffee?" he said, because coffee was a real thing and real things were what he still owned.

Iona sat. Not across from the empty chair. Across from **him**. "I'm not going to pod until you tell me if you're staying."

* * *

The overlay did not wait for breakfast to finish.

**URGENT — TIER PRESSURE CASCADE · SECTOR 14**

Noah's queue opened without permission—allocation maps, valve flags, the boring grids that kept civilization drinking without anyone needing to think about the pipe. His civic score band flickered at the edge of vision: green, then amber, a micro-dip the system explained with the gentle cruelty of math that counted **response time** as virtue.

He could resolve the flag in ninety seconds from the link. He could keep his score stable. He could leave his body at the table the way he had since grief taught him that attention was a currency and Running Double was how the day agreed to start.

The window chair behind him waited anyway—eyes aimed at nothing, jaw ready to chew on schedule, hands loose on a mug like a passenger in his own life.

Iona watched the chair. Then watched him. "You're doing it."

"I'm not—"

"You are." No sermon. Just fact. "You always do when you're afraid."

The sync tone pulsed once, inviting depth. The empty shell's fingers twitched toward the mug Noah's hands already held. Two places. One morning. The bargain that had once felt like relief now felt like **theft** with better branding.

On the tile, a headline refreshed: **AUTHORITIES: MERCER TRANSFER UNDER REVIEW**. Subhead: *No public threat · reassurance bands holding*. Markets liked reassurance. Families were supposed to like it too.

Noah wanted to like it. The want felt physical—shoulders unknotting on command, breath syncing to the tile the way his body had learned to sync to grids. That was the old tax. That was the old **relief**.

Iona's homework tile chimed at the edge of her vision. She ignored it. "Mom used to say together meant in the same room."

"Your mom—" He stopped. Grief had its own nouns and he was tired of borrowing them from the link. "Together means **present**," he said, and heard how late the sentence was.

"Then be present."

Not accusation. Request. The kind children made when they still believed adults could choose.

* * *

The alcove was three steps down the hall and a lifetime from the kitchen.

Second dock: cabinet, strip light, the quiet machinery of a legal Second that had never been illegal—only **convenient**. Noah stood in the doorway and felt the hum in his teeth the way Greenland's researchers felt graphs in their chest. Infrastructure. Miracle. Habit.

His overlay offered one last kindness: *Disconnect will impact civic score · employer queue · HVI stability band. Confirm?*

Stability. The word civilization used when it meant **don't move**.

Noah thought of Week one headlines. Week two aisles he had only heard about in tones on the tile. Week three warnings in transit rooms he would never be cleared to enter. He thought of a daughter who had stopped stealing toast first and started asking if he would **stay**.

He thought of one body, one room, one attention—the nostalgia their world had politely deleted and his daughter had asked about in a kitchen that now demanded an **answer**.

He placed both hands on the dock rim.

Not prayer. Not heroism. **Choice**—sacrifice without ROI, the variable dashboards never learned to count.

"Confirm," he said.

The sync tone wavered. The LED strip stuttered—green, amber, then **dark**. The overlay peeled away like weather lifting, leaving his sight ordinary and slightly too bright. The hum dropped to silence so complete the apartment seemed to hold its breath with him.

Link status: **OFFLINE**.

One channel. One life. The phrase from Month One returned without laughter attached.

Behind him, bare feet on laminate.

Noah turned. Iona stood in the hall shadow, arms wrapped around herself—not afraid of the dock, afraid of the **week**. She looked at his face the way she had looked at the tile: checking for the version that lied politely.

He was there. Fully. No shell in the window chair. No latency between joke and mouth. Just a father with coffee on his breath and toast burned past preference and hands that had chosen **slow**.

She didn't hug him. She didn't need to. She crossed to the table and sat in the chair that had held his absence for years, picked up the blackened toast, and took a bite.

"Better," she said.

Noah laughed—on time for once.

* * *

The morning after was already arriving in the queue he would have to read **manually**—department pings, reroute lag, passwords typed without a duplicate to split the labor. Harder. The tile would keep insisting calm. The score would dip and explain itself in fonts designed to feel like ethics.

Noah washed the pan. One pair of hands. Real water. Real steam.

The dock stayed dark above the cabinet edge—repair tape not yet applied, silence not yet familiar, the slower way work would arrive now that messages could not borrow his face while his attention went elsewhere.

Iona brushed crumbs into her palm and asked, quietly, "Are you staying today?"

"Yes," he said.

She nodded once, believing him—not because the world had earned trust, but because he had finally stopped performing it through a second pair of eyes.

Outside, New Manhattan continued. The Core would hum whether he synced or not.

In this apartment, the link was dead.

That was not triumph.

That was **enough**—for one morning, one table, one child who had asked for presence and received it without a speech.

Harder mornings were coming. Noah knew that the way fathers knew weather now—not from the tile, from the **body**.

He made more toast. Edges deliberately blackened. The kitchen kept its honest noise.

Somewhere beneath the Spine, an investigator walked toward a relay band she had never been cleared for until the hour turned—Second present, cleaner, braver, **easier**—and the city was about to ask the same question in a different grammar.

Noah did not know that yet.

He only knew the dock was dark.

He only knew his daughter was eating.

That was the job.

That was the only honest breakfast left.
