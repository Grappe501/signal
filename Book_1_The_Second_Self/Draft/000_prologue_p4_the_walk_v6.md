# Prologue P.4 — The Walk

One metre.

Regolith gave under the boot—a sensation so honest it made his eyes sting in Suite Seven where nothing had changed and everything had. Individual grains through the sole. Cold climbing the suit's thermal layers. Not pain. Not yet. Information from a world that didn't care if he labelled it. His shadow lengthened on the dust, darker shape moving with him, the only thing on Mars that looked like it belonged in a story with an ending.

He stood in that shadow one beat longer than procedure wanted. Fan hiss. Link tone. His breath. The planet could wait.

Two metres. He stepped around a fist-sized rock because his brain still insisted on courtesy even here. Second print, shallower on the near side where the fines were loose. The link made the balance adjustment feel like a gift and a theft at once. Both could be true.

Three metres. Sky thin black-blue above rust. Horizon curved with a gentleness that made Earth feel like a rumour you could choose to believe in. Beauty without comfort. The kind that didn't offer to keep you. He'd spent his life selling comfort to civilisation. Out here comfort didn't apply.

Four metres. Five.

The ground rose—a gentle slope toward a low ridge that existed for no human reason except to exist. Each step changed the angle of his boot on fines. The link translated the difference without mercy. Prints behind him—a sentence getting longer, fourth depression deeper where his weight settled, fifth angled where the slope began. A trail now. Proof you could read from orbit if you cared to look.

On Earth his legs twitched in the rig again—leak the public feed cut around, honesty the medical team wrote on a chart he'd never read. Mars body stayed composed. Steady breath on the HUD. Hands still at his sides. He noticed that and didn't have language for why it unsettled him yet: the version of him other people got to keep.

Six metres. Suit joints whispered when he moved—distant paper. Every schoolroom on the planet was watching this waveform. Geography had stopped mattering for **attention**. Not for bodies. Not yet. He could feel it in his knees—something already changed, no word for it yet that wasn't marketing.

Seven metres.

Commentary tried to come back—two voices overlapping, then dropping to whispers, then nothing. Scripts built for delay, for buffers, for the safe lie that he was somewhere else watching a feed. There was no feed between him and his footfall. Only now. Dead air where analysis used to live.

He almost said something stupid into the open channel—*Hello, Mars* or *We did it* or some other sentence that would age badly by dinner. He swallowed it. Humanity was the walk, not the caption.

He kept walking because stopping early turned miracle back into marketing.

Eight.

Earth hung in the sky. Bright pinhole. Blue-white. Impossibly small. Room where his blood was still warm. His boot came down clean while his left leg jerked in the rig—a split second the feed would cut later, honesty the medical team would chart. Connection wasn't together. He was learning that the hard way.

Nine metres. Ten.

HUD timer crossed from yellow into red. EVA window complaining. He ignored it the way you ignored boards until you couldn't. Sound thinned again—not deleted this time, just him refusing to listen. Shadow stretched longer downslope, his shape and the mast shadow crossing like bad geometry. Camera mast turned when he turned. He was absurdly aware of the camera angle. Still a geek. Still him. Good.

On Earth his legs twitched again at the red timer. Mars body didn't flinch. Steady breath. Composed face inside the helmet. The version they got to keep.

Eleven metres. The ridge was closer now—nothing heroic about it, just enough elevation to make the module look smaller behind him. Regolith colour shifted where wind had scoured a harder pan, paler stripe across rust. His boot left a shallower print there. Detail a drone would have smoothed out. He let it stay.

Twelve metres. Fan hiss louder in his ears—or maybe the silence on Earth had gotten heavier. In Suite Seven someone adjusted a strap he couldn't see. On Mars nothing adjusted. Suit telemetry in the lower corner of his HUD—oxygen green, pressure green, link green. Everything stable. Everything wrong.

Thirteen.

He stopped—not because the voice told him to, because his body asked. Not fear. Not procedure. Animal request to stand still in a place no animal had ever stood with this kind of attention.

He looked back.

Farshore had shrunk to a white toy against rust—landing struts, cable veins, airlock he hadn't bothered to return to. The print trail between them looked fragile. Human. The kind of thing that would become iconography before anyone agreed what it meant.

Earth still hung in the sky. Smaller from here. He couldn't love it properly from this distance. Couldn't hate it either. Planet that made him. Planet currently betting against him.

Wind didn't come to fill the silence. Almost no air to carry it. That emptiness was the point.

In Suite Seven the medical team watched his vitals climb and flatten—a separate drama no camera cared about.
