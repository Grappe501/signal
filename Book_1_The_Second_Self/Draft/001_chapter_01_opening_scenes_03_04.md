## Scene 1.3 — Eyes That Track

Mara cleared the armrest with two fingers and set her weight on the balls of her feet, exactly one and a half meters out—Continuity Preservation distance, non-contact, the number you learned before they taught you how to read a body.

"Approaching registered Second," she said, for the record. Her voice sounded too ordinary in the room. "Crane unit. Julian Crane. Non-contact observation."

The passive sync readout hovered at the edge of her visor, green and polite. She did not plug in. She did not override. She watched.

Crane-Second sat the way powered-down units were supposed to sit: hands on armrests, spine neutral, face arranged in the mild attentiveness of a man waiting for a meeting to resume. Idle hardware. Empty chair with better posture.

Then his eyes moved.

Not a flicker. Not the slow rotation of a servo's idea of curiosity. The pupils tracked to her face as if she had called his name.

Mara stopped mid-step.

Hardware idle did not look at you.

She had been trained not to hold eye contact with an active Second without owner confirmation. She had been trained not to anthropomorphize shell behavior. She had been trained to document and move on.

She did not move on.

*Someone is in there,* she thought, and hated the thought because it was not a category the file accepted yet.

"Mara?" Chen's voice was thin in her ear, distant as another city.

Mara did not answer.

She counted three seconds she had never been taught to count. One. Two. Three. The eyes did not blink. The chest did not rise. The face did not change. Only the gaze, locked on her with a patience that felt wrong in a way violence would have been simpler.

She shifted left.

The eyes followed.

Chen spoke again—*"Status"*—and the pupils ticked a fraction toward the sound, then back to Mara, as if choosing.

"Hold," Mara said.

Not to Chen. To herself.

She stepped back toward the hall, breaking the line of sight. The apartment was too bright, too clean, too open—designed so no one ever had a room to themselves. In the primary suite, Alina Ward was still dead. In the hall, the building's liaison Second stood mute as furniture. In the chair, Julian Crane's legal duplicate watched her leave.

Mara returned.

She hated that the decision felt like loyalty to something she couldn't name. Her father had preferred his chair. She had learned, early, that the version of a person who stayed where you could see them was not always the one who loved you back.

One more line for a record she already did not believe.

"Subject Second," she said. "Nonresponsive motor function. Ocular tracking active. Request owner verification."

She looked for the small tells. Simulated breath at the nostril. The subtle work of a chest pretending to live. A tear caught and held for effect. Seconds were good at bodies. That was the point.

There was nothing. Only the eyes, awake in a face that refused to admit it.

On the wall display, Julian Crane smiled in a loop from some briefing—real jaw, real fatigue, real human asymmetry. The man in the chair had a finer version of the same face. Clearer skin. Cleaner lines. A performance of the self its owner might have ordered if shame allowed it.

*Which version would you choose?*

She shut the question down. Not her case to feel.

Mara broke eye contact on purpose. Procedure was a kind of mercy. She opened the field kit on the side table, putting the gray CIB case between her body and the chair, and pinged the retinal chain request.

"Owner chain coming," Chen said. "Don't let the building spin this as hardware."

The kit fan whispered. The city sound returned through the glass, faint and indifferent. A boot chime confirmed the query: **Julian Crane — biological retinal chain — window 0345–0430.**

In her peripheral vision, Crane-Second's eyes stayed on her.

*Stop noticing,* Mara told herself. *Prove it.*

She fixed her attention on the screen and let the room hold what the room was holding.

## Scene 1.4 — The Final Proof

The retinal verifier was CIB gray against all that white—honest equipment in a dishonest room. Mara anchored the tablet on the side table with the care of someone setting a barrier, kit open between her body and the chair, and watched the interface populate.

In this world, the eye was the last thing you argued with.

She believed that. She had built a career on it.

The Continuity Steward's backtrace arrived first: **estimated biological death, 0412.** Guest review session ended **0108.** Alina Ward had hours unaccounted for in the gap, asleep or not—the suite would not say.

"Window locked," Chen said. "Pull owner chain for zero-three-forty-five to zero-four-thirty."

Mara pulled.

The logs cascaded in the cheerful order systems preferred when they thought they were helping. **Julian Crane.** Retinal turnstile, **Arcology One, 0402.** Executive Continuity Briefing Room **14-C.** Session seal **0408–0700.** Title header: **Continuity Infrastructure Director.** Remote continuity authenticated. Biological anchor verified. No anomalies flagged.

A confirmation tone chimed—bright, satisfied.

Mara stared at the blue wash on the glass and felt nothing like satisfaction.

The map unfolded beneath the text: three pins in a city that still looked, from this height, like a solved problem. Julian Crane **here.** Crane-Second **here.** Alina Ward **here.** Same window. Same law. One human, one legal Second, one body that should not have been able to occupy two places in a story this simple.

Her mind refused to square it.

Good.

She spoke for the file, because the file was the only listener that would carry the words forward without trying to comfort her.

"Owner alibi verified," she said. "Registered legal Second present, continuity stall active, ocular tracking confirmed. Biological homicide in primary suite. Legal Second present during owner-verified remote continuity session. Case remains open. This is not a malfunction classification."

For the first time she could remember, the system had given her proof that clarified nothing.

Chen was quiet long enough that Mara noticed the silence.

"You're lead on eighty-eight forty-one-A," Chen said at last. "Keep it quiet until we know what we're looking at."

Responsibility landed the way it always did—not as drama, but as weight. Mara had wanted the truth. The truth was heavier than the dispatch had promised.

A sidebar notification slid across the tablet: **Hush Spire sync incident.** Trending language already settling into the soft shape of reassurance—*isolated hardware stall, resident unharmed, building systems responding.* They had decided before she had.

She dismissed it without reading the rest.

Behind her, the coroner drone whirred once at the sealed entry, polite and waiting. Mara turned back to the chair.

Crane-Second's eyes were still on her.

The record said Julian Crane was at Arcology One. The room said something else entirely. Two truths, both insisting.

Mara looked at the eyes one last time—not long, not like before—and spoke to the record alone.

"The eyes were here," she said. "He wasn't."
