# Chapter 1 — Scene 1.2: The Perfect Room

The biometric seal accepted her CIB override without hesitation. A wall panel lit in the entry hall—too bright, too courteous, light with no source she could name. **0652** sat in the corner in polite precision, as if lateness were a moral failure.

Somewhere beyond the seal, a coroner drone waited with the patience of a scheduled service.

"Welcome, Investigator," the Continuity Steward said, ambient and calm, as if nothing in this building could happen off-script. "Continuity preservation mode active."

The UI scrolled its courtesy: **Owner: Julian Crane.** **Guest: Alina Ward**, overnight · compliance counsel. Compliance review session logged **11:14 PM.** Respectable names. Names from filings, not from her life. Julian Crane—the kind of owner whose name on a filing made buildings behave.

Mara stepped past the panel into a living area that had perfected itself before she arrived.

Platinum didn't decorate. It curated. Panoramic glass held the city at arm's length—dawn over a skyline that still looked solved from this height, the glass too clear, the climate whispering at a volume designed to disappear. Nothing had been left to chance. Even the silence had been tuned.

In a UI corner, smaller and brighter than it needed to be: **auto-sanitize complete, 0638.** Trace recovery partial. Expected feature. Expected loss. She had seen the flag a hundred times in Platinum units and never once seen it hide a murder. That was the point of Platinum. The morning had been corrected before she'd been invited to see it.

In the short hall, every door stood open by design—remote presence architecture, the unit's way of refusing privacy. Togetherness as visibility. Primary suite ahead, darker. In the living area, a designer chair faced the glass; she kept it in her peripheral vision as she moved. It did not shift. It did not resettle. It did not do any of the small things a body did when a room changed around it.

The fabric of her jacket whispered against itself when she moved. Too loud.

Mara went to the suite first. Procedure before instinct.

Alina Ward was forty-one in the guest registry and forty-one forever in the bed: sleepwear, the posture of sleep made obscene by a single decisive injury at the temple—cranial compression, minimal blood, the neat violence of strength applied without mess. No struggle. No overturned furniture. On the nightstand, closed beside the polished charging dock, a paper notebook—real paper, pen tucked in the spine, ink that couldn't be autosaved. Mara looked at it longer than she should have. Rare enough to mean something, or nothing.

She muted her comms with a touch. Exhaled once. The sound was the loudest thing in the suite.

Back at the living threshold, she kept her voice level—bedroom dark behind her, living bright ahead, glass everywhere between.

"Biological deceased," she said. "Primary suite. Guest Alina Ward. I need coroner release and owner location."

"Owner?" Chen asked.

"Julian Crane. Second visible in living area. Haven't approached."

She did not look at the chair until she had said it plain. Shock was a luxury that contaminated rooms.

When she looked, the room gave her both versions at once. On the wall display, Julian Crane smiled through a briefing loop—real fatigue, real asymmetry, the human signal breaking through the feed. In the chair, Crane-Second held the powered-down pose: hands on armrests, spine neutral, face arranged for patience—and a finer version of the same face. Clearer skin. Better lines. A self improved by permission.

Mara stopped herself there. Her father had preferred the improved version too.

In the hall, the building liaison Second remained where it had been since the lobby—same muted courtesy, same empty welcome. It did not cross the threshold. The Steward spoke as if the unit required no human witness at all.

"Second continuity stall exceeds threshold. Owner biological status: remote verified."

Witness without agency. Mirror without help.

Mara pulled gloves on. Latex snapped in the hush like something alive refusing to stay quiet. She holstered her scanner, cleared nothing yet, and took her place one and a half meters from the chair—Continuity Preservation distance, non-contact, the last clean line before the room changed categories again. Through the glass behind him, the city made its distant, uncorrected noise. In the chair, nothing—no breath, no shift, no response from a body the Steward still called idle.

The figure did not move.

Not yet.
