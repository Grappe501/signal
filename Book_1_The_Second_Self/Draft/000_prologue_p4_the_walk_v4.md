One metre.

Regolith gave under the boot—a sensation so honest it made his eyes sting in Suite Seven where nothing had changed and everything had. Individual grains through the sole. Cold climbing the suit's thermal layers. Not pain. Not yet. Information from a world that didn't care if he labelled it. His shadow lengthened on the dust, darker shape moving with him, the only thing on Mars that looked like it belonged in a story with an ending.

Two metres. He stepped around a fist-sized rock because his brain still insisted on courtesy even here. The link made the balance adjustment feel like a gift and a theft at once. Fine. Both could be true.

Three metres. Sky thin black-blue above rust. Horizon curved with a gentleness that made Earth feel like a rumour you could choose to believe in. Beauty without comfort. The kind that didn't offer to keep you. He'd spent his life selling comfort to civilisation. Out here comfort was a category error.

Four metres. Five.

On Earth his legs twitched in the rig again—leak the public feed cut around, honesty the medical team wrote on a chart he'd never read. Mars body stayed composed. Steady breath on the HUD. Hands still at his sides. He noticed that and didn't have language for why it unsettled him yet: the version of him other people got to keep.

Six metres. Suit joints whispered when he moved—distant paper. Look, every schoolroom on Earth was watching this waveform, and what it meant—if you thought about it from first principles—was that geography had stopped mattering for **attention**. Not for bodies. Not yet. But for the thing humans had always assumed had to stay in one skull. The last two years of frantic launch—early, again, before the balance sheets killed the future—had been leading here. Not a stunt. A phase transition. The kind you only named after you'd already fallen through it.

Seven metres. Commentators tried to speak on the open channel and failed. Scripts built for delay, for buffers, for the safe lie that he was somewhere else watching a feed. There was no feed between him and his footfall. Only now.

He kept walking because stopping early turned miracle back into marketing.

Eight metres. Nine. Ten.

HUD timer crossed into red. EVA window complaining. He ignored it the way you ignored boards until you couldn't. Boots left prints behind him—a sentence getting longer, each depression slightly different where the ground changed and the link translated the difference without mercy. Gentle rise toward a low ridge that existed for no human reason except to exist. Camera mast turned when he turned. He was absurdly aware of his head angle as a compositional problem. Still a geek. Still him. Good.

Eleven metres. Twelve.

Thirteen.

He stopped—not because the voice told him to, because his body asked. Not fear. Not procedure. Animal request to stand still in a place no animal had ever stood with this kind of attention.

Earth hung in the sky. Bright pinhole. Blue-white. Impossibly small. Room where his blood was still warm. He couldn't love it properly from here. Couldn't hate it either. Planet that made him. Planet currently betting against him. Fair.

Wind didn't come to fill the silence. Almost no air to carry it. That emptiness was the point.

In Suite Seven the medical team watched his vitals climb and flatten—a separate drama no camera cared about.
