On Mars—already, impossibly, already—his Second stood in Farshore prep corridor C with gloved hands on the airlock frame. Narrow corridor, intentional: hex lighting cool blue along the bulkhead, dust-free deck plating with traction hexes embedded, Continuity logo above the cycle panel. Smile without a mouth. Corporate.

Through the link he saw his Second's arms as if they were his own. Matte white suit. Relay insignia on the shoulder. Slight forward lean of a man who couldn't stand still even when standing still. Polymer under his palms—the same instant bite the straps gave his wrists in Suite Seven. No lag. No ghost of delay. No polite allowance for the speed of light.

His stomach turned. The rock slipped in his palm on Earth. His Second's hands did not slip.

Ten.

Nine.

Eight.

Integers dry in his ear. No music. No anthem. Prestige thriller, not a launch video. On the monitor beside the rig, two breath waveforms leaned toward each other like they wanted to merge. His chest rose in Nevada. His Second's chest rose in the corridor. Same rhythm. Same count. Two HUD timers he wasn't supposed to read at once and could.

Seven.

Six.

Sync bar filling green. Clean. Offensive. His body braced for the old failure—the half-second stutter, mind arriving after the foot, proof that awareness belonged in one skull and one set of bones. Every prior test had taught his nervous system to expect delay. Delay was how reality apologised for distance.

Five.

Four.

Three.

Two.

One.

Zero.

Mars was here.

Pressure in the boots on hex deck plating. Weight in the knees. Fan whisper in a suit that wasn't his biological suit but was, impossibly, him. In Suite Seven his ankles flexed against the straps like he'd taken a step on another planet while his body stayed horizontal on Earth. Someone in medical swore softly off-feed. Good. Physics ought to swear back.

For one fractured instant he couldn't tell which floor he was on. Rig tilted. Corridor tilted. Both down. Copper taste—stress breaking loose, scatter threatening order. He reached for a joke. Dumb. Human. The joke died like it had seen the view.

On Mars, his Second did not stumble. Composed. Core calm. The version the link thought cameras deserved. That would bother him later. Not now.

"Latency?" the control lead asked. Too careful.

"Zero," someone said. Again, like repetition made it safer. "Zero point zero."

Not latency. **Correlation lock.** The board said zero because the instruments measured the wrong metaphor—round-trip packet time for something that had never been packets. Adrian almost laughed—the helpless kind—and swallowed it.

On the monitor beside the rig, the control room went still. Engineers at consoles. One lead pulling her headset off like it was broken. Latency column a vertical line of zeros where classical networking wasn't supposed to allow zeros. Somewhere a physicist who'd called him a fraud in public was watching the same number and developing a crack in his worldview. Fine. Join the queue.

Walking was the only thing that still made sense. One awareness. Two places. **4.8 billion** watching. Alone.

Each bootfall on the hex deck—small sound, obscene fidelity through the link. When he reached the airlock his Second's gloved fingers hit the cycle panel from muscle memory. Earth fingers twitched in the straps. Puppet echo. He'd pretend not to notice later.

The inner door cycled. Amber light through the visor. Three seconds of pump vibration in the boots. Green blink.

Exterior door still closed. Red dust waiting on the other side.
