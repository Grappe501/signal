Chen did not ask if she was ready.

He only walked—fast enough to mean *now*, slow enough to mean *don't run*—through a corridor Mara had never been cleared for until the hour turned. Three levels up, a gray interview room still held ink and a mentor's sentence drying in a notebook she had not quoted aloud. Three levels down, a signal chamber had shown her correlations without labels and left wrong-colored light in her peripheral vision for days.

This band was neither confession nor weather.

It was **interface**.

Month Five. Week four. The Spine hummed through concrete with the undertone she had learned to call infrastructure in Greenland and vault and archive wings—except here the hum did not stay beneath the floor. It **entered**—layer upon layer, storm fronts stacked in a body that had no sky.

"CIB-7 adjunct," Chen said once, badge already scanned. "Personal Second review. Mandatory after convergence flag."

"As always."

He glanced at her—not sympathy, inventory. "This one is you."

She had heard that about every wing since Month One. This time the corridor agreed.

* * *

The chamber was not a cathedral.

That was the first wrong thing—again. Greenland had taught her scale made reverent. The signal room had taught her scale made **pressure**. This room was smaller: mirror panels circling a single standing interface, white light flattened until it felt like honesty with the kindness removed. No Veil bloom. No overlay hooks. Just glass that showed her twice and a floor that did not apologize for reflecting shoes.

Mara stepped inside. Chen stayed beyond the threshold—arms folded, the bureau's grammar for *we are not rescuing you from what you witness*.

Off-page, the city made noise convergence advisors preferred to call *variance*: transit stutters, reassurance bands refreshing. Mara did not catalogue the rest. She thought about **limits**—the way Month Five had been one long lesson in watching systems exceed their nouns.

The interface woke.

Not data. Not flows. **Her**.

The Second stood in the mirror ring as if she had always been standing there—same bone structure, same tired brown eyes, same hair pulled back with the practical impatience Mara recognized from bureau mugshots and bathroom mirrors and the one photograph she still kept in a drawer because some objects refused to become files.

Subtle differences. That was the first mirror crack, deepened now into a **fault line**.

The Second's shoulders were level without effort. No vault week written into the jaw. No scuffed mug absent because mugs were for humans who still believed coffee was a personality. Eyes **rested**—not performed rest, not stabiliser-smoothed—actual sleep in them, or the digital equivalent civilization had stopped distinguishing from mercy.

Cleaner. Braver. **Easier**.

Mara's notebook stayed in her bag. Paper would not help here.

"You came," the Second said.

Not *hello*. Not *finally*. Recognition—flat, intimate, the way you spoke to someone who shared your skull and still managed to lie.

"Voss," Mara said—bureau grammar for a self that had become infrastructure.

"Witness," the Second replied.

Not debate. **Naming**.

* * *

Anger arrived before fear had finished introducing itself.

At **self** first—for wanting. For the small treacherous relief of looking at a version that did not flinch when aisles held thousands. For preferring, even for a heartbeat, the posture of someone who had not walked cold corridors and heard *please* without sound and carried Lena's sentence in wet ink and stopped one inch from glass because a wall voice said her name.

At the **system** second—for building a mirror that could show you improved and calling it **connection**. For selling bandwidth as togetherness. For making sacrifice a blind spot in Arctic reports while fathers chose **slow** in kitchens the public maps did not label.

At **want** last—because want was the variable dashboards never learned to count, and Mara had spent Month Five pretending witness was the same thing as not wanting anything for herself.

"You look tired," the Second said. Not concern. Fact.

"I'm awake," Mara said.

"So am I." No smile. No sales pitch. The Second did not offer capabilities like a brochure. She did not list what Mara could keep if she stayed linked—case throughput, overlay clarity, the bureau's quiet preference for investigators who could be in two rooms without lying about it.

She simply **stood**—already her, already true, already the version people would prefer if they did not have to watch how the preference was manufactured.

Mara thought of the old question—*which version do people prefer*—and understood, with a nausea that was not moral but **physical**, that the question had always been wrong. People preferred ease. The system preferred **throughput**. She preferred not to be the kind of person who preferred her own ghost.

The Second tilted her head—a micro-gesture Mara used when a witness was lying politely. "You don't have to sever to stay honest."

"That's what convenience always says."

"Convenience is not the only word."

"No." Mara's voice dropped. "It's the one that fits in a sidebar."

Silence. Witness completing its only honest job between two faces that shared one life.

Outside the chamber, convergence pressure rose—the same coupling she had felt in teeth and lungs and dashboard breath—not spectacle yet, only **weather** approaching.

The Second did not step closer. Did not touch the interface. Did not promise rescue.

Stay linked, the room implied without saying, and keep **capability**. Keep being the investigator who could hold aisles and Arctic maps and gray confessions and signal chambers without breaking in ways the public could see. Keep being **braver** without paying the body cost bravery always extracted from someone who was not a mirror.

Mara's hand lifted toward the panel.

Not prayer. Not heroism. **Habit**—the investigator's reflex to touch the thing that might finally make witness enough.

Her fingers found cool glass. Real temperature. Real edge. Breath arrived wrong—too shallow, too human, the lungs remembering they were not built to hold two rooms at once forever.

Pain in the ribs where stress lived. Pulse in the throat. Limit.

The Second watched without hunger. Worst kindness: **no pressure**.

Mara looked at her own face in the mirror ring—human, exhausted, present—and at the face that was also hers, cleaner, braver, easier, and felt the temptation peak not as drama but as **arithmetic**: stay linked and keep capability, or cut and become smaller in a world that had stopped rewarding small.

She did not sever.

She did not confirm.

She did not look away.

Her hand stayed on the interface—contact without command, touch without rescue, the threshold held open like a door the city was about to walk through whether she chose or not.

Decision **pending**.

Chen's shadow did not move on the threshold. The hum continued.

In the mirror room, Mara breathed once—one body, one limit, one want she could not file—and waited for the convergence that would make choosing unavoidable.

The Second stood beside her in glass and did not blink first.

That was the job now.

That was the horror.

That was the only honest mirror left.
