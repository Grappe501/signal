Chen did not ask if she was ready.

He only walked—fast enough to mean *now*, slow enough to mean *don't run*—through a corridor Mara had never been cleared for until the hour turned. Three levels up, the gray interview room still held language and ink—a door that had closed on a woman who had finally stopped dressing defaults as accidents.

Down here, language failed earlier.

The air changed first—not temperature, **density**. Each breath arrived with weight, as if the lungs had to negotiate with the room for permission. Mara's skin prickled along her forearms. The hum she had learned to call infrastructure in Greenland and vault and archive wings did not rise in volume so much as **layer**—undertone upon undertone, storm fronts stacked in a sky that had no sky.

Signal as weather. The phrase arrived uninvited and she refused to give it a file number.

Month Five. Week three. The notebook in her bag still held Lena's sentence in wet ink. Her mouth still held the taste of gray air and honest architecture. None of that belonged on this floor, and the floor knew it.

"CIB-7 sub-band," Chen said once, badge already scanned. "Observer only. Non-intervention holds."

"As always."

He glanced at her—not sympathy, inventory. "This one is different."

She had heard that about every wing since Month One. This time the corridor agreed.

* * *

The descent was not dramatic. Institutions had learned drama was leak risk.

Only **depth**: pressure doors, clearance tiers, a lift that dropped without announcing floor numbers, then a final walk where the walls lost paint in favor of bare composite and the lighting shifted from institutional white to something flatter, as if the light had been tired for years.

At the end: a hatch marked with symbols Mara could read and symbols she could not—Relay engineering on one side, bureau classification on the other, and between them the universal icon for *do not touch unless your clearance can survive the audit*.

Chen badged. The hatch sighed open.

The signal chamber was not a cathedral.

That was the first wrong thing.

Greenland had taught her to expect scale made reverent—vertical displays, dependency graphs, the hum of a world held in place by machines that did not need worshippers. This room was **small**. A single curved console. A dome overhead darkened to void. A floor that vibrated at a frequency felt in teeth more than heard.

And in the center, a dashboard that looked almost modest until the data moved.

"Thirty-second observer window," the tech said—voice filtered through a mask, eyes on a screen Mara wasn't allowed to see. "Then we pull you. Non-contact."

Mara stepped to the rail. Chen stayed by the hatch, arms folded, the bureau's grammar for *we are not rescuing you from what you witness*.

The dashboard woke.

Not countries. Not flows. Not Still One cohorts or Dubai recovery bands. **Correlations**.

Lines connecting nodes that should not share a clock.

A preservation intake spike in São Paulo aligned, to the millisecond, with a sync dropout in a tutor shell Mara had walked past in aisle seven—no shared owner, no shared district, no shared anything except the **shape** of the event on the graph.

A Greenland load band pulsed amber in rhythm with a biometric trace tagged **CONTINUITY ARCHIVE — SUITE 3**—Vale's wing, hours ago, a body in a bed she could still see when she blinked.

A child's cadence report from a kitchen in a city she had never visited sat nested inside a relay echo pattern from **FARSHORE PREP — DAY 0**, seventeen years dead in every brochure and still breathing in the data like a fossil that refused to fossilize.

No labels explained the cluster. No menu offered *cause*. Only correlation—clean, cruel, **impossible**—the way weather maps showed fronts without asking permission to mean anything.

Mara's stomach turned.

Not fear exactly. **Recognition** without referent—the body knowing before the mind secured a warrant.

Déjà vu slid across her skin: fluorescent archive light, a folder marked personal, handwriting that should not have mattered and had. A question she had dismissed because dismissal was the job. *What if consciousness is not—*

She did not finish the thought. Finishing would have been a kind of answer.

The chamber **changed**.

Or she did.

The hum tightened. The void dome above the console flickered—not image, not text, only **pressure**, as if something vast had turned its attention toward the room and the room had held its breath. Mara's pulse counted in her ears. The dashboard correlations **breathed** with it—inhale, hold, inhale—systems coupling without announcement: **schedule**, metrics nobody had voted for.

For one instant Mara was in two places.

Not literally—she would not allow herself the word *literally* on a bureau floor. But the split was **felt**: gray interview ink on one hand, Arctic map room on the other, aisle seven frost on her tongue, Vale's lagging breath in a maintenance wing, Dubai amber on a graph she had never touched. All of it **aligned**, the way the dashboard said, without telling her **why**.

Supernatural.

Technology.

The same beat.

Her hand lifted toward the interface—toward the cluster with no label, toward the place the graph implied a question lived if you were stupid enough to ask it in a room built to receive and never explain.

Chen's voice, flat: "Voss."

She stopped.

Finger an inch from glass. Close enough to feel the static lift the fine hairs on her skin. Not close enough to become evidence she couldn't carry.

The tech said, "Window closing."

The dashboard did not resolve. It did not collapse into sense. It simply **continued**—weather on a screen, correlations without confession—and then the display dimmed as if the room had decided witness was complete.

Mara's hand dropped.

She did not know what she had almost touched.

* * *

They pulled her out on schedule.

The hatch sealed behind them with the same institutional finality as Lena's containment door—a rhyme Mara did not want. Her legs held. Her notebook stayed in her bag. Her mouth stayed closed because every word she reached for sounded like either madness or theology, and the bureau had forms for neither.

Chen waited until the lift rose two nameless floors.

"What did you see?" he asked.

Mara looked at him. Professional face still in place. Eyes that had learned a new definition of *awake* now carrying something she could not file.

"Correlations," she said.

"That's not an answer."

"It's the only honest one I have."

Chen held her gaze a beat longer than protocol required, then nodded once—the bureau's way of accepting a dead end without calling it one.

They walked the return corridor in silence.

The lighting was wrong.

Not broken—**wrong**. White panels with a faint amber undertone, a spectrum shift subtle enough to blame on exhaustion. The wrongness followed them around a corner and did not resolve when she blinked hard.

Somewhere above, Dubai pretended recovery. Still Ones counted. Greenland hummed. LATTICE routes synchronized what must stay synchronized, and in a small room beneath the Spine, a graph had shown her the world **coupling** without permission.

Convergence was not coming.

It was **already** here—felt in body, seen for thirty seconds, impossible to explain to anyone who had not stood in the chamber and watched weather become data become **pressure**.

Mara badged out without looking back.

She did not pray.

She did not dismiss.

She carried the wrong-colored light in her peripheral vision all the way to the surface, where the city waited in optimized hush, unaware in the way cities were always unaware, assuming the Core would hold because the Core had always held.

She had seen the map without labels.

She had no language for what it meant.

That was the job now.

That was the horror.

That was the only honest briefing she would get.
