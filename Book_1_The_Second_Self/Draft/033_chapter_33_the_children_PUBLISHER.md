# Chapter 33

The school did not know how to be honest yet.



Month Three, week eleven—the same month the overlay stuttered and adults called it weather and children learned which teachers still smiled too fast when a hallway made a real sound. The School of Everything had not collapsed. It had **friction** now, the way a polished floor had friction when someone spilled water and didn't wipe it before the next class: not dangerous, just awkward, everyone pretending they hadn't noticed.



Iona noticed.



She noticed the way she noticed mittens left under benches and sentences that didn't adapt—by storing first and naming later. The atrium still tried to show you things before you asked. Watershed maps still glowed under shoes. But the glow had a new footer in small type adults read and children read faster: **PERSONAL LAYER · OPTIONAL · ASSISTANCE NOT REQUIRED FOR ATTENDANCE.** Some kids wore the optional badge like a team jersey. Some wore nothing and looked proud in a quiet way. Some looked at their parents' faces on pickup day and couldn't tell whether pride or fear was winning.



Iona's overlay stayed off for morning block because she liked hearing her own footsteps in the corridor without the building suggesting a better cadence. Her friend Mira had turned hers back on after three days of gray hallways because, Mira said, *homework looked mean without it.* They didn't fight about it. They ate lunch with the subject closed, which was its own kind of post-failure friendship—difference allowed, not optimized into a lesson.



Adults were the strange ones.



Not villains. Not monsters. Just **uncomfortable** in the new texture, like clothes that fit yesterday and pinched today. Ms. Albright checked her tablet three times during attendance and still wrote names on paper backup because paper didn't stutter. The counselor posted a wellness slide about **connection hygiene**—call someone, touch grass, hydrate—and laughed at her own joke too loudly when the slide autocorrected *touch grass* to *touch baseline*. Mr. Okafor in civics paused when the public feed replayed the stutter meme and then skipped the clip without comment, which was comment enough.



In the hallway between second and third period, two parents argued in whispers near the pickup doors—not about grades, about **filters**. One wanted the school to default bloom back on for "emotional safety." One wanted mandatory off-hours so children would "remember reality." Both sounded like they were reading scripts they hadn't written. A kid walked between them with a lunch box and the expression children got when adults forgot they could hear.



Iona watched and did not watch. That was witness manners without a badge—store first, name later, the way some adults moved through rooms that hadn't decided what they were yet. She had Dad and school and a drawer that stuck in humidity and a book that did not adapt. Enough geography for one twelve-year-old.



The wellness terminal by the fountain chimed at passing students: **FRIENDSHIP REMINDER · HAVE YOU CHECKED IN TODAY?** Green if you tapped yes. Amber if you skipped. Nobody had explained why friendship needed a reminder like hydration. The building assumed you would forget people unless it pinged you.



At lunch Mira showed her the peer kindness board on the cafeteria screen—names scrolling when someone awarded a token, points stacking beside compliments like food groups on a nutrition poster. "It's supposed to make us nicer," Mira said, not sounding convinced.



"It makes us **visible** when we're nice," Iona said.



Mira considered that with the seriousness of someone who had already learned that visibility was its own kind of payment. "Maybe that's the same thing to adults."



They ate without resolving it. That was also post-failure—questions allowed to stay open.



Iona tapped **skip** on the friendship terminal and felt the amber land on her profile like a small shame she hadn't earned.



* * *



She wrote the questions in the stairwell because stairwells were still the honest places—concrete steps, real window light, no floor projection telling her where rivers went when she got a sentence wrong.



Not homework. Not for a teacher. For her.



The first one came from the fountain terminal and the counselor slide and Mira's mom saying *just call her, honey, the app says you haven't* like the app knew intimacy better than daughters did.



**Why reminders to call friends?**



She wrote it on the inside cover of a notebook she didn't use for school anymore—the paper kind, because some habits survived even when buildings wanted everything on glass. Her handwriting sat uneven on the line. She didn't fix it. Fixing was what overlays did.



Second period civics had run a kindness module after lunch—peer points for holding doors, gratitude tokens, a leaderboard for compliments that reset every Friday so nobody could win forever but everybody could try. A boy had said thank you to get a token and then forgotten to mean it. The teacher called it **practice**. Iona called it something else in her head.



**Why score kindness?**



The third arrived from everywhere at once—the wellness bands, the stable score Dad glanced at and looked away from, the HVI talk from Month Two she half remembered as *numbers that said you were good at being a person*, the way adults relaxed when metrics greened and tightened when they didn't, like numbers were weather you could dress for.



**Why trust numbers?**



Three questions. Not a speech. Not cruel. Precise the way she was precise when she said **Books.** in a room that laughed—not to wound, to see if the words fit the world. They didn't fit yet. That was why you kept them.



She thought about the storm Month Two when rain came through the glass without a comfort slider and she had asked Dad why people didn't like real things anymore. True answer, not enough. She thought about the book in her drawer, paper weight on paper worksheets, authors who wanted to be read their way. She thought about optional overlays and adults arguing about filters and friendship reminders like people were tasks.



The civics screen had flashed a **TRUST THE METRICS** banner after the stutter week—stable schools, stable tiers, stable hearts, all in the same font as transit on time. A boy in front of her had joked that his little sister thought HVI was a pet. The class laughed because laughing was easier than asking what the numbers measured when the overlay failed.



None of the questions were meanness. They were **compass** work—the kind of pointing that didn't need a destination on the same day, only a direction honest enough to walk.



She closed the notebook and listened to footsteps above and below. Real stairs. Real building. Real children being loud in a gym somewhere without a mix board making the noise civic.



* * *



Home still knew her.



Gold on the laminate again—Dad had turned the filter back on after the honest week, and the apartment had agreed to be kind in the way that made homework tiles soft and fruit too bright and the city beyond the glass look sponsored. Iona smelled dinner and sync hum and the particular distracted warmth of a parent who loved you and sometimes lived one room away in his head.



She washed her hands. Hung her jacket. Did not go straight to the drawer. Secrets had rhythm too; checking them every day turned treasure into inventory.



Noah was at the counter with the mug that had the chip at the rim, scrolling something on the wall screen with the half-attention he used when work hadn't quite released him. He looked up and smiled the on-time smile.



"How was school?" he asked.



"Fine."



"Just fine?"



"School." She shrugged one shoulder—the gesture that meant *longer answer available if you earn it* and also *don't make me perform fine for a graph.*



He almost asked the follow-up. She saw it—the sensible question forming, the overlay probably offering him a parenting prompt about emotional check-ins because the city had decided feelings were maintenance too. He chose mercy instead.



"Snack's out. Math later?"



"Later."



She ate an apple that tasted brighter than apples needed to taste and watched him glance at a notification—*Your score is stable*—the way adults looked at numbers when numbers had started meaning *you're allowed to relax.* She thought about question three and did not say it yet. Questions had order. She wasn't ready to spend all three in one room.



After dinner, when homework had been done enough and the sync tone had gone polite in the alcove, she stood at the kitchen edge with her notebook closed in her hand and the overlay off because home allowed that now if you chose it.



Noah rinsed a plate. "You seem like you're carrying something."



"I'm thinking."



"About?"



She could have opened the notebook. Could have asked all three in a row and watched his face do the adult thing—kind, afraid, searching for handles. She didn't want handles. She wanted truth without a score attached.



One fragment slipped out anyway—not the whole list, just the edge of the second question, quiet enough to be mistaken for rhetorical if he needed that out.



"Why score kindness?"



Noah paused mid-rinse. Water ran a second too long. She watched his shoulders decide between depth and deflection the way she'd watched teachers skip stutter memes—protective, incomplete.



"Kindness isn't scored," he said, warm, reasonable, wrong in the way adults were wrong when they answered the system instead of the child. "The school uses points to **encourage**—"



"That's scoring."



"It's—" He turned off the tap. Laugh almost arrived—the Month One laugh from the one-life question, reflex before thought. It didn't come. Month Three had taught him something too. "It's complicated, Iona."



"I know." She said it without cruelty. Precision again. Not pushing when pushing would turn her into a lecture. She stored his answer the way she stored storms and book spines and amber skips on friendship terminals—in the drawer of the mind, beside the paper one.



He came over and put his hand on her shoulder, brief, present—the 6w7 gift for making rooms believe they were safe because he had decided to be pleasant. "You don't have to fix the world tonight."



"I'm not trying to fix it."



"I know." He didn't, not fully. He loved her in the abstract way parents loved children who tried. That was okay for tonight.



She went to her room when he returned to the screen and the sync hum rose again. Closed the door. Opened the drawer a finger-width—not for the book tonight, just to feel paper weight exist in the same apartment as optimization. Proof that some things still refused to adapt.



The three questions sat in the notebook on her desk, written in her uneven hand, unanswered, not needing answers yet.



**Why reminders to call friends?**  

**Why score kindness?**  

**Why trust numbers?**



Compass set.



Outside, the city kept mild weather and calm markets. Inside, Iona lay on her bed staring at the ceiling glow and listened for footsteps that didn't come. The secret held. The questions held harder.



Month Three continued the way months did when you were twelve and had started seeing the invoice adults pretended was weather.



She did not know what came next. She knew which way not to lie.
