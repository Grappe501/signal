# Chapter 14

The wind arrived before the forecast did.

Noah heard it first—a low push against the tower glass, the kind of sound the building usually edited into something softer before it reached the living room. Today it came through raw. He was at the counter slicing apples for Iona's after-school plate when the overlay at the edge of his vision stuttered once, weather tile flickering from *Mild · optimized transit* to a blank gray square and back again.

He glanced at the window wall. The apartment sat mid-tier in a tower that had been marketed on views—floor-to-ceiling glass, responsive light, the promise that you could live inside the city's best version of itself without leaving your couch. From this height New Manhattan still looked solved: lanes open, pods drifting, the afternoon behaving the way Month Two afternoons were supposed to behave. Then the sky above the river broke wrong. Not Veil-wrong. **Weather**-wrong. A shelf of cloud moving faster than the polish allowed, darkening the glass before the room's ambient light could decide whether to compensate.

"Whoa," Iona said from the couch. She had homework on her lap and one sock on, the other abandoned under the cushion—the domestic chaos Noah had stopped trying to optimize because some messes were proof of occupancy.

The overlay chimed at his ear—Second dock requesting sync, work queue nudging with the polite insistence of infrastructure that assumed you'd always answer. A pressure flag in Urban Water Allocation blinked amber at the lower edge of his sight, the kind of problem his job paid him to resolve in two places at once. Noah muted the link with a thumb swipe he didn't explain to himself. Once. The sync tone cut mid-pulse.

The apartment dimmed.

The responsive ceiling panels failed to brighten against the storm front. The city glow through the glass thinned until the living room held its own gray—an honest shadow, rain beginning to sheet down the pane in streaks that didn't arrange themselves for viewing.

Noah set the knife down and crossed to the window.

Iona was already there, forehead near the glass, breath fogging a small circle. Twelve years old and not afraid—**fascinated**, the way she'd been fascinated by the paper book in the school overlay when the lesson tried to skip past it. She had pressed her palm to the haptic field in class as if weight could be borrowed through light. Here there was nothing to borrow. Only water finding the glass and losing.

Noah stood beside her and felt the floor vibrate when thunder rolled in, low and unprocessed, traveling through the tower bones the way sound traveled before anyone learned to smooth it. The living room's dock alcove went dark—the Second's LED strip dying with the sync tone, as if the apartment itself had chosen a single channel for once.

The overlay tried again. A soft banner at the bottom of his sight offered *Storm comfort mode · enable?* Below it, a smaller line suggested *Reduce auditory variance · child present.* The city noticing a child and offering to protect her from the lesson the sky had brought. Noah dismissed both. The icons blinked, confused, then vanished when the system either gave up or dropped the handoff. The room stayed dark. The rain stayed loud. No Veil bloom softened the edges. No assistance layer sanded the moment. For as long as the front held, the apartment belonged to weather.

On the tier below, someone's balcony planter tipped—soil and ceramic skidding in a mess the cleanup drones would handle later, but for now the failure was visible, almost personal. Noah watched it without commentary. Iona watched the lightning.

"That's real," Iona said.

Noah almost laughed—the reflex from breakfast, from the one-life question, from every month he'd spent treating optimization as mercy. The laugh didn't come. The storm was too present for reflex.

"Yeah," he said. "That's real."

He could have linked. Could have cleared the amber flag while his body stayed at the window and his attention went where the city paid him to go. Running Double still worked. The habit was muscle. Tonight the muscle refused—not heroism, just the sense that splitting himself while his daughter watched honest rain would be a kind of lie he was tired of not naming.

So he stayed. One room. One attention. The apples browning on the counter behind them, the homework tile dark on the couch, the storm doing work no overlay had scheduled.

* * *

Through the glass the city sounded like a city again.

Sirens without mixing. Horns with edges. Wind finding gaps between towers and making noise the overlays usually folded into background calm. Somewhere on the waterfront a warning klaxon held too long—raw, unfashionable urgency—before cutting off as if someone upstream had remembered embarrassment. Lightning lit the river white for a half-second and Iona's face with it—sharp cheekbone, wide eyes, the reflection of rain on glass doubling her so she looked like two girls watching the same storm, one inside the building and one inside the weather.

Noah put his hand on the sill. The metal was cold. Real cold, not haptic suggestion. He thought about *Books*, about the class laughing, about his own silence in the adaptive room when pride and fear had tied his tongue. He thought about the teacher's professional smile smoothing the moment forward, the watershed map brightening, the system noticing friction and offering to sand it.

His life had other channels—water tiers, grief management, the domestic work of staying present when the overlay asked you to divide. He'd felt a smaller fracture in his own kitchen when Iona asked about one life and he'd laughed. This was his channel: glass, rain, a child who stored questions the way other children stored candy.

Iona watched a pod abort its lane and slide sideways on the wind, correction algorithms fighting physics in plain sight.

"They usually make it prettier," she said.

"They do."

"Even the scary parts?"

"Especially the scary parts."

She considered that with the seriousness of someone who had already learned that adults often called smoothing *care*.

The thunder peaked—a crack close enough to rattle the pane—and Iona flinched once, then leaned closer, as if proximity to the glass could keep the honest thing from leaving. Noah did not pull her back. Presence was the job here. Not explanation. Not fixing the sky.

Below them a tier screen flickered out and stayed dark for three breaths before rebooting with mild weather and calm markets, as if the storm had been a brief disagreement the city preferred not to archive. Noah watched the reboot and felt nothing as clean as fear. Something closer to recognition—the same unnamed absence he'd walked home with after parent observation morning, when *Books.* had fallen into the room with the wrong texture and he'd followed his daughter upstairs without fixing what she'd named.

Iona kept her eyes on the rain.

"Why don't people like real things anymore?" she said.

The question was quiet. Not accusatory. Not precocious. It had the same weight as her *Books.* answer—the weight of something observed and stored until the room could hold it.

Noah felt his chest tighten the way it had tightened when she'd asked about one life and he'd laughed because the past sounded like nostalgia this civilization had politely deleted. He didn't laugh now. Deflection failed here.

"People got used to easier," he said. "Safer. Prettier. The overlays help when things are hard."

"Hard like storms?"

"Hard like grief. Like boredom. Like waiting." He searched for better words and found only honest ones. "It helps until you forget what unhelped felt like."

Iona nodded once, filing the answer under a category he couldn't read from her face.

Thunder receded to the next district. Rain steadied. The apartment creaked—joists and glass and old concrete doing their jobs without commentary. Noah's partial answer sat between them like a mug with no handle. True. Not enough.

* * *

The storm passed the way storms passed when the city wanted its schedule back.

Thirty minutes, maybe less—time compressed around parenting the way it always did when you stayed present without measuring. The sky lightened in layers. The river stopped looking like a warning. At the window the rain thinned to mist, then to residue, wet glass catching the first return of optimized glow from the tiers below.

The overlay re-engaged without asking permission this time.

Noah felt it bloom at the edge of his vision—transit green, weather mild again, the success chime too bright for the hour returning like a guest who assumed they'd never been unwelcome. The living room brightened two degrees. The rain on the glass became decorative. New Manhattan smoothed itself back into the city he navigated for work every day: lanes open, markets calm, the polite fiction that continuity meant comfort.

Relief came in the same breath as loss. He noticed both and didn't try to rank them.

The apartment remembered its polish slowly—warmth returning to the floor panels, the couch surface smoothing a crease Iona had left, the wall screen cycling back to transit on time and weather mild as if the last hour had been a private experiment Noah and his daughter had run without permission. He should have felt grateful. Gratitude was what the overlay expected. Instead he felt the gap between the two versions of the room the way you felt air where a mug handle should be.

Iona yawned against his shoulder, tired in the satisfied way of someone who had gotten what they came for from an afternoon. She didn't ask again about real things. The question was done for now—the way some questions lived in a room after the thunder ended, occupying space without needing to be repeated.

Noah walked her to her room—socks retrieved, homework abandoned until after dinner, teeth brushed without the overlay's mood stabilization chime. The system had learned bedtime, but tonight she moved through it half asleep, trusting the ordinary.

When he returned to the living room the sync hum had resumed at the alcove, soft, confirming link strength, the infrastructure of a life that still worked. His work queue waited where he'd left it. The amber flag still blinked. Running Double could resume.

He stood at the window above the drying glass and watched the city become pretty again.

Unsettled—not afraid, not grieving, not yet in denial—the way he'd been unsettled since the school overlay stuttered over paper and his daughter said *Books.* like the word still mattered. Something was missing from the restored view. He couldn't name it without sounding like nostalgia, and nostalgia was what the world had taught him to laugh at.

The real things question stayed in the room after the thunder ended.

Noah did not call anyone who might have had an answer for that. He let the overlay show him mild weather and stable scores and went to finish the apples before they browned—ordinary, miraculous, insufficient—while Month Two continued outside the glass without asking whether continue was enough.
