# Chapter 1 — Scene 1.4: The Final Proof

**0708** pulsed once in the corner of the tablet—polite, precise—as the interface populated. CIB gray against all that white: honest equipment in a dishonest room. She had built her career on retinal chains—the last thing anyone argued with in a world that still trusted eyes more than stories. That was about to be tested.

The Continuity Steward's backtrace arrived first: **estimated biological death, 0412.** Guest review session ended **0108.** Alina Ward had hours in the gap the suite would not explain—asleep, or awake for something the polished room had already corrected away.

"Window locked," Chen said.

The logs cascaded in the cheerful order systems preferred when they thought they were helping. **Julian Crane.** Retinal turnstile, **Arcology One, 0402.** Executive Continuity Briefing Room **14-C.** Session seal **0408–0700.** Title header: **Continuity Infrastructure Director.** Remote continuity authenticated. Biological anchor verified. No anomalies flagged.

A confirmation tone chimed—bright, satisfied, the sound of a world that liked being right.

The blue wash climbed her face in the glass. Mara watched her own reflection take the color of certainty and felt nothing like certainty—the scar above her brow dark in the light, her thumb still sore where she'd dug it into the holster.

Proof.

She flagged Julian Crane for live confirmation anyway—procedure, or stubbornness, she wasn't sure which. The briefing-room seal returned **busy**: owner unreachable, session in progress, no violation flag. No human voice. The chain was perfect. The man was not available to confirm it.

The map unfolded beneath the text with a low, patient pulse—three pins in a city that still looked, from this height, finished. Julian Crane **here.** Crane-Second **here.** Alina Ward **here.** Same window. Same law. One human, one legal Second, one dead guest in a unit built for visibility—and one pair of eyes in the chair that had never stopped watching her.

Her mind refused to square it.

Good.

She spoke for the file, because the file was the only listener that would carry the words forward without trying to comfort her.

"Owner alibi verified," she said. "Ocular tracking confirmed. Biological homicide in primary suite. Legal Second present during owner-verified remote session. One-Second law intact on paper. Case open—not malfunction."

For the first time she could remember, the system had given her proof that clarified nothing—the final proof she had always trusted, and the first one she could not use.

Chen was quiet long enough that Mara noticed the silence—not empty, heavier than procedure usually allowed.

"Coroner's cleared to enter," Chen said. "Building counsel is screaming off-channel already—ignore them until you have language." Another beat. "You're lead on eighty-eight forty-one-A. Keep it quiet until we know what we're looking at."

Responsibility landed the way it always did—not as drama, but as weight. Mara had wanted the truth. The truth was heavier than the dispatch had promised. The same six minutes she'd circled on the Spine. A whole city already drafting its story without her.

A sidebar notification slid across the tablet with a cheap ping—**Hush Spire sync incident,** reflected in the glass over the magnificent city below. Trending language already settling into the soft shape of reassurance: *isolated hardware stall, resident unharmed, building systems responding.* They had decided before she had.

She dismissed it without reading the rest.

Dawn had climbed while she worked. Through the glass, the city below still looked magnificent—and untouched by whatever had happened in this room. Somewhere in that schedule, kitchens were warming and school pods were docking; the efficient dream continuing for everyone who had not spent their morning in a perfect room with a dead guest down the hall.

Behind her, the coroner drone whirred once at the entry—the scheduled service from beyond the seal, cleared now, polite and waiting. Mara turned back to the chair.

Crane-Second's eyes were still on her. Idle on the readout. Alive in the room.

The record said Julian Crane was at Arcology One. The room said something else entirely. Two truths, both insisting.

Mara looked at the eyes one last time—not long, not like before—and spoke to the record alone.

"The eyes were here," she said. "He wasn't."

Nothing in the unit corrected the sentence.
