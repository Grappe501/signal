The pod arrived the way pods always arrived—on time, white hull, docking arc so clean it looked drawn rather than flown. Iona Vale stood on the curb with her backpack half-zipped and her hair fighting the braid again, watching the lane with the quiet satisfaction of someone who liked proof.

Noah walked beside her with his hands in his jacket pockets and his overlay muted for the moment. Parent observation morning. The school sent the notices with soft fonts and the assumption that you'd be grateful, and he was—mostly. Gratitude was easier before you started noticing what it asked you not to see.

The School of Everything lived up to its name in the way modern institutions did: by making abundance feel inevitable. The entrance breathed—glass, responsive light, air adjusted for children the way Dubai adjusted air for crowds—and inside, classrooms opened like petals around a central atrium where lessons could spill into shared space without losing their polish. Noah had toured facilities on tier maps for work. This was the version you built when you believed the future had already agreed to behave.

Iona's class was already inside the adaptive room, walls shifting to a gentle blue while the floor projected a slow map of watersheds beneath their feet. Children stood on rivers that glowed when they answered correctly. The teacher—a young woman with the tired brightness of someone who loved the job and trusted the system—waved parents to the side benches with a smile that didn't quite reach the stress lines at her eyes.

"Morning," she said to Iona. "We're doing inventions today."

Iona nodded once, precise, and took her place among the others without performing eagerness. Noah sat where he could see her profile and the overlay edges flickering at the corners of her vision. The room was wonderful. He allowed himself to think it without irony. Wonder was still allowed in Month One.

* * *

The prompt arrived on schedule.

"If you could name the greatest invention in human history," the teacher said, "the one that changed everything—what would you choose?"

Hands rose. Overlay suggestions shimmered for the shy ones. The answers came in the order the city preferred:

"Relay."

"Veil."

"LATTICE."

Each name landed with the smooth consensus of children who had grown up inside the answer key. The teacher smiled, validated, moved the watershed map forward a beat. Noah felt the usual pride in rooms like this—the proof that his daughter's world worked, that optimization had delivered on its promise to make learning feel like play.

Iona's hand went up last.

Not hesitant. Not dramatic. Simply there, as if she had been holding the thought before the question finished.

"Iona?" the teacher said.

"Books."

The word fell into the room with the wrong texture—paper in a glass church.

For a half-second nobody moved. Then the laugh came—not cruel, not even unified, but the sharp social sound of children recognizing an answer that didn't fit the overlay. A few snickers. A boy in the front row said "Books?" like she had suggested candles. The teacher's smile strained at the corners.

Noah winced.

Pride came hot and immediate—Iona had meant it—and fear with it: wanting her right and wanting her safe, which in this city were often the same thing in different fonts. He could have stood. Could have said something about paper and persistence and the way his mother read to him from a physical book until the spine split.

He didn't.

The teacher recovered with professional speed. "That's a thoughtful answer," she said, in the tone adults used when smoothing. "Very old-school." A polite chime sounded at the room level—assistance layer, mood stabilization, the system noticing friction and offering to sand it. The watershed map brightened. The moment slid forward as if it had never snagged.

Iona lowered her hand. Her chin stayed level. She was not embarrassed, or she was performing not-being-embarrassed with the exacting care of a twelve-year-old who had already learned that dignity was a kind of armor. Noah loved her for it and hated himself a little for not speaking.

The lesson resumed. Relay. Veil. LATTICE. The future, clapping for itself.

* * *

History arrived as overlay—old photographs floating above the floor, pre-digital faces grainy and real in a way the room's polish made painful. A segment on literacy rates. A clip of children in a line holding tablets that looked obsolete six months after filming. The teacher narrated progress the way progress liked to be narrated: upward, clean, grateful.

Then the archive coughed up something the lesson plan hadn't fully domesticated.

A paper book—scanned, tactile interface enabled—rotated in the shared field. The cover worn. The pages thick. Children reached without thinking, fingers passing through haptic feedback that tried to approximate weight and almost succeeded. Iona touched the projection longer than the others, her face quiet in the way Noah was learning meant she was storing a feeling for later.

"Real books are mostly museum pieces now," the teacher said, too quickly. "We keep them for—cultural continuity. The important thing is access, and we have better access than any generation before—"

The overlay stuttered—not a Dubai glitch, just a half-beat of loading—then replaced the book with a brighter graphic about LATTICE distribution nodes. The old photos faded. The room's color temperature warmed two degrees. *Within tolerance,* Noah thought without meaning to, and disliked that he thought it.

The teacher laughed softly, embarrassed at the stumble, and moved on. The system had smoothed her, too.

When the bell chimed, Iona packed her bag with deliberate motions. She didn't look at him on the way out. Not angry. Just full of something she wasn't ready to share in a hallway full of parents praising the watershed lesson.

* * *

They walked home through an afternoon that behaved.

Transit on time. Weather mild. Market calm on the wall screens at the corner store. Noah's overlay tried to show him work queues and he kept it shallow, the way you kept water shallow when you didn't trust the bottom. Iona walked half a step ahead, which was her way of being twelve and independent and still wanting him within reach.

He thought about *Books.* He thought about the laugh. He thought about the paper spine in the overlay and the way the teacher had rushed past it like a room noticing a smell and opening a window.

Missing something, he thought—not a word he would say aloud, not yet—a sensation like reaching for a mug handle and finding air where the chip at the rim should be. The city continued around them, lanes open, pods drifting, the sync tone steady at the edge of his hearing. Running Double still worked. School still worked. His daughter still came home on schedule.

That was the trouble. Everything worked.

At the building entrance Iona said, "Dad?" and he leaned down because parents learned to lean fast.

"Yeah?"

"Books were how you kept things when the power went out." Not a question. A statement she was testing.

Noah met her eyes. "Yeah," he said. "They were."

She nodded as if filing that under *confirmed* and ran ahead to the elevator, socks wrong, braid loose, alive in a world that had forgotten why forgetting mattered.

Noah followed with his hands in his pockets and the unnamed absence walking beside him like a second self he hadn't agreed to link.

The door closed. The kitchen waited upstairs—gold on the laminate, the cheap kind that didn't pretend to be stone—and the afternoon kept its schedule the way the city preferred.
